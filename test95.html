Question 5 of 50
A patient with ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome) on mechanical ventilation is showing signs of barotrauma. What is the priority nursing action?
A. Increase the PEEP to improve oxygenation.
B. Decrease the tidal volume to reduce risk of injury. ✓
C. Increase the FiO2 to prevent hypoxemia
D. Administer a diuretic to decrease pulmonary edema.
💡
Explanation
Barotrauma occurs when the lungs are subjected to excessive pressure or volume, leading to injury such as pneumothorax. Reducing tidal volume helps minimize the risk of further injury, as supported by evidence in ARDS management. Increasing FiO2 (Option C) or PEEP (Option A) can exacerbate barotrauma, and diuretics (Option D) would not address the immediate concern of ventilator-associated injury.

Question 7 of 50

B. Hypotension
C. Hypercapnia
D. Respiratory acidosis
💡
Explanation
• Tachypnea (increased respiratory rate) is an early compensatory response to hypoxia or hypercapnia and often precedes more severe signs like hypotension and respiratory acidosis. • Hypotension and hypercapnia (high carbon dioxide levels) are typically more indicative of a worsening condition. • Respiratory acidosis is a late-stage condition that might result from ventilation failure, not an early sign.
Question 8 of 50
What is the normal value for tidal volume in a mechanically ventilated patient?
A. 4-8 mL/kg of body weight ✓
B. 10-12 mL/kg of body weight
C. 5-7 L/min
D. 50-70 mmHg
💡
Explanation
• Tidal volume is the amount of air delivered to the lungs with each breath. The normal value is 4-8 mL/kg of body weight to avoid overdistension of the lungs. • 10-12 mL/kg is higher than the recommended tidal volume for most patients, especially in adults with acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). • 5-7 L/min refers to minute ventilation, not tidal volume. • 50-70 mmHg typically refers to the partial pressure of oxygen (PaO2) in the blood.
Question 9 of 50
What is the abnormal value for plateau pressure during mechanical ventilation, which can indicate a risk of lung injury?
A. 10-12 cmH2O
B. 15-20 cmH2O
C. 25-30 cmH2O
D. 30-35 cmH2O ✓
💡
Explanation

Plateau pressure is a key parameter in mechanical ventilation that reflects the pressure applied to the alveoli of the lungs during the inspiratory pause (when airflow is momentarily stopped).

It is measured during an inspiratory hold maneuver, and it reflects the static compliance of the lung-thorax system — in simpler terms, how stiff or flexible the lungs are.

Question 10 of 50
What is the normal range for positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) in mechanically ventilated patients?
A. 0-5 cmH2O
B. 5-10 cmH2O ✓
C. 10-15 cmH2O
D. 15-20 cmH2O
💡
Explanation
• PEEP is the pressure applied to the airways at the end of expiration to prevent alveolar collapse. The normal range for PEEP is typically 5-10 cmH2O, particularly in patients with conditions like ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome). • 0-5 cmH2O is often used in patients who do not have severe lung disease or ARDS. • 10-15 cmH2O and 15-20 cmH2O are higher ranges that may be used in certain severe cases but can increase the risk of barotrauma and other complications.
Question 11 of 50
Which of the following is the most likely cause of increased peak inspiratory pressure (PIP) in a mechanically ventilated patient?
A. Airway obstruction ✓
B. Decreased cardiac output
C. Increased tidal volume
D. Decreased PEEP
💡
Explanation
• Airway obstruction, such as a kinked endotracheal tube or mucus plug, often causes an increase in peak inspiratory pressure (PIP) because of the increased resistance to airflow. • Decreased cardiac output can affect oxygenation but is less likely to cause a rise in PIP directly. • Increased tidal volume could lead to high pressure if not properly adjusted, but airway obstruction is a more immediate and likely cause. • Decreased PEEP typically reduces lung recruitment and might cause hypoxemia, but not a significant increase in PIP.
Question 12 of 50
What should be the priority action if a patient on mechanical ventilation suddenly develops decreased breath sounds on one side?
A. Increase FiO2
B. Check for pneumothorax ✓
C. Increase tidal volume
D. Adjust ventilator settings
💡
Explanation
• Decreased breath sounds on one side could indicate a pneumothorax (collapsed lung), which requires immediate attention. A pneumothorax can be life-threatening and must be diagnosed and treated quickly. • Increasing FiO2 may improve oxygenation but won’t address the underlying problem if a pneumothorax is present.
• Increasing tidal volume can lead to barotrauma and worsen a pneumothorax.
• Adjusting ventilator settings may be necessary later, but the priority is to check for and treat the pneumothorax.
Question 13 of 50
What is the most common complication associated with endotracheal intubation in mechanically ventilated patients?
A. Laryngeal injury
B. Tracheal stenosis
C. Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) ✓
D. Hemorrhage
💡
Explanation
Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) is one of the most common and serious complications in patients receiving mechanical ventilation. It typically develops after 48 hours or more of intubation and is caused by the aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions, resulting in an infection in the lungs. This infection is primarily due to the mechanical ventilation disrupting normal airway defenses and increasing the risk of bacterial colonization. Laryngeal injury and hemorrhage can occur during intubation but are less common compared to VAP. Tracheal stenosis is a long-term complication that may result from prolonged intubation, but it does not occur as frequently as VAP.
Question 14 of 50
What is the typical goal for tidal volume (VT) in a mechanically ventilated patient with acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)?
A. 10-12 mL/kg
B. 8-10 mL/kg
C. 6-8 mL/kg ✓
D. 4-6 mL/kg
💡
Explanation

Question 15 of 50
Which of the following is the primary purpose of PEEP in mechanical ventilation?
A. To increase oxygenation ✓
B. To reduce airway resistance
C. To improve ventilator synchrony
D. To increase tidal volume
💡
Explanation
• PEEP (Positive End-Expiratory Pressure) is primarily used to prevent alveolar collapse at the end of expiration, thereby improving oxygenation and increasing the functional residual capacity (FRC). • Reducing airway resistance is not the primary purpose of PEEP; it mainly works by improving alveolar recruitment. • Improving ventilator synchrony and increasing tidal volume are not the primary goals of PEEP. Instead, these are controlled by other settings like tidal volume and trigger sensitivity.

Question 23 of 50
A patient with a known history of severe COPD is admitted with acute shortness of breath. His SpO2 is 85% on room air. Which of the following is the most appropriate initial oxygen delivery device?

 ✓

💡

Nasal cannula (B) and a simple mask (D) deliver variable FiO2, making it difficult to control the exact dose of oxygen.
Question 24 of 50
The primary advantage of a High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC) over a standard nasal cannula is that it:
A. Is less expensive and more readily available.
B. Delivers a more precise and consistent FiO2.
 ✓
C. Is more comfortable for all patients.
D. Can be used at higher flow rates without humidification.
💡
Explanation
A standard nasal cannula is a low-flow system where the FiO2 varies with the patient's breathing pattern. While it is often more comfortable due to heated humidification (D is false), its key clinical benefit is the accurate delivery of a set oxygen concentration.

B. Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP).
 ✓
C. Drying of the nasal mucosa.
D. Suppression of the hypoxic drive.
💡
Explanation
In premature infants, the retinal blood vessels are underdeveloped.  ✓
D. "It is safe to smoke in another room with the door closed if the oxygen is off."
💡

B. 0.40-0.60
C. 0.60-0.80
D. 0.80-1.00
💡
Explanation
