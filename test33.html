The blood group that contains no antibodies
A. A
B. B
C. O
D. AB ✓
💡
Explanation

The blood group AB is the only blood type that contains no antibodies against A or B antigens in the blood plasma. Here’s why:

  • Type A: Contains antibodies against B antigens.
  • Type B: Contains antibodies against A antigens.
  • Type O: Contains both anti-A and anti-B antibodies.
  • Type AB: Does not have antibodies against A or B antigens because individuals with this blood type have both A and B antigens on their red blood cells and thus do not produce antibodies against them.

Rapid administration of blood containing citrate causes:
A. Hypocalcaemia ✓
B. Hypercalcemia
C. Hypokalaemia
D. Hyperkalaemia
💡
Explanation
Rapid administration of blood containing citrate can lead to hypocalcemia, a condition characterized by low levels of calcium in the blood. Citrate is commonly used as an anticoagulant in blood storage, and when transfused into the recipient, it binds to calcium, reducing the amount of free (ionized) calcium available in the bloodstream.

Question 10 of 50
Platelets should not be administered under which condition?
A. The platelet bag is cold ✓
B. The platelets are 2 days old
C. The platelet bag is at room temperature
D. The platelets are 12 hours old
💡
Explanation
Platelets are living blood components that are very sensitive to temperature. They must remain viable and functional before transfusion. Key Storage Guidelines: • Temperature: Platelets must be stored at room temperature (20–24°C), not in a refrigerator. • Duration: They remain stable for up to 5 days with continuous gentle agitation to prevent clumping. • Cold exposure destroys platelets. Cold temperatures activate platelet aggregation, leading to loss of platelet function. Clinical implication: If a platelet bag has been accidentally refrigerated or becomes cold, those platelets are not suitable for transfusion because they will no longer circulate properly or aid in clot formation. Hence, cold platelet bags must never be administered to a patient.
Question 11 of 50
What is the first-line management for a suspected bacterial contamination in platelet transfusion?
A. Stop the transfusion and start broad-spectrum IV antibiotics ✓
B. Administer antihistamines
C. Perform immediate crossmatching
D. Increase the transfusion rate to reduce exposure time
💡
Explanation
Platelets are stored at room temperature (22°C), making them highly susceptible to bacterial contamination. A contaminated platelet transfusion can cause septic transfusion reactions, presenting as fever, hypotension, chills, tachycardia, and DIC. Common pathogens include Gram-negative bacteria (Pseudomonas, Yersinia), which produce endotoxins, leading to rapid clinical deterioration. Immediate cessation of transfusion, blood culture, and IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam) are critical to prevent septic shock. Platelets should always undergo bacterial culture screening before transfusion.
