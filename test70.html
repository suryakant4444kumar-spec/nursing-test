Question 29 of 50
Reflex response to a sudden loss of support felt by an infant as if he is falling is called as
A. Startle reflex
B. Glabellar reflex
C. Moro reflex ✓
D. Tonic neck reflex
💡
Explanation
The correct option is Option 3 : Moro reflex.
Explanation
  • The Moro reflex is a primitive, involuntary response found in infants from birth until about 3 to 6 months of age. It is specifically triggered by a sudden loss of support or a sudden shift in head position, which makes the infant feel as if they are falling.
  • When the reflex is triggered:
  • Stage 1: The infant suddenly flings their arms and legs outward (abduction) and extends their neck.
  • Stage 2: They then quickly pull their limbs back toward the body (adduction) in a motion that looks like a self-embrace or hug, often accompanied by crying.
  • Pediatricians use this reflex to evaluate the integration and health of a newborn's central nervous system.
Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: Startle reflex – While the Moro reflex is often colloquially called the "startle reflex," clinical literature sometimes distinguishes them. A true startle response (or Strauss reflex) typically involves a simpler flinch or jump that does not follow the specific two-stage "arm extension and retraction" pattern of the Moro reflex and persists throughout life.
  • Option 2: Glabellar reflex – This is elicited by repetitive tapping on the forehead (glabella). It causes the infant to blink and is used to test the trigeminal and facial nerves, not balance or falling.
  • Option 4: Tonic neck reflex – Also known as the "fencing posture," this occurs when an infant’s head is turned to one side, causing the arm on that side to straighten while the opposite arm bends. It is triggered by head rotation, not a loss of support.
