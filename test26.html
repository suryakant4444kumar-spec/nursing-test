  • The Z-score criteria is primarily and directly applicable to a Normal distribution.
  • The Z-score (also known as a standard score) is a statistical measurement that describes a value's relationship to the mean in a group of values, specifically identifying how many standard deviations a data point is from the mean.
  • Reasons for Applicability
  • Standardization: A Z-score converts a raw score from any normal distribution into a score on the standard normal distribution, which has a mean of
  • and a standard deviation of
  • Assumption of Normality: The interpretation and use of Z-score tables for finding probabilities or percentiles strictly assume the underlying data follows a normal distribution.
  • Symmetry Requirement: Because Z-scores rely on the properties of the normal curve (such as being symmetrical and bell-shaped), they do not accurately represent data in a skew deviation.
  • Statistical Tests: While the Chi-square test and Paired t-test are related to normal distribution principles, they use their own specific distributions (
  • and distribution, respectively) rather than the standard Z-score criteria directly.

  • The Z-score criteria is applicable to Option 1: Normal distribution.

Question 6 of 50
Identify the statistical diagram shown
A. Frequency polygon
B. Histogram
C. Line diagram
D. OGIVE ✓
💡
Explanation
  • A frequency polygon is a type of line graph that represents quantitative data. It is constructed by plotting the frequency of each class interval against its midpoint and joining these points with straight line segments.
  • Key Aspects of a Frequency Polygon:
  • Construction: It can be drawn by joining the midpoints of the tops of the bars in a histogram or by plotting the points independently.
  • Closed Figure: The polygon is closed by connecting the endpoints to the baseline (at zero frequency).
  • Purpose: It is used to visualize the shape of a data distribution and is especially useful for comparing multiple distributions on a single graph.
  • Other Options Explained:
  • Histogram (Option 2): Uses adjacent vertical bars to represent the frequencies of class intervals.
  • Line Diagram (Option 3): A general term for a graph using lines, sometimes used interchangeably, but "frequency polygon" is the specific statistical term.
Ogive (Option 4): A graph that represents cumulative frequencies.

Question 9 of 50
Identify the statistical diagram shown
A. Simple bar chart
B. Multiple bar chart
C. Cluster bar chart
D. Component bar chart ✓
💡
Explanation

These are various types of bar charts used to compare categorical data, with heights proportional to values. Simple bars show one variable, while multiple/clustered bars group side-by-side bars for comparison. Component (stacked) bars display sub-categories within a total bar, showing the composition of the whole.

  • Simple Bar Chart: Represents one categorical variable with individual bars.
  • Multiple/Cluster Bar Chart: Groups multiple sets of bars together side-by-side to compare different categories.
  • Component (Sub-divided/Stacked) Bar Chart: Displays bars that are split into components or stacked on top of one another to show total and sub-category values.
  • Percentage Bar Chart: A component bar chart where each bar is represented as a percentage (100% total).
