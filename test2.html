Question 3 of 50
What is the cell organ which synthesizes protein, is
A. Mitochondria
B. Golgi body
C. Lysosome
D. Ribosome ✓
💡
Explanation
Ribosomes are the cell organelles responsible for protein synthesis. They translate messenger RNA (mRNA) into amino acid sequences, which are the building blocks of proteins. Ribosomes can be found floating freely in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum, forming the rough ER. They read the genetic instructions provided by mRNA and facilitate the assembly of amino acids into proteins through a process called translation.

Question 6 of 50
Proteins that are to be used outside the cell are synthesized:
A. In the mitochondria
B. On free ribosome
C. On the smooth endoplasmic reticulum
D. On the rough endoplasmic reticulum ✓
💡
Explanation

Proteins that are to be secreted outside the cell are synthesized on ribosomes attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER). These ribosomes synthesize the proteins, and then the proteins are transported through the RER to be processed and packaged for secretion or incorporation into the cell membrane.

Here's why the other options are incorrect:

  • Option 1 (In the mitochondria): Mitochondria are involved in energy production, not protein synthesis for secretion outside the cell.

  • Option 2 (On free ribosomes): Free ribosomes produce proteins that are used inside the cell, not for secretion outside the cell.

  • Option 3 (On the smooth endoplasmic reticulum): The smooth ER is involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification, not in synthesizing proteins for secretion.

Question 7 of 50
Protein glycosylation occurs in
A. endoplasmic reticulum
B. Golgi bodies ✓
C. Mitochondria
D. Peroxisomes
💡
Explanation

  • Protein glycosylation is the process in which carbohydrate molecules are attached to proteins.

  • This modification helps in proper folding, stability, and function of proteins.

  • Although the initial steps of N-linked glycosylation start in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the major processing and modification of glycoproteins occur in the Golgi apparatus.

  • The Golgi adds and modifies sugar residues, producing mature glycoproteins ready for transport.

  • Thus, the main site of glycosylation in eukaryotic cells is the Golgi bodies.

Question 8 of 50
The watery fluid of a cell in which many organelles are found including nucleus is known as
A. Cytoplasm
B. Nucleoplasm
C. Cytosol ✓
D. Chromoplast
💡
Explanation

  • The cytosol is the semi-fluid component of the cytoplasm in which all the cell organelles, including the nucleus, are suspended.

  • It is composed mainly of water, salts, and organic molecules, and serves as the site for many metabolic reactions.

  • The cytoplasm includes both the cytosol and the organelles (except the nucleus).

  • The nucleoplasm is the fluid inside the nucleus, while chromoplasts are pigment-containing plastids in plant cells.

  • Hence, the watery fluid part of the cell where organelles are embedded is called the cytosol.

Question 13 of 50
The genetic exchange process in which donor DNA is introduced to the recipient by a virus is:
A. Transfection
B. Transformation
C. Conjugation
D. Transduction ✓
💡
Explanation
The correct option is Option 4: Transduction.
Explanation
Transduction is the process where donor DNA is transferred to a recipient cell by a virus (specifically a bacteriophage, a virus that infects bacteria). The virus acts as a vector, accidentally packaging fragments of the host's DNA into new viral particles and then injecting this DNA into a new host cell during a subsequent infection.
Why other options are incorrect--
Option 1: Transfection is the deliberate, artificial introduction of nucleic acids into eukaryotic (animal, plant, etc.) cells using non-viral methods, such as chemical or electrical means, and does not occur naturally in bacteria.
Option 2: Transformation is the process by which a bacterial cell takes up free, "naked" DNA directly from its surrounding environment, not through a viral intermediary.
Option 3: Conjugation is the transfer of genetic material through direct, physical cell-to-cell contact, typically via a temporary structure called a sex pilus.
Question 14 of 50
Which of the following mechanism facilitates transfer of large particles across cell membrane?
A. Active transport
B. Facilitated transport
C. Passive diffusion
D. Phagocytosis ✓
💡
Explanation
Phagocytosis is the process by which cells engulf large particles, such as debris, pathogens, or foreign substances, and transfer them across the cell membrane. This is a form of active transport, where energy is required for the cell to engulf and internalize the large particles. It is specifically designed for transferring large particles, unlike other mechanisms like active transport or passive diffusion, which are typically involved in the movement of smaller molecules.

This mRNA carries the genetic information from the DNA in the nucleus to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where protein synthesis will take place.

Question 24 of 50
What is the correct sequence of mitosis?
A. Telophase, Anaphase, Prophase, Telophase
B. Metaphase, Anaphase, Prophase, Telophase
C. Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase ✓
D. Anaphase, Prophase, Metaphase, Telophase
💡
Explanation

  • Prophase – The chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the nuclear membrane starts to break down, and the mitotic spindle begins to form.

  • Metaphase – The chromosomes align at the equatorial plane (metaphase plate) of the cell.

  • Anaphase – The sister chromatids are pulled apart toward opposite poles of the cell.

  • Telophase – The chromatids reach the poles, and the nuclear membrane reforms around the separated sets of chromosomes, preparing for the final division.

Question 26 of 50
Number of chromatids at metaphase is :
A. One in mitosis and two in meiosis
B. Two in both mitosis and meiosis ✓
C. One in both mitosis and meiosis
D. Two in mitosis and four in meiosis
💡
Explanation

During metaphase, chromosomes are aligned at the cell's equator. Each chromosome consists of two sister chromatids, which are identical copies of the chromosome formed during DNA replication.

  • In mitosis, each chromosome has two chromatids (one from each homologous chromosome).

  • In meiosis, the chromosomes also consist of two chromatids at metaphase I. At metaphase II, the chromatids of each chromosome are still present as individual chromatids.

Question 28 of 50
The type of epithelium that is most efficient in allowing simple of substances across cell is the
A. Squamous epithelium ✓
B. Columnar epithelium
C. Transitional epithelium
D. Stratified epithelium
💡
Explanation

Question 35 of 50
Codons are present on
A. DNA
B. mRNA ✓
C. RNA
D. rRNA
💡
Explanation

  • Codons are sequences of three nucleotides on messenger RNA (mRNA) that specify particular amino acids during protein synthesis.

  • Each codon corresponds to a specific amino acid or a stop signal in the genetic code.

  • The process of transcription copies the DNA sequence into mRNA, which then carries the genetic information from the nucleus to ribosomes.

  • At the ribosome, tRNA reads the codons on mRNA through its anticodons, ensuring correct amino acid placement.

  • Therefore, codons are located on mRNA, not on DNA or rRNA.

Question 36 of 50
Formation of scar tissue is ..........
A. Fibrosis ✓
B. Granulation
C. Adhesion
D. Keloid
💡
Explanation

  • Fibrosis refers to the formation of excess fibrous connective tissue as part of the healing process after tissue injury.

  • It occurs when fibroblasts produce large amounts of collagen, leading to the development of scar tissue.

  • While fibrosis is a normal part of wound repair, excessive fibrosis can interfere with organ function (e.g., pulmonary or liver fibrosis).

  • Granulation tissue forms earlier during healing, providing a base for scar formation.

  • Adhesion refers to abnormal fibrous bands between tissues, and keloid is an overgrowth of scar tissue.

  • Hence, the formation of scar tissue is known as fibrosis.
