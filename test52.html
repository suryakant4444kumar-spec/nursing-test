Question 1 of 50
C.S.F. findings in bacterial meningitis is ...
A. Increased protein and decreased sugar ✓
B. Increased protein and increased sugar
C. Decreased protein and increased sugar
D. Decreased protein and decreased sugar
💡
Explanation

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) findings in bacterial meningitis typically include:

  • Increased protein: due to inflammation and the presence of bacteria and white blood cells.

  • Decreased glucose (sugar): because bacteria consume glucose and impair glucose transport.

  • Increased white blood cells, especially neutrophils.

  • Turbid appearance of CSF.

Question 2 of 50
Trendelenburg test is positive due to injury to which of the following nerve
A. Obturator
B. Sciatic
C. Superior Gluteal ✓
D. Inferior Gluteal
💡
Explanation

The Trendelenburg test is used to assess the function of the superior gluteal nerve. A positive Trendelenburg sign occurs when there is weakness or dysfunction in the gluteus medius and gluteus minimus muscles, which are innervated by the superior gluteal nerve. When these muscles are weak, the pelvis tilts downward on the side opposite to the weakened muscle during single-leg standing, indicating a positive test.

This test helps diagnose conditions like hip abductor weakness due to superior gluteal nerve injury.

Question 7 of 50
Seizure in a meningitis client can be controlled by all of the following interventions, Except
A. Administration of phenytoin
B. Side lying position ✓
C. Control of fever
D. Minimal environmental stimuli
💡
Explanation

- Administration of phenytoin: an antiepileptic drug used to prevent or control seizures in meningitis when indicated.

Control of fever: reducing hyperthermia raises the seizure threshold and helps prevent febrile or fever-triggered seizures.

- Minimal environmental stimuli: reducing noise, bright lights and handling can decrease cerebral excitability and help prevent seizure provocation. - Why side-lying position is the exception: -

Placing the patient in the side-lying (recovery) position is a safety measure during a seizure to protect the airway and reduce aspiration risk, not an intervention that controls or stops the seizure itself.

- It is an important nursing action for safety and post-seizure care, but it does not pharmacologically or physiologically terminate the seizure.

Question 8 of 50
Neurological manifestation of Whipple's disease is:
A. Encephalopathy
B. Cerebellar ataxia ✓
C. Focal neurological deficits
D. Seizures
💡
Explanation
Whipple's disease is a rare bacterial infection caused by Tropheryma whipplei, which can affect multiple systems, including the nervous system. One of the neurological manifestations of Whipple's disease is cerebellar ataxia, which involves difficulty with coordination and balance due to damage to the cerebellum. Other symptoms may include encephalopathy and seizures, but cerebellar ataxia is one of the more common neurological signs.

A. Providing comfort measure ✓

💡
Explanation

Viral encephalitis is an inflammation of the brain caused by a viral infection, and its treatment primarily focuses on supportive care. Providing comfort measures may include:

  • Ensuring the patient is in a quiet, safe, and comfortable environment.

  • Managing fever with antipyretics.

  • Ensuring proper hydration and nutrition.

  • Monitoring for neurological changes and seizures.

  • Preventing complications such as pressure ulcers or deep vein thrombosis (DVT).

Question 10 of 50
The most common complication of Tuberculous meningitis is:
A. Encephalitis
B. Subdural empyema
C. Hydrocephalus ✓
D. Brain abscess
💡
Explanation
Hydrocephalus is the most common complication of tuberculous meningitis. In tuberculous meningitis, the infection can lead to inflammation of the meninges, which can block the normal flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) around the brain and spinal cord. This can result in the accumulation of CSF, causing hydrocephalus (a condition characterized by an abnormal increase in CSF within the ventricles of the brain).

Question 16 of 50
____bladder is expected when a patient is experiencing spinal shock
A. Atonic ✓
B. Normal
C. Spastic
D. Uncontrolled
💡
Explanation

  • Spinal shock is a condition that occurs following a spinal cord injury, where there is a temporary loss of all reflexes and motor activity below the level of the injury. It typically leads to a period of flaccid paralysis and loss of sensation, which can also affect the bladder.

  • The bladder during spinal shock becomes atonic, meaning it loses its normal tone and function. The bladder does not contract properly, leading to urinary retention. The patient may not be able to empty the bladder effectively, and there is often a loss of voluntary control over urination.

Question 22 of 50
A patient in a coma moves their limbs in an effort to remove a painful stimulus. According to the GCS, what is their motor response score?
A. 6
B. 5 ✓
C. 4
D. 3
💡
Explanation

  • In the GCS motor response, the scoring is:

    • 6 – Obeys commands

    • 5 – Localizes pain (purposeful movement to remove a painful stimulus)

    • 4 – Withdraws from pain (flexion, non-purposeful)

    • 3 – Abnormal flexion (decorticate)

    • 2 – Extension (decerebrate)

    • 1 – No motor response

  • The question states that the patient moves their limbs in an effort to remove a painful stimulus, which indicates purposeful movement toward the stimulus → localizing pain.
