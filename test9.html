The gland that is located below Hypophyseal Fossa of Sphenoid bone is:

💡
Explanation
-The pituitary gland is located in the hypophyseal fossa, which is a depression in the sphenoid bone at the base of the skull. It sits directly below the hypothalamus and is sometimes referred to as the "master gland" because it controls various other endocrine glands.

The hormone secreted by Posterior Pituitary gland
A. Insulin
B. Glucagon
C. Corticotrophin
D. Oxytocin ✓
💡
Explanation

The Posterior Pituitary gland (also known as the neurohypophysis) does not actually produce hormones; rather, it stores and releases hormones that are produced by the hypothalamus. Two key hormones stored and released by the posterior pituitary are:
1. Oxytocin – This hormone is involved in various functions, including stimulating uterine contractions during childbirth and the release of milk during breastfeeding.
2. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH), also known as Vasopressin – This hormone helps regulate water balance in the body by promoting water reabsorption in the kidneys.

Question 4 of 50
ADH secretion increases when there is ............
A. Hyperkalemia
B. Hypokalemia
C. Hypernatremia ✓
D. Hyponatremia
💡
Explanation

ADH (Antidiuretic hormone), also known as vasopressin, helps regulate the body's water balance by increasing water reabsorption in the kidneys. Its secretion is primarily stimulated by increased blood osmolality (which can occur in conditions like hypernatremia, where there is an elevated concentration of sodium in the blood).

When the body experiences hypernatremia (high sodium levels), it signals the need for more water retention to balance the sodium concentration, so the secretion of ADH increases. This leads to reduced urine output and retention of water in the body to restore proper fluid balance.

If blood glucose decreases below normal levels,
A. The nervous system will be excited
B. Breakdown of fat will increase ✓
C. Breakdown of protein will increase
D. A large amount of urine will be produced
💡
Explanation

  • When blood glucose levels fall below normal (hypoglycemia), the body compensates by increasing the breakdown of fat to produce fatty acids, which are then used for energy.

  • The breakdown of protein also increases, but typically to a lesser extent than fat, particularly when energy reserves are low.

  • A large amount of urine is associated with hyperglycemia (high blood glucose), not hypoglycemia.

  • The nervous system does not become excited in response to low glucose, but symptoms like confusion, weakness, and irritability can occur due to lack of glucose for energy.

  • While glucagon can have some effects on other tissues, its primary and most significant target is the liver.

Question 22 of 50
Calcitonin is secreted by
A. Thyroid gland ✓
B. Parathyroid gland
C. Both of the above
D. None of the above
💡
Explanation

  • Calcitonin is a hormone secreted by the thyroid gland, specifically by the parafollicular cells (also known as C-cells).

  • Its primary function is to lower blood calcium levels by inhibiting the activity of osteoclasts, which are responsible for breaking down bone tissue. This reduces the release of calcium into the bloodstream.

  • The parathyroid gland, on the other hand, secretes parathyroid hormone (PTH), which has the opposite effect of calcitonin, increasing blood calcium levels.

Question 26 of 50
The hormone that increases blood glucose levels and lipid breakdown, but slows, protein breakdown is
A. Glucagon
B. Insulin
C. Glucocorticoid
D. Growth hormone ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Growth hormone (GH)

    • ⬆️ Blood glucose – GH is anti–insulin in action, it decreases glucose uptake in tissues and increases gluconeogenesis.

    • ⬆️ Lipid breakdown (lipolysis) – mobilizes fat from adipose tissue for energy.

    • ⬇️ Protein breakdown – GH actually promotes protein synthesis and slows protein catabolism, helping in tissue growth.

Other options (why they’re incorrect here):

  • Glucagon – Increases blood glucose and promotes lipolysis, but it promotes protein breakdown in the liver to use amino acids for gluconeogenesis.

  • Insulin – Lowers blood glucose, promotes lipid storage, and decreases protein breakdown (opposite glucose effect).

  • Glucocorticoids – Increase blood glucose and can cause protein breakdown (muscle wasting), not slow it.
