💡
Explanation

0.9% sodium chloride solution, also known as normal saline, is isotonic with respect to body fluids, meaning it has the same osmotic pressure as blood and other bodily fluids.

0.09% sodium chloride is too dilute to be considered normal saline.

9.0% sodium chloride is hypertonic and would be much too concentrated for normal saline.

0.45% sodium chloride is referred to as half-normal saline and is hypotonic.

Question 9 of 50
The most common cause of hypomagnesemia in the ED is likely
A. Alcoholism ✓
B. Diuretic therapy
C. Acute tubular necrosis
D. Chronic diarrhea
💡
Explanation
Between 30% and 80% of alcoholics have magnesium deficiency. Patients with hypomagnesemia are frequently asymptomatic or manifest only nonspecific symptoms. The most prominent symptoms in the ED are neuromuscular and cardiovascular, and magnesium deficiency tends to mimic calcium deficiency. The mechanism of hypomagnesemia in alcoholism is thought to be a combination of malnutrition, increased renal excretion, and GI losses from vomiting and diarrhea. Diuretic therapy is also a very prevalent cause of hypomagnesemia, although the subsequent volume loss increases magnesium reabsorption in the proximal tubule. Therefore, magnesium depletion in the setting of diuretic therapy tends to be modest. Hypomagnesemia is the most common electrolyte abnormality in ambulatory diabetic patients and is also common in DKA. In these patients, magnesium is lost through the urine due to glycosuria.
Question 10 of 50
In respiratory acidosis, patient will show which of the following features?
A. A low pCO,
B. Lowered plasma bicarbonate
C. Reduced rate and depth of respiration ✓
D. Decreased hydrogen ion concentration in plasma
💡
Explanation
In respiratory acidosis, the body experiences an increase in carbon dioxide (pCO₂) levels, leading to an increase in hydrogen ion concentration (lowered pH). This is usually due to hypoventilation or impaired lung function. In response to this, the patient may exhibit a reduced rate and depth of respiration as the body tries to retain more carbon dioxide. The body compensates for the acidosis by increasing bicarbonate levels over time, but initially, plasma bicarbonate may not be significantly lowered. Therefore, the correct feature in respiratory acidosis is a reduced rate and depth of respiration.
