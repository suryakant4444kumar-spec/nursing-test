Question 1 of 50
Bad breath is known as......
A. Periodontal disease
B. Halitosis ✓
C. Cheilosis
D. Sordes
💡
Explanation

Periodontal disease: This refers to infections and conditions affecting the structures around the teeth, such as the gums and bone. It can contribute to bad breath, but it is not the term specifically used for bad breath.

Halitosis: This is the medical term for bad breath. It encompasses any unpleasant odor emanating from the mouth.

Cheilosis: This condition involves inflammation and cracking at the corners of the mouth, often due to vitamin deficiencies or fungal infections. It is not related to bad breath.

Sordes: This refers to a collection of dried secretions or crusts on the lips and in the mouth, often seen in severe illnesses. It is associated with poor oral hygiene but is not the term for bad breath itself.

A. Parietal peritoneum
B. Visceral peritoneum ✓
C. Abdominal peritoneum
D. Pelvic peritoneum
💡
Explanation

  • The visceral peritoneum is the serous membrane that covers and lines the organs within the abdominal cavity, such as the stomach, intestines, liver, and other digestive organs.

  • The parietal peritoneum, in contrast, lines the walls of the abdominal cavity.

  • The abdominal peritoneum and pelvic peritoneum are not terms used to describe the specific membrane lining the organs, but rather refer to the regions or parts of the peritoneal cavity.

💡
Explanation

Stimulation of the parasympathetic nervous system (primarily via the vagus nerve) enhances gastrointestinal motility and secretions to aid digestion. It promotes secretion of digestive enzymes, gastric acid, and increases peristalsis.

Explanation of other options:

  2. Decreased dilatation of portal vein branches – parasympathetic stimulation does not reduce portal vein dilation.

  3. Increased tone of anal sphincters – parasympathetic stimulation generally relaxes the internal anal sphincter to facilitate defecation.

Question 11 of 50
The structure that is located near the laryngopharynx to prevent a food bolus from entering the airway during deglutition is the
A. Cricoid
B. Epiglottis ✓
C. Palate
D. Tongue
💡
Explanation

The process of swallowing (deglutition) requires precise coordination to ensure food and liquid enter the esophagus and not the airway (larynx and trachea). The structure specifically designed for this protective function is the epiglottis.

Here is a detailed breakdown of each option:

  • Option 1: Cricoid - This is the cricoid cartilage, a complete ring of cartilage that forms the inferior part of the larynx. Its primary function is to provide structural support to the larynx and maintain a patent (open) airway. It is not involved in actively deflecting food.

  • Option 2: Epiglottis - This is the correct answer. The epiglottis is a leaf-shaped, elastic cartilage located at the superior opening of the larynx. During normal breathing, it is upright, allowing air to pass into the larynx. During swallowing, the larynx is pulled upward, and the base of the tongue pushes the epiglottis down like a trap door, covering the entrance to the larynx (the glottis). This action directs the food bolus over the epiglottis and into the esophagus behind it, effectively preventing aspiration.

  • Option 3: Palate - The palate forms the roof of the mouth and separates the oral cavity from the nasal cavity. The soft palate, specifically, elevates during swallowing to close off the nasopharynx, preventing food from going up into the nose. It does not protect the airway (larynx) from below.

  • Option 4: Tongue - The tongue is crucial for manipulating food into a bolus and initiating the swallowing process by pushing the bolus backward into the pharynx. While it helps push the epiglottis down, the tongue itself is not the structure that seals the airway.

Explanation

Chief cells of the gastric glands secrete pepsinogen, which is an inactive precursor (zymogen) of the enzyme pepsin. Pepsinogen is converted into active pepsin in the acidic environment of the stomach, where it helps in the digestion of proteins.

Explanation of other options:
2. Trypsinogen – secreted by the pancreas, not the stomach; it is the inactive precursor of trypsin.
3. Intrinsic factor – secreted by parietal cells, not chief cells.
4. Hydrochloric acid – secreted by parietal cells, not chief cells.

💡

Unlike peristalsis, which moves the contents forward, segmentation focuses on mixing and maximizing nutrient absorption.

Question 18 of 50
One of the end products of carbohydrate digestion in the small intestine is
A. Sucrose
B. Maltose
C. Lactose
D. Fructose ✓
💡
Explanation

The final end products of carbohydrate digestion in the small intestine are monosaccharides — mainly glucose, fructose, and galactose.

  • Complex carbohydrates are first broken down by enzymes like amylase and maltase, sucrase, and lactase.

  • Sucrose → broken into glucose + fructose by sucrase.

  • Maltose → broken into two glucose molecules by maltase.

  • Lactose → broken into glucose + galactose by lactase.

Thus, fructose is one of the end products of carbohydrate digestion in the small intestine.

Question 20 of 50
Most of the absorption of fats occurs in the
A. Duodenum
B. Jejunum ✓
C. Ileum
D. Caecum
💡
Explanation
Most of the absorption of fats occurs in the jejunum, which is the middle portion of the small intestine. After fats are emulsified by bile salts in the duodenum, they are broken down by pancreatic enzymes (such as lipase) into fatty acids and monoglycerides. These smaller molecules are then absorbed through the villi of the jejunum into the lymphatic system before entering the bloodstream.
Question 21 of 50
In intestinal absorption, the chylomicron is transferred to the lymph capillaries of the villus by
A. Reverse osmosis
B. Facilitated transport
C. Active transport
D. Passive diffusion ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Chylomicrons are tiny lipoprotein particles formed inside the intestinal mucosal cells after absorption of fats.

  • These chylomicrons contain triglycerides, cholesterol, and phospholipids.

  • They are too large to directly enter the blood capillaries, so they pass into the lacteals (lymph capillaries) present in the intestinal villi.

  • The movement of chylomicrons into the lymphatics occurs by passive diffusion, without requiring energy (ATP).

  • From the lymphatic system, they eventually enter the bloodstream through the thoracic duct.

Question 25 of 50
A 67-year-old female with diabetes, hypertension, and a history of an aortoiliac bypass graft presents with abdominal pain and dark, “funny-smelling” stools. She states the symptoms started 2 days ago and seem to have gone away as she had a “nearnormal” bowel movement this morning and no longer has pain. On examination, she has guaiac positive, dark brown, but not melenic stool. Which of the following must be excluded as a cause of hemorrhage?
A. Acute mesenteric ischemia
B. Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA)
C. Aortoenteric fistula ✓
D. Duodenal ulcer
💡
Explanation
This patient has an aortoenteric fistula until proven otherwise. The classic presentation is abdominal pain and GI bleeding that resolves spontaneously (the so-called herald bleed) in a patient with a prior history of aortic graft. The initial bleeding is thought to stop when splanchnic pressure drops and allows adequate clot to form. However, the initial bleed is often followed by a massive and often fatal hemorrhage days or possibly weeks later. The fistula is caused by S. aureus or Escherichia coli infection of the prior graft and typically forms between the aorta and the distal duodenum. Therefore, all patients with a history of aortic graft and GI bleeding should undergo esophagogastroduodenoscopy to search for a fistula in the distal duodenum. Most of these patients, however, have an alternative, more common cause of GI bleeding.
