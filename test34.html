Question 1 of 50
The "golden period" for suturing traumatic wounds is typically considered to be:
A. 1-2 hours
B. 6-8 hours
 ✓
C. 12-18 hours
D. 24 hours
💡
Explanation
The golden period is the time after injury when the risk of infection is low enough that primary closure can be performed. While variable, 6-8 hours is the general guideline for most body parts. Wounds on the face have a longer golden period (up to 24 hours) due to excellent blood supply.
Question 2 of 50
Which sign is the least reliable indicator of an infected wound?
A. Purulent drainage
B. Periwound erythema
C. Localized warmth
D. Serosanguinous drainage
 ✓
💡
Explanation
Serosanguinous drainage (a clear, pink, thin, and watery fluid) is a normal part of the inflammatory phase of healing. Purulent drainage, erythema, and warmth are classic cardinal signs of infection.
Question 3 of 50
The primary purpose of packing a deep, tunneling wound is to:
A. Absorb all moisture to keep the wound dry.
B. Prevent premature surface closure while allowing healing from the base upward.
 ✓
C. Provide a scaffold for skin grafts to adhere to.
D. Apply direct pressure to stop capillary bleeding.
💡
Explanation
If a deep wound closes at the surface first, it can trap bacteria and exudate inside, leading to an abscess. Packing keeps the wound edges apart, absorbs exudate, and ensures the wound heals by secondary intention from the deepest tissue outward.
Question 4 of 50
Which dressing type is most appropriate for a clean, granulating wound with minimal exudate?
A. Alginate
B. Transparent Film
C. Hydrocolloid
 ✓
D. Foam
💡
Explanation
Hydrocolloid dressings are ideal for this scenario. They maintain a moist environment, promote autolytic debridement, and are waterproof. Alginates (A) are for heavily exuding wounds. Films (B) are for superficial wounds or as a secondary dressing. Foam (D) is for moderate to heavy exudate.
Question 5 of 50
When irrigating a contaminated wound, the minimum recommended pressure is achieved by using:
A. A 35-mL syringe and an 18-gauge angiocatheter.
 ✓
B. A bulb syringe.
C. A 10-mL syringe and a 25-gauge needle.
D. Pouring saline directly from a bottle.
💡
Explanation
This combination generates approximately 8-15 psi, which is effective at removing bacteria and debris without damaging tissue or driving bacteria deeper. A bulb syringe or pouring generates significantly lower pressure (<1-2 psi), which is ineffective. A small-bore needle would generate too high a pressure.
Question 6 of 50
The main advantage of a monofilament suture over a braided suture is:
A. Higher tensile strength.
B. Better knot security.
C. Reduced risk of harboring bacteria.
 ✓
D. Faster absorption rate.
💡
Explanation
Braided sutures have interstices (small spaces) between fibers that can trap bacteria, potentially leading to infection. Monofilament sutures have a single, smooth strand that minimizes this risk.

Question 30 of 50
Which stage of a pressure ulcer is characterized by full-thickness tissue loss with visible bone, tendon, or muscle?
A. Stage I
B. Stage II
C. Stage III
D. Stage IV ✓
💡
Explanation

Pressure ulcers are classified into four stages based on depth of tissue damage:

  • Stage I – Intact skin with non-blanchable redness.

  • Stage II – Partial-thickness skin loss involving epidermis and/or dermis (blister or shallow ulcer).

  • Stage III – Full-thickness skin loss involving subcutaneous tissue, but bone, tendon, or muscle are NOT exposed.

  • Stage IV – Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle. There may be tunneling and undermining.

Stage IV is the most severe form and carries a high risk of infection, including osteomyelitis and sepsis.
