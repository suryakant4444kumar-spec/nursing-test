Question 1 of 50
All of following are clinical feature of a congenital hypertrophied pyloric stenosis in ' children are except
A. Dehydration
B. Nonbilirus vomiting
C. Regurgitation ✓
D. Visible peristalsis
💡
Explanation

The correct option is Option 3: Regurgitation.

  • In Congenital Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (CHPS), the hallmark clinical feature is projectile vomiting, which is forceful and dynamic. Regurgitation is generally considered a passive process where stomach contents flow back up without force, and while it might be used colloquially to describe mild symptoms, it is not a defining clinical feature of this condition.
  • Explanation
  • Nonbilious vomiting: This is the classic presentation. Because the obstruction occurs at the pylorus (before the bile duct entry into the duodenum), the vomitus contains only gastric contents and no bile.
  • Visible peristalsis: As the stomach tries to force food through the narrowed pyloric canal, vigorous gastric contractions may be visible moving from left to right across the infant's upper abdomen.
  • Dehydration: Repeated, forceful vomiting leads to significant loss of fluids and electrolytes (notably hydrogen, chloride, and potassium), often resulting in dehydration and hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis.

Why other options are incorrect --
Option 1: Dehydration is a direct consequence of the persistent projectile vomiting seen in CHPS.
Option 2: Nonbilious vomiting is the most characteristic symptom used to diagnose CHPS.
Option 4: Visible peristalsis is a classic physical sign found during examination of an infant with pyloric obstruction.

Question 11 of 50
For what should a nurse assess in a newborn of a mother who is known to abuse opioids?
A. Dehydration
B. Hyperactivity
C. Hypotonicity of muscles ✓
D. Prolonged periods of sleep
💡
Explanation

Newborns of mothers who abuse opioids are at risk for Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS), which can include symptoms such as:

- Muscle rigidity or hypotonicity
- Irritability
- Tremors
- High-pitched crying
- Feeding difficulties

The nurse should assess for signs of NAS and monitor the newborn's behavior, muscle tone, and overall well-being. Hypotonicity (low muscle tone) is one of the potential symptoms, although hypertonicity (increased muscle tone) can also occur.

Other options might not be directly related to opioid exposure:
- Dehydration could be a concern due to feeding difficulties or excessive sweating, but it's not the primary focus.
- Hyperactivity might be seen in NAS, but hypotonicity or other withdrawal symptoms are more specific to opioid exposure.
- Prolonged periods of sleep are less likely, as NAS often causes irritability and difficulty sleeping.

Question 37 of 50
Which of the following is a complication of Hirschsprung disease if left untreated?
A. Bowel perforation ✓
B. Jaundice
C. Malnutrition
D. Chronic diarrhea
💡
Explanation
The correct option is Option 1: Bowel perforation.
Explanation
  • If left untreated, Hirschsprung disease can lead to severe and potentially life-threatening complications, the most serious being Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC). The accumulation of stool due to the lack of nerve cells leads to bacterial overgrowth, inflammation, and increased pressure in the colon, which can ultimately cause a bowel perforation (a hole or rupture in the intestinal wall). Bowel perforation is a medical emergency that allows stool and bacteria to leak into the abdominal cavity, potentially leading to a deadly infection called sepsis.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: Jaundice is a symptom that may be associated with the condition in newborns, but it is not a direct complication of the disease if left untreated in the way that a bowel perforation is.
  • Option 3: Malnutrition is a potential consequence of untreated Hirschsprung disease due to impaired nutrient absorption and poor feeding, but the more immediate and acute, life-threatening complication among the choices is a bowel perforation resulting from severe obstruction and enterocolitis.
  • Option 4: Chronic diarrhea is not a primary complication. While newborns with the condition can experience "explosive stools" or watery diarrhea (especially as a symptom of enterocolitis), the primary characteristic is severe chronic constipation and obstruction.
