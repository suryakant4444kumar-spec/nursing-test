Question 4 of 50
Which of following is a risk factor for bladder cancer?
A. High fat diet
B. Prolonged bed rest
C. Exposure to dyes and paints ✓
D. High fiber diet
💡
Explanation

  • Bladder cancer risk increases with exposure to industrial chemicals.

  • Workers in dye, paint, rubber, leather, and printing industries are especially at risk.

  • These chemicals contain aromatic amines, which are known bladder carcinogens.

  • They get absorbed and later excreted in urine, damaging bladder lining.

  • High fat or high fiber diet does not significantly increase bladder cancer risk.

  • Therefore, exposure to dyes and paints is a major occupational risk factor.

A. Certain drugs
B. Tobacco use ✓
C. Obesity
D. Oral contraceptives
💡
Explanation

  • Tobacco use is the strongest and most well-established risk factor for both renal (kidney) and bladder cancers.

  • Carcinogens in tobacco are filtered by the kidneys and stored in the bladder, damaging the lining.

  • Smokers have 2–4 times higher risk of developing these cancers.

  • Certain drugs may slightly increase risk but are not the major cause.

  • Obesity is a risk factor for kidney cancer but not strongly linked to bladder cancer.

  • Therefore, tobacco use is the most significant and common risk factor for both cancers.

Question 11 of 50
Risk factors for endometrial cancer include
A. Increased amount of circulating estrogen
B. Early menarche
C. Presence of other reproductive organ cancer
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

Endometrial cancer (cancer of the uterine lining) is strongly influenced by hormonal exposure, particularly unopposed estrogen. Let's analyze how each option is a well-established risk factor.

  • Option 1: Increased amount of circulating estrogen - This is the single most important risk factor. Estrogen stimulates the growth of the endometrium. When this growth is not balanced by progesterone (a state known as "unopposed estrogen"), it can lead to hyperplasia and eventually cancer. This can occur in conditions like:

    • Obesity (fat cells convert androgens into estrogen)

    • Estrogen-producing tumors (e.g., granulosa cell tumor of the ovary)

    • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

    • Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) with estrogen alone (without progesterone)

  • Option 2: Early menarche - Starting menstruation at an early age (e.g., before 12) increases the number of years a woman is exposed to menstrual cycles and, therefore, to estrogen. This lengthens the cumulative lifetime exposure to estrogen, which is a key driver of endometrial cancer risk.

  • Option 3: Presence of other reproductive organ cancer - This is a significant risk factor for two main reasons:

    1. Genetic Syndromes: The most important is Lynch Syndrome (Hereditary Non-Polyposis Colorectal Cancer or HNPCC). Women with this genetic condition have a very high lifetime risk (up to 60%) of developing endometrial cancer, and it is often their first diagnosed cancer. They are also at high risk for colorectal and ovarian cancer. Therefore, a personal history of colorectal cancer or a family history of related cancers increases endometrial cancer risk.

    2. Shared Risk Factors: Cancers like estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer share similar risk factors (e.g., obesity, nulliparity). Furthermore, some treatments for other cancers (like Tamoxifen for breast cancer) can increase the risk of endometrial cancer.

Question 12 of 50

A. Unopposed estrogen therapy
B. Obesity
C. Hypertension
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
Type 1 endometrial carcinoma is estrogen-dependent and is commonly associated with conditions causing prolonged estrogen exposure.
● Unopposed estrogen therapy increases endometrial proliferation, significantly raising cancer risk.
● Obesity leads to peripheral conversion of androgens to estrogen in adipose tissue, increasing estrogen levels.
● Hypertension is part of the metabolic syndrome (along with obesity and diabetes) commonly associated with Type 1 endometrial carcinoma.
● Hence, all listed factors are recognized risk factors, making All of the above the correct answer.

  • CT and X-ray are not primary diagnostic tools for prostate cancer.

Question 28 of 50
Treatment of cancer of prostate includes
A. Bilateral orchiectomy
B. Luteinizing hormone releasing hormone agonists
C. Antiandrogen drugs
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Prostate cancer growth is largely dependent on androgens (male hormones).

  • Bilateral orchiectomy reduces testosterone production by removing the testes.

  • LHRH agonists (e.g., leuprolide) suppress testosterone chemically, acting like medical castration.

  • Anti-androgen drugs (e.g., bicalutamide) block the effect of testosterone on cancer cells.

  • These therapies are often used alone or in combination as androgen deprivation therapy (ADT).
