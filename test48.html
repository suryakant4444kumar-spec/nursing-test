What does the acronym RICE stand for in the first aid treatment of bleeds in hemophilia?

B. Rapid Intervention, Cold pack, Elastic bandage, Elevation
C. Rest, Immobilize, Cold, Elevate
D. Reduce, Ice, Compress, Engage
💡
Explanation
• Rest: Prevents further injury and minimizes bleeding. • Ice: Reduces swelling and pain by constricting blood vessels. • Compression: Limits internal bleeding and supports the joint or muscle. • Elevation: Reduces blood flow to the injured area by lowering pressure, which helps slow bleeding. RICE is a simple, effective method to manage joint and muscle bleeds, especially important in hemophilia where internal bleeding can cause joint damage

Question 6 of 50
What should be used to wrap an ice pack before applying it?
A. A dry towel
B. A damp towel ✓
C. A newspaper
D. A plastic bag
💡
Explanation

When applying an ice pack to the body, it should never be placed directly on the skin because it can cause skin irritation, frostbite, or tissue damage.

• A dry towel is commonly used to wrap the ice pack before application.
• The towel acts as a protective barrier between the ice and the skin.
• This helps prevent excessive cold exposure and protects the skin.
• Cold application is used to reduce swelling, pain, inflammation, and bleeding after injury.
• Usually, the ice pack is applied for 15–20 minutes at a time with proper protection.

Therefore, an ice pack should be wrapped in a dry towel before applying it.

B. Iron 10mg/day
C. Vitamin. B12 25mg/day
D. Folic acid 10 mg /day
💡
Explanation

Sickle cell anemia causes increased red blood cell turnover and hemolysis, leading to higher demand for folic acid to support new red blood cell production.

Folic acid supplementation (5 mg/day) is commonly prescribed to prevent folate deficiency and support erythropoiesis.

Iron supplementation is generally avoided unless there is documented iron deficiency because iron overload can be harmful.

Vitamin B12 is not routinely used unless a specific deficiency exists.

The difference between 5 mg and 10 mg folic acid is not significant clinically; 5 mg/day is standard.

Question 34 of 50
Health education to a client on anticoagulant therapy includes
A. Report any signs of bleeding
B. Do lab studies such as PT, INR as ordered
C. Both a and b ✓
D. None of the above
💡
Explanation

Health education for a client on anticoagulant therapy should include both of the following:

  • Reporting any signs of bleeding: Patients on anticoagulants are at increased risk for bleeding, so it is important for them to report any unusual signs such as bruising, blood in urine or stool, or prolonged bleeding from cuts.
  • Doing lab studies such as PT (prothrombin time) and INR (International Normalized Ratio): Regular monitoring of these lab values helps ensure that the anticoagulant therapy is at a therapeutic level and that the patient is not at increased risk of bleeding or clotting.

Question 38 of 50
Hemolytic anemias are characterized by
A. Shortened red cell survival
B. Bone marrow shows erythroid hyperplasia
C. Reticulocyte count and unconjugated bilirubin level will be elevated
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
  • Shortened red cell survival – Red blood cells are destroyed prematurely in hemolytic anemia, leading to a reduced lifespan of the cells.
  • Bone marrow shows erythroid hyperplasia – The bone marrow compensates for the increased destruction of red blood cells by producing more, leading to an increase in erythroid (red blood cell) precursors.
  • Elevated reticulocyte count and unconjugated bilirubin level – Reticulocyte count is elevated as the bone marrow attempts to produce more red blood cells. Unconjugated bilirubin levels rise due to the breakdown of hemoglobin from the destroyed red blood cells.

Explanation

  • Reticulocyte level is typically increased in sickle cell anemia as the body compensates for the destruction of sickled red blood cells (hemolysis) by producing more immature red blood cells (reticulocytes) to replace the lost cells.

  • Leukocyte level may increase during an infection or inflammation, but it is not a primary feature of sickle cell anemia itself.

  • Hemoglobin level is often decreased in sickle cell anemia due to the altered form of hemoglobin (hemoglobin S) and chronic hemolysis.

  • Hematocrit level can be low in sickle cell anemia because of the reduction in healthy red blood cells.
    Therefore, reticulocyte level is the parameter that would be increased in a child with sickle cell anemia.

Most common manifestation of sickle cell anaemia is:
A. Severe anaemia
B. Haemolytic crisis
C. Sequestration crisis
D. Repeated episodes of skeletal pain ✓
💡
Explanation
The most common and hallmark manifestation of sickle cell anemia is recurrent episodes of severe pain, often referred to as sickle cell crises. These pain episodes typically occur due to the blockage of blood flow in the small blood vessels by sickled red blood cells, leading to tissue ischemia and pain, particularly in the bones and joints (skeletal pain). This condition is often triggered by factors such as dehydration, cold temperatures, and infection. While other options like anaemia, haemolytic crisis, and sequestration crisis are also common complications of sickle cell disease, pain crises are the most frequently encountered manifestation.
