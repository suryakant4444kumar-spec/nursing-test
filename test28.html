Question 2 of 50
Which of the following is the terminal method of contra ceptive in male?
A. IUD
B. Barrier method
C. Vasectomy ✓
D. Condom
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 3: Vasectomy.
  • Explanation
  • A terminal method of contraception refers to a surgical, permanent, and generally irreversible procedure that results in sterilization.
  • Vasectomy is the terminal method for males.
  • It involves a surgical procedure where the vasa deferentia (the ducts that carry sperm from the testes) are cut or sealed.
  • This prevents sperm from being released during ejaculation, thereby preventing fertilization.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: IUD (Intrauterine Device): This is a long-acting but reversible contraceptive device inserted into the uterus, making it a method exclusively for females.
  • Option 2: Barrier method: This is a category of temporary spacing methods (like diaphragms or cervical caps) that physically prevent sperm from meeting the egg.
  • Option 4: Condom: While used by males, a condom is a temporary barrier method intended for use during a single act of intercourse; it is not a terminal or permanent solution.
Question 3 of 50
Multiload device refers to?
A. 1st generation IUCD
B. 2st generation IUCD ✓
C. OC
D. Barrier contraceptive
💡
Explanation
  • Multiload devices (such as ML Cu 250 or ML Cu 375) refer to copper-releasing intrauterine devices (IUCDs), which are categorized as 2nd generation IUCDs. They are designed to release copper ions to prevent fertilization, typically used for long-term contraception.
  • 1st generation: Inert (e.g., Lippes loop).
  • 2nd generation: Copper-releasing (e.g., Copper T, Multiload).
  • 3rd generation: Hormonal (e.g., LNG-20).
  • Therefore, the correct option is Option 2: 2nd generation IUCD.

Question 6 of 50
IUD associated most commonly with expulsion is
A. Lippes loop ✓
B. Cu-T 200
C. Cu-T 380
D. LNG IUD
💡
Explanation
  • Based on comparative clinical studies, the intrauterine device (IUD) most commonly associated with expulsion among the provided options is the Lippes loop (Option 1).
  • Comparison of Expulsion Rates
  • Studies comparing older, non-medicated IUDs with modern medicated (copper or hormonal) devices consistently show higher expulsion rates for the Lippes loop:
  • Lippes loop vs. Cu-T 380: In randomized clinical trials, the expulsion rate for the Lippes loop was approximately 6.7%, while the Cu-T 380A had a rate of 3.5% at 12 months post-insertion.
  • Lippes loop vs. Cu-T 200: Research across multiple countries found that Cu-T 200 expulsion rates were significantly lower than those of the Lippes loop. Specifically, for women with three or fewer children, the Cu-T 200 showed a marked advantage in retention.
  • Copper IUDs vs. LNG-IUD: In some specific cohorts, copper IUD users were found to be more prone to expulsion than levonorgestrel-releasing (LNG) IUD users.

Question 12 of 50
The composition of Mala-N used for spacing pregnancy is
A. Norgestrel
B. Ethinyl estradiol
C. Desogestrel
D. both a and b ✓
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4: both a and b.
  • Explanation
  • Mala-N is a combined oral contraceptive (COC) pill distributed by the Government of India under the National Family Planning Programme. It works by using a combination of two synthetic hormones to prevent pregnancy:
  • Ethinyl Estradiol (0.03 mg): A synthetic estrogen that inhibits ovulation and alters the uterine lining.
  • Levonorgestrel (0.15 mg): A synthetic progestogen (a type of Norgestrel) that thickens cervical mucus and prevents fertilization.
  • Because Mala-N contains both a progestogen (Norgestrel/Levonorgestrel) and an estrogen (Ethinyl estradiol), Option 4 is the most accurate choice among those provided.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: Norgestrel – While this is a primary active ingredient, it is only half of the composition. Mala-N is a "combined" pill, meaning it must contain both estrogen and progestogen to be classified as such.
  • Option 2: Ethinyl estradiol – Similar to Option 1, this is only one part of the formulation. A pill containing only estrogen would not be used for this specific contraceptive purpose.
  • Option 3: Desogestrel – This is a third-generation progestogen found in other brands (like Novelon or Femilon) but is not the progestogen used in the Mala-N formulation.

Question 15 of 50
IUD needs to be removed if............. Complication occurs
A. Excessive bleeding
B. Uterine perforation
C. Planning for pregnancy
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4: All of the above.
  • Explanation
  • An Intrauterine Device (IUD) must be removed if any of these conditions are met to ensure patient safety and health:
  • Excessive bleeding: Heavy or irregular vaginal bleeding is a common side effect, but if it becomes excessive, severe, or causes anemia, removal is recommended.
  • Uterine perforation: This is a serious, though rare, complication where the IUD pierces the uterine wall. Immediate removal (often via laparoscopy) is required to prevent damage to neighboring organs.
  • Planning for pregnancy: Since the IUD is a reversible contraceptive, it is removed whenever a patient decides they wish to conceive. Fertility typically returns to normal immediately after removal.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1, Option 2, and Option 3 are all valid individual reasons for removal, but they are not the only reasons. Therefore, selecting just one would be incomplete, making Option 4 the most accurate choice.

Question 19 of 50
Abute contraindication to the OCP includes
A. Thromboembolism
B. Liver disease
C. Ca breast
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4: All of the above.
  • Explanation
  • According to guidelines from the World Health Organization (WHO) and major medical bodies, combined oral contraceptive pills (COCs) are absolutely contraindicated (Category 4) in several high-risk conditions because the health risks outweigh any benefits.
  • Thromboembolism: Estrogen increases the risk of blood clots. A current or past history of venous thromboembolism (VTE), deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism, or stroke is a primary contraindication.
  • Liver disease: Active or severe liver conditions, including malignant liver tumors, hepatocellular adenoma, and severe cirrhosis, preclude OCP use because the liver metabolizes the hormones.
  • Ca breast: Current or past breast cancer is an absolute contraindication as it is an estrogen-dependent neoplasia, and hormonal intake could stimulate tumor growth.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Options 1, 2, and 3 are all individually correct as absolute contraindications. Therefore, selecting only one would be incomplete, making "All of the above" the most accurate choice.

Question 21 of 50
Which of the following is a common side effect of Intrauterine Device?
A. Vaginal Bleeding ✓
B. Pelvic Inflammatory disease
C. Uterine perforation
D. Ectopic pregnancy
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1 : Vaginal Bleeding.
  • Explanation
  • Vaginal Bleeding: This is considered the most common side effect of an Intrauterine Device (IUD). Both copper and hormonal IUDs frequently cause changes in menstrual patterns.
  • Copper IUDs often lead to heavier, longer periods and spotting between cycles, especially in the first 3–6 months.
  • Hormonal IUDs typically cause irregular spotting or light bleeding initially, though they usually lead to lighter or absent periods over time.
  • Approximately 5% to 15% of users have their IUD removed within the first year specifically due to bleeding issues.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2 : Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): While IUDs slightly increase the risk of PID within the first 20 days post-insertion (due to bacteria introduced during the procedure), it is a serious complication rather than a common side effect. Overall, the long-term risk of PID in IUD users is low and similar to the general population.
  • Option 3 : Uterine Perforation: This is a rare and serious complication that occurs when the device punctures the uterine wall, typically during insertion. It occurs in only about 1 in 1,000 procedures (0.1%).
  • Option 4 : Ectopic Pregnancy: IUDs are highly effective at preventing all types of pregnancy, actually reducing the overall absolute risk of ectopic pregnancy compared to using no contraception. However, in the rare event that a pregnancy does occur with an IUD in place, there is a higher relative chance that it will be ectopic.
