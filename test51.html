Question 5 of 50
Which condition is most commonly confused with Bell's Palsy?
A. Stroke ✓
B. Migraine
C. Meniere's disease
D. Trigeminal neuralgia
💡
Explanation

Bell's Palsy is an acute, unilateral lower motor neuron facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) palsy causing sudden facial muscle weakness or paralysis. It is often confused with stroke, especially when the facial weakness is sudden. However, in stroke (upper motor neuron lesion), typically the forehead muscles are spared due to bilateral cortical innervation, whereas in Bell’s Palsy, the entire half of the face including the forehead is affected.

  • Option 1 (Stroke): Most commonly confused because both present with facial weakness but differ in pattern and associated symptoms.

  • Option 2 (Migraine): May cause neurological symptoms but does not cause isolated facial paralysis.

  • Option 3 (Meniere's disease): Causes vertigo and hearing loss, not facial paralysis.

  • Option 4 (Trigeminal neuralgia): Causes facial pain but not paralysis.

Question 22 of 50
Which clinical findings does the nurse anticipate a client with an exacerbation of multiple sclerosis to experience? EXCEPT
A. Double vision
B. Resting tremors ✓
C. Flaccid paralysis
D. Scanning speech
💡
Explanation

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a disease characterized by the demyelination of nerve fibers in the central nervous system, leading to a wide variety of symptoms. During an exacerbation of MS, the symptoms may become more pronounced.

  • Option 1 (Double vision): Double vision (diplopia) is a common symptom in multiple sclerosis, caused by damage to the optic nerves or the muscles controlling eye movement. This is a typical finding in MS exacerbations.

  • Option 2 (Resting tremors): Resting tremors are more commonly associated with Parkinson's disease, not MS. Although MS can cause tremors, they are typically action tremors (seen when trying to move), not resting tremors.

  • Option 3 (Flaccid paralysis): Flaccid paralysis is not typical in MS. In MS, paralysis is usually spastic, with stiff, tight muscles rather than flaccid (loose) muscles.

  • Option 4 (Scanning speech): Scanning speech (slow, segmented speech) is a classic symptom of MS, caused by motor control issues affecting the muscles responsible for speech.

This phenomenon occurs when the body temperature rises, affecting nerve conduction, and can cause increased weakness, blurred vision, or other neurological symptoms.

Question 28 of 50
A nurse is caring for a client in the home who has the diagnosis of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). Which position should the nurse recommend that the client assume after eating?
A. Sims
B. Sitting ✓
C. Side-lying
D. Semi-Fowler
💡
Explanation

  • Option 1: Sims position: This position, which involves lying on the left side with the right knee drawn up toward the chest, is typically used for procedures or comfort in specific conditions. It is not recommended immediately after eating because it can increase the risk of aspiration.

  • Option 2: Sitting: Sitting upright after eating is the most appropriate recommendation for a client with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). Sitting upright helps with swallowing and digestion and reduces the risk of aspiration, which is a major concern for ALS patients due to their weakening muscles that affect swallowing and respiratory function.

  • Option 3: Side-lying: Side-lying may not be ideal after eating, especially for ALS patients, as it could increase the risk of aspiration or choking if the client has difficulty swallowing.

  • Option 4: Semi-Fowler: Although the semi-Fowler position (elevating the head of the bed to about 30–45 degrees) can help with breathing and reduce reflux, it is not as effective as sitting upright for preventing aspiration after meals in ALS patients.
