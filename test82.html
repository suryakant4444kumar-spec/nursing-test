B. Reaction formation
C. Cognitive distortion

💡
Explanation
Transference occurs when a client projects feelings, emotions, or expectations from a past relationship (often with a significant figure like a parent) onto someone else, in this case, the nurse. The client is transferring their feelings about their father onto the nurse, which is a classic example of transference in therapy.
Question 3 of 50
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) may be used to treat:
A. Dysthymic disorder
B. Major depressive disorder ✓
C. Cyclothymic disorder
D. Bipolar I disorder
💡
Explanation
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is primarily used to treat major depressive disorder, particularly in cases where the condition is severe, treatment-resistant, or accompanied by symptoms such as suicidal ideation. It can also be used for bipolar I disorder during severe depressive episodes. However, it is not typically used for dysthymic disorder or cyclothymic disorder unless they are severe and unresponsive to other treatments.

Question 10 of 50
Psychogenic amnesia is characterized by
A. Antegrade Amnesia
B. Retrograde Amnesia
C. Both with confabulation
D. Patchy impairment of Personal memories ✓
💡
Explanation
Psychogenic (dissociative) amnesia typically produces a selective, often sudden loss of autobiographical/personal memories (who one is, personal events), i.e. a patchy retrograde impairment. - The memory loss is usually retrograde (loss of past personal information) rather than antegrade; new learning and retention (anterograde memory and procedural skills) are generally preserved. - The amnesia is often selective for stressful or traumatic events and can spare general knowledge and skills (semantic and procedural memory). - Presentation may include sudden onset after psychological stress and sometimes a dissociative fugue (travel, identity confusion). - Confabulation (spontaneous fabrication of memories) is more characteristic of organic amnestic syndromes (e.g., Korsakoff) and is not a defining feature of psychogenic amnesia; if frank confabulation is prominent, an organic cause should be strongly considered. - Neurological exam and investigations (EEG, CT/MRI) are often normal in psychogenic amnesia, supporting a functional rather than structural cause.

Question 13 of 50
A mental disturbance characterized by confusion, disordered speech, clouding of consciousness and hallucinations is known as
A. Dementia
B. Delirium ✓
C. Hallucination
D. Psychosis
💡
Explanation

  • Delirium:

    • Acute onset of confusion, disorganized thinking, clouded consciousness, and hallucinations or illusions.

    • Often fluctuates throughout the day.

    • Usually caused by medical illness, intoxication, or withdrawal.

  • Dementia:

    • Chronic, progressive decline in memory and cognition, usually without clouding of consciousness in early stages.

    • Not typically associated with acute confusion or hallucinations early on.

  • Hallucination:

    • A symptom — false sensory perception — not a syndrome or diagnosis by itself.

  • Psychosis:

    • A broad term indicating loss of reality testing, may include delusions and hallucinations, but does not necessarily involve clouded consciousness.

Question 14 of 50
The most common cause of dementia is
A. Stroke
B. Alzheimer's disease ✓
C. Thyroid abnormalities
D. Trauma
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 2: Alzheimer's disease.
  • Explanation
  • Alzheimer's disease is universally recognized as the most common cause of dementia, accounting for an estimated 60% to 80% of all cases. It is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the accumulation of amyloid plaques and tau tangles in the brain, which leads to the death of nerve cells and a steady decline in cognitive function.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: Stroke: While strokes are a major cause of vascular dementia, they are the second most common cause of dementia, not the first.
  • Option 3: Thyroid abnormalities: These are considered secondary or reversible causes of dementia-like symptoms. While they can cause significant cognitive impairment, they are far less common than Alzheimer's.
  • Option 4: Trauma: Traumatic brain injuries (TBI) can lead to dementia (such as chronic traumatic encephalopathy) or increase the risk of developing it later in life, but they represent a much smaller percentage of total cases compared to Alzheimer's.

Question 17 of 50
What question best assesses the client's long-term memory?
A. “What is your current age?”
B. “What is today's date?”
C. “What occurred last January?”
D. “What is your date of birth?” ✓
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4: “What is your date of birth?”
  • Explanation
  • To assess long-term (remote) memory, the nurse must ask about information from the distant past that can be independently verified. A client's date of birth is a piece of semantic memory stored over many decades and remains relatively stable, making it an ideal indicator of remote recall.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: “What is your current age?” This is frequently used to assess orientation or general knowledge, but since the answer changes annually and is related to current status, it is less effective for specifically isolating long-term storage than a fixed historical fact like a birth date.
  • Option 2: “What is today's date?” This assesses orientation to time and immediate/recent memory. It does not measure the retrieval of information from years ago.
  • Option 3: “What occurred last January?” This assesses recent memory (events from the past few months). Long-term memory assessment typically focuses on events or facts from much earlier in a person's life.
Question 18 of 50
The nurse would expect a client with early Alzheimer's disease to have problems with:
A. Remembering his own name
B. Balancing an account &check book. ✓
C. Self-care measures.
D. Unidentified any object and family member.
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 2: Balancing an account check book.
  • Explanation
  • In the early stage of Alzheimer's disease, individuals typically experience difficulty with complex cognitive tasks and executive functioning. Balancing a check book is a prime example of such a task, as it requires:
  • Mathematical calculations and numerical skills.
  • Sequential planning and organization.
  • High levels of attention to detail and problem-solving.
  • While minor memory lapses (like forgetting a name or misplacing keys) occur early, the inability to perform functional, multi-step tasks like managing finances is a key diagnostic indicator for nurses assessing early cognitive decline.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: Remembering his own name. Personal identity and long-term memory of one's own name are typically retained until the very late stages of the disease.
  • Option 3: Self-care measures. While judgment regarding grooming might slightly decrease, significant issues with basic self-care (bathing, dressing, eating) usually manifest in the middle to late stages.
  • Option 4: Unidentified any object and family member. Agnosia (failure to recognize objects) and failure to recognize family members are characteristic of moderate to severe (mid-to-late) Alzheimer's, not the early stage.

Question 20 of 50
What nursing intervention is most helpful in meeting the needs of an older adult with the diagnosis of dementia of the Alzheimer type?
A. Providing nutritious foods high in carbohydrates and proteins
B. Offering opportunities for choices in the daily schedule to stimulate interest
C. Simplifying the environment as much as possible and eliminating the need for decisions and choices ✓
D. Developing a consistent plan with fixed time schedules to provide for emotional needs
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 3: Simplifying the environment as much as possible and eliminating the need for decisions and choices.
  • Explanation
  • For patients with Alzheimer's disease, cognitive decline leads to an inability to process complex information or make decisions. Simplifying the environment and eliminating the need for choices is the most helpful intervention because it reduces sensory overload, anxiety, and frustration. When faced with multiple choices, these patients often become overwhelmed, which can lead to agitation or complete withdrawal. Providing a simple, predictable environment helps maintain the patient's sense of security and minimizes behavioral outbursts.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: While adequate nutrition is important to prevent malnutrition and weight loss in dementia, specific high-carbohydrate and high-protein diets are not the most helpful primary nursing intervention for the overall cognitive and behavioral needs of the patient.
  • Option 2: Offering many choices is counterproductive. Individuals with Alzheimer's lose the executive function required to make decisions; providing "opportunities for choices" often causes confusion and distress rather than stimulating interest.
  • Option 4: Although consistency is vital, a "fixed time schedule" can sometimes be too rigid if it does not account for the patient's fluctuating levels of agitation or fatigue. The priority is simplifying the daily experience to match their declining cognitive capacity.
Question 21 of 50
What is the most appropriate nursing intervention for clients who exhibit mild cognitive impairment?
A. Behavioural confrontation
B. Reality orientation
C. Reflective communication ✓
D. Reminiscence group therapy
💡
Explanation

  • Reflective communication involves understanding the patient's feelings and thoughts, and responding in a way that acknowledges them. For patients with mild cognitive impairment, it helps in maintaining communication, validating their experiences, and promoting a supportive environment, which is important for their cognitive and emotional well-being.

  • Option 1: Behavioral confrontation is not appropriate for mild cognitive impairment as it can increase confusion and frustration for the patient. It is more suitable for addressing challenging behaviors, but not for cognitive impairments.

  • Option 2: Reality orientation is typically used for more advanced cognitive decline, such as in dementia, where patients have significant disorientation about time, place, and identity. It may not be as effective or appropriate for those with mild cognitive impairment.

  • Option 4: Reminiscence group therapy can be helpful, but it’s not the primary intervention for mild cognitive impairment. It’s more suited for patients who may benefit from reflecting on past experiences to improve social interaction and memory, but reflective communication is more direct and person-centered for this stage.

Question 22 of 50
Causes of delirium are….
A. Head Trauma
B. Infection
C. Metabolic Disorder
D. All of the Above ✓
💡
Explanation

Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention, awareness, and cognition. It is caused by a direct physiological consequence of a general medical condition, substance intoxication or withdrawal, or multiple etiologies.

The causes of delirium are best remembered using the helpful mnemonic "I WATCH DEATH", which categorizes the vast majority of underlying factors:

  • Infection (e.g., Urinary Tract Infection, Pneumonia, Sepsis, COVID-19)

  • Withdrawal (e.g., from alcohol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates)

  • Acute Metabolic (e.g., Electrolyte imbalances, Acid/Base disorders, Liver or Kidney failure)

  • Trauma (e.g., Head Trauma, Post-surgical state, Hypothermia/Heatstroke)

  • CNS Pathology (e.g., Stroke, Seizure, Brain tumor, Hemorrhage)

  • Hypoxia (e.g., from heart failure, pulmonary embolism, severe anemia)

  • Deficiencies (e.g., Vitamin B12, Thiamine - B1, Folate)

  • Endocrinopathies (e.g., Thyroid disorders, Glucose abnormalities - both high and low)

  • Acute Vascular (e.g., Hypertensive emergency, Shock)

  • Toxins/Drugs (e.g., Side effects of medications like opioids, anticholinergics, steroids)

  • Heavy Metals (e.g., Lead, Mercury poisoning)

Analysis of the Options:

  • Option 1: Head Trauma – This is a direct cause. Trauma can lead to intracranial bleeding, swelling, and direct brain injury, precipitating delirium.

  • Option 2: Infection – This is one of the most common causes, especially in older adults. Systemic infections cause inflammation and can disrupt brain function.

  • Option 3: Metabolic Disorder – Conditions like hyponatremia (low sodium), hypercalcemia (high calcium), or diabetic ketoacidosis disrupt the biochemical environment of the brain, leading to delirium.

Question 31 of 50
Which of the following is NOT true regarding Brief Cognitive Therapy?
A. It puts greater emphasis on the cause of the problem ✓
B. It uses here and now approach
C. It uses goal-oriented approach
D. It uses time limited approach
💡
Explanation

Brief Cognitive Therapy (BCT) is a short-term, structured therapeutic approach that focuses on addressing current psychological issues by modifying dysfunctional thoughts and behaviors.

Key Features

Goal-Oriented:

BCT is focused on setting clear, achievable goals. The therapist and patient work together to identify specific problems and develop strategies to address them.

Time-Limited:

Typically involves a limited number of sessions (6-20), making it a short-term treatment compared to other forms of therapy.

Here-and-Now Approach:

Focuses on the present rather than delving into past experiences. The therapy aims to solve current issues, such as anxiety, depression, or stress, through practical, present-day strategies.

Cognitive Restructuring:

BCT helps patients identify and challenge negative thought patterns (cognitive distortions) that contribute to emotional distress. The goal is to replace these thoughts with more realistic and positive ones.

Behavioral Techniques:

Includes practical techniques like problem-solving, relaxation training, and stress management to help patients manage their symptoms in real time.

Active Participation:

The patient is actively involved in the therapy process, often completing tasks or assignments between sessions, reinforcing the cognitive-behavioral approach.

Focus on the Present:

Emphasizes current symptoms and problems rather than focusing on the underlying causes, which distinguishes it from psychodynamic therapies.

Indications:

Depression: For patients with mild to moderate depression, focusing on current negative thought patterns.

Anxiety Disorders: Addressing anxious thoughts and behaviors that impact daily functioning.

Stress Management: Helping patients cope with chronic stress or life changes.

Adjustment Disorders: For individuals struggling with life transitions or personal challenges.

Nursing Responsibilities:

Patient Education:

Explain the goals of therapy, the time-limited nature, and the focus on present issues. Help patients understand the value of active participation.

Monitoring Progress:

Regularly assess the patient's progress toward their goals and offer feedback. Track symptom reduction and behavioral changes.

Supporting the Therapeutic Process:

Encourage patients to practice techniques learned in therapy, such as cognitive restructuring and relaxation exercises, outside of sessions.

Promote Self-Efficacy:

Help patients gain confidence in using the tools provided in BCT to manage their symptoms and make improvements independently.

Benefits:

Short-Term: Effective in a relatively short period, leading to quicker improvements.

Goal-Focused: Helps patients achieve specific outcomes, leading to increased satisfaction.

Flexible: Can be adapted for use with a wide range of mental health disorders

Brief Cognitive Therapy (BCT) was developed by Aaron T. Beck, a psychiatrist and psychotherapist. Beck is also known for developing Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), and BCT is essentially a time-limited, focused version of CBT.

While CBT is a comprehensive and long-term therapy model, BCT is a more short-term and goal-oriented approach aimed at resolving immediate psychological issues by addressing dysfunctional thought patterns and behaviors. Beck's work laid the foundation for both therapeutic approaches.
