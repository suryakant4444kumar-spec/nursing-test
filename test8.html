Question 1 of 50
Cell most sensitive to hypoxia is?
A. Neuron ✓
B. Liver cell
C. Stem cell
D. Muscle
💡
Explanation

Neurons have very high metabolic demand and minimal ability to store glycogen, so they depend constantly on oxygen and glucose.Therefore, they are the first cells to get damaged in hypoxia (usually within 3–5 minutes).

Question 2 of 50
In a neuron, the cell processes that receive information and carry it to the cell body are called.
A. Dendrites ✓
B. Axons
C. Tracts
D. Projections
💡
Explanation

Dendrites are the branched extensions of a neuron that receive electrical signals from other neurons and transmit this information to the cell body. They are crucial for sensory input and communication between neurons.

  • Axons: These are long, slender projections that carry nerve impulses away from the cell body to other neurons or muscles.

  • Tracts: Bundles of axons in the central nervous system (CNS).

  • Projections: A general term for extensions from the cell body but not specifically for receiving information.

Question 3 of 50
The gaps between the segments of oligodendrocytes or between individual Schwann cells are called
A. Nodes of Ranvier ✓
B. Synapse
C. Synaptic cleft
D. Sulci
💡
Explanation

The Nodes of Ranvier are small gaps in the myelin sheath that covers nerve fibers, and they occur between the segments of oligodendrocytes (in the central nervous system) or between individual Schwann cells (in the peripheral nervous system). These nodes are crucial for the proper conduction of electrical impulses along the nerve fibers.

  • The myelin sheath is formed by oligodendrocytes (in the CNS) or Schwann cells (in the PNS), and it insulates the nerve fibers, speeding up the transmission of electrical signals.

  • The Nodes of Ranvier are essential for a process called saltatory conduction, where electrical impulses "jump" from one node to the next, greatly increasing the speed of nerve signal transmission.

Question 4 of 50
The white matter of the nervous system is formed by the
A. Nerve cell bodies and their dendrites
B. Nerve tracts ✓
C. Nuclei
D. Sulcile
💡
Explanation

white matter of the nervous system is primarily composed of myelinated nerve fibers, which form nerve tracts. These tracts are responsible for transmitting electrical signals across different parts of the central nervous system. The white appearance comes from the myelin sheath that insulates the nerve fibers, enhancing signal transmission speed.

  • Option 1: Nerve cell bodies and dendrites are part of gray matter, not white matter.

  • Option 3: Nuclei are clusters of neuron cell bodies located in the central nervous system and are also part of the gray matter.

  • Option 4: Sulci are grooves or indentations in the brain, not related to the composition of white matter.

Question 5 of 50
Identify the correct statement regarding neurons:
I. Synaptic vesicles contains neurotransmitters
II. Dendrites typically carry signals toward the cell body
III. Schwann cells forms myelin sheath
IV. Nodes of Ranvier are found in dendrite
A. I, II, III, IV
B. I, III, IV only
C. I, II, IV only
D. I, II, III only ✓
💡
Explanation

Statement I: Synaptic vesicles contain neurotransmitters — True. Synaptic vesicles are small sacs located in the axon terminals of neurons. They store neurotransmitters, which are released into the synaptic cleft to transmit signals between neurons.
Statement II: Dendrites typically carry signals toward the cell body — True. Dendrites are the branches of a neuron that receive signals from other neurons and transmit those signals toward the cell body.
Statement III: Schwann cells form myelin sheath — True. Schwann cells are responsible for forming the myelin sheath around the axons of peripheral neurons, which helps to speed up the transmission of electrical impulses along the axon.
Statement IV: Nodes of Ranvier are found in dendrites — False. The Nodes of Ranvier are gaps in the myelin sheath along the axon of a neuron, not in the dendrites. These nodes facilitate the rapid conduction of action potentials along the axon.

Question 10 of 50
The number of pairs of cranial nerves are:
A. 14
B. 16
C. 10
D. 12 ✓
💡
Explanation

  • There are exactly 12 pairs of cranial nerves in the human body.​

    • These nerves originate directly from the brain (specifically the cerebrum and brainstem) rather than the spinal cord.

    • They are numbered with Roman numerals I through XII based on their location from the front (anterior) to the back (posterior) of the brain.​

    • Each pair controls specific functions like smell, sight, eye movement, facial expression, and swallowing.

  • Option 1: 14 (Incorrect)
    This number is incorrect for humans. (Note: Some sources historically debated a "Terminal Nerve" or Nerve 0, but in standard clinical and nursing exams, the answer is always 12).

  • Option 2: 16 (Incorrect)
    There is no anatomical basis for 16 cranial nerves.

  • Option 3: 10 (Incorrect)
    While the Vagus nerve is cranial nerve number 10 (X), the total count is 12. The first 10 originate from the brainstem, but nerves I (Olfactory) and II (Optic) originate from the cerebrum/forebrain.

Question 25 of 50
Vasoconstriction in skin is due to
A. Sympathetic ✓
B. Parasympathetic
C. Wheal and flare
D. Warm climate
💡
Explanation
Vasoconstriction in the skin is primarily mediated by the sympathetic nervous system. The sympathetic nervous system causes the blood vessels in the skin to constrict (narrow), which reduces blood flow and helps conserve body heat. This process is part of the body's response to cold temperatures or other conditions requiring the maintenance of core temperature. In contrast, the parasympathetic system is associated with vasodilation (widening of blood vessels). The "wheal and flare" response refers to a skin reaction involving swelling and redness, which is not related to vasoconstriction.

Question 34 of 50
Monro-Kellies hypothesis is about
A. Equilibrium of cranial content ✓
B. Affected mental function
C. Increasing intracranial pressure
D. Unresponsive to environment
💡
Explanation
The Monro-Kellie hypothesis states that the cranial vault is a rigid space, and it contains three components: brain tissue, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). The total volume of these components remains constant under normal conditions. If the volume of one component increases (such as due to swelling of the brain or bleeding), it must be compensated for by a decrease in the volume of the other components to maintain equilibrium and prevent an increase in intracranial pressure (ICP). If this compensation is insufficient, it can lead to increased ICP, which can be dangerous and affect brain function.
