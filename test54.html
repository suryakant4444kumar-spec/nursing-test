Question 1 of 50
Which of the following dietary nutrient provide to the patient with functional hypoglycaemia?
A. Unsaturated fats
B. Complete proteins
C. Complex carbohydrates ✓
D. Simple sugars
💡
Explanation
For a patient with functional hypoglycemia, complex carbohydrates are recommended because they are digested more slowly and help to provide a steady release of glucose into the bloodstream, helping to stabilize blood sugar levels. Simple sugars can cause rapid spikes and drops in blood sugar, which can worsen hypoglycemia.
Question 2 of 50

A. Peer control of blood glucose level
B. Advancing age ✓
C. Obesity
D. Physical inactivity
💡
Explanation

Advancing age is a risk factor that cannot be changed, while the others (peer control of blood glucose, obesity, and physical inactivity) are modifiable through lifestyle changes and interventions.

The bicarbonate-carbonic acid buffer system helps maintain the pH of the body fluids; in metabolic acidosis, there is a decrease in bicarbonate because of an increase of metabolicacids. At physiological pH, these 2 keto acids dissociate completely, and the excess hydrogen ions bind the bicarbonate, resulting in decreased serum bicarbonate levels. Option A: Due to carbon dioxide forming carbonic acid in the body when combining with water, the amount of carbon dioxide expired can cause pH to increase or decrease. Question 17 of 50
Insulin forces which of the following electrolytes out of the plasma and into the cells?
A. Calcium
B. Magnesium
C. Phosphorus
D. Potassium ✓
💡
Explanation
Insulin forces potassium out of the plasma, back into the cells, causing Hypokalemia. Potassium is needed to help transport glucose and insulin into the cells. Insulin shifts potassium into cells by stimulating the activity of Na+-H+ antiporter on cell membrane, promoting the entry of sodium into cells, which leads to activation of the Na+-K+ ATPase, causing an electro genic influx of potassium. IV insulin leads to a dose-dependent decline in serum potassium levels Option A: When levels of blood sugar, or glucose, rise, it is efficiently taken up by betacells. Within the cells, glucose initiates a chain of molecular events that lead to calcium channel opening, allowing more calcium ions to flow into the beta-cells. Calcium ions stimulate insulin release to the blood. Option B: Magnesium has an important role in insulin action, and insulin stimulates magnesium uptake in insulin-sensitive tissues. Impaired biological responses to insulin are referred to as insulin resistance. Option C: A rise in insulin causes phosphate to move to the intracellular compartment. Additionally, the increased anabolism leads to the formation of high-energy phosphate bonds, further depleting phosphate levels

Question 34 of 50
What is the primary characteristic of Hyperglycaemic Hyperosmolar Non-Ketotic Syndrome (HHNS)?
A. High blood glucose and ketonemia
B. Severe dehydration and hyperglycaemia without ketosis ✓
C. Hypoglycaemia with acidosis
D. High blood pressure and low blood glucose
💡
Explanation

Hyperglycaemic Hyperosmolar Non-Ketotic Syndrome (HHNS), also called Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS), is a serious complication mainly seen in Type-2 Diabetes Mellitus. It is characterized by extremely high blood glucose levels (often >600 mg/dL) leading to severe dehydration and increased plasma osmolarity. Unlike diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), ketone production is minimal or absent, so ketosis and acidosis are usually not present. Patients often show altered consciousness, weakness, and profound dehydration, making early recognition and treatment with IV fluids and insulin essential.

Question 35 of 50
The type of diet a nurse should suggest for a diabetic patient would be:
A. Low carbohydrate diet ✓
B. Low vitamin diet
C. High carbohydrate diet
D. High fat diet
💡
Explanation

For a diabetic patient, managing blood sugar levels is critical, and diet plays a central role in this. The primary focus for a diabetic diet is to regulate carbohydrate intake, as carbohydrates directly affect blood glucose levels.

  • Low carbohydrate diet: Reducing the intake of carbohydrates, particularly simple sugars and refined carbs, helps control blood sugar levels. Complex carbohydrates, like whole grains, are often recommended because they release glucose more slowly into the bloodstream. This type of diet helps to prevent blood sugar spikes and maintain more stable glucose levels.

  • Low vitamin diet: A low vitamin diet is not recommended for diabetic patients. In fact, a balanced diet rich in vitamins and minerals is crucial for overall health and managing diabetes. Deficiencies in vitamins like vitamin D, B12, and others can negatively affect diabetes control and overall well-being.

  • High carbohydrate diet: A high carbohydrate diet can lead to blood sugar spikes, making it harder to control diabetes. Excess carbohydrates in the diet can increase the body's need for insulin and lead to poor glucose control.

  • High fat diet: While fats are an essential part of the diet, especially healthy fats (like those found in avocados, nuts, and olive oil), a high fat diet should be approached with caution. High-fat diets can lead to weight gain, which may worsen insulin resistance in diabetic patients.

  • Option 1 (Adrenal adenoma): Less common cause of ACTH-independent Cushing's (~10%).

  • Option 4 (Adrenal carcinoma): Rare cause (<5%).
