Question 1 of 50
Acquired aplastic anaemia usually manifests with all except?
A. Purpura
B. Oral ulcers
C. Hepatosplenomegaly ✓
D. Fatigue
💡
Explanation

  • Acquired aplastic anaemia is a bone marrow failure disorder characterized by pancytopenia and hypocellular marrow.

  • Purpura – Common due to thrombocytopenia (↓ platelets).

  • Oral ulcers – Common due to neutropenia (↓ white cells leading to infections).

  • Fatigue – Common due to anaemia (↓ red cells).

  • Hepatosplenomegaly – Absent in acquired aplastic anaemia; its presence suggests another diagnosis such as leukemia or hypersplenism.

Hence, hepatosplenomegaly is not a feature of acquired aplastic anaemia.

Question 2 of 50
Which of the following statement is NOT correct about immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)?
A. It is an autoimmune disorder
B. Immune mediated destruction of platelets occurs
C. Anti-platelet antibodies will not cross the placenta in a pregnant woman ✓
D. Spontaneous resolution of Acute form of ITP within two months
💡
Explanation

  • Autoimmune disorder → True
    ITP is caused by the body's immune system attacking its own platelets.

  • Immune-mediated destruction of platelets → True
    Autoantibodies (mostly IgG) bind to platelets, leading to their destruction in the spleen.

  • Spontaneous resolution of acute ITP (especially in children) → True
    Most cases of acute ITP in children resolve within 2 months, often after a viral infection.

  • Anti-platelet antibodies can cross the placenta → False
    These are IgG antibodies, which do cross the placenta, and may cause thrombocytopenia in the newborn.

Question 12 of 50
Cause of iron deficiency anemia in CRF is ...
A. Iron absorption is not taking place
B. Unable to convert iron to transferrin form
C. Decreased erythropoietin secretion ✓
D. Increased RBC destruction
💡
Explanation
In Chronic Renal Failure (CRF), iron deficiency anemia is primarily due to decreased erythropoietin secretion. Erythropoietin, a hormone produced by the kidneys, stimulates the production of red blood cells in the bone marrow. In CRF, the kidneys' ability to produce erythropoietin is impaired, leading to reduced red blood cell production, which contributes to anemia. Although iron absorption and other factors may also play a role, the decreased erythropoietin secretion is a key cause of anemia in CRF.

Question 19 of 50
Treat sickle cell anemia?
A. Folic acid 5mg/day ✓
B. Iron 10mg/day
C. Vitamin. B12 25mg/day
D. Folic acid 10 mg /day
💡
Explanation

Sickle cell anemia causes increased red blood cell turnover and hemolysis, leading to higher demand for folic acid to support new red blood cell production.

Folic acid supplementation (5 mg/day) is commonly prescribed to prevent folate deficiency and support erythropoiesis.

Iron supplementation is generally avoided unless there is documented iron deficiency because iron overload can be harmful.

Vitamin B12 is not routinely used unless a specific deficiency exists.

The difference between 5 mg and 10 mg folic acid is not significant clinically; 5 mg/day is standard.

Question 20 of 50
Correct about iron deficiency anemia is ......
A. Reduced hemoglobin and packed cell volume
B. Reduced mean corpuscular volume
C. Microcytic hypochromic cells on peripheral smear
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

Iron deficiency anemia is a common type of anemia that occurs when the body does not have enough iron to produce hemoglobin, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. The following are characteristics of iron deficiency anemia:

  • Reduced hemoglobin and packed cell volume (Option 1): In iron deficiency anemia, both hemoglobin levels and packed cell volume (hematocrit) are reduced due to the insufficient production of hemoglobin.

  • Reduced mean corpuscular volume (MCV) (Option 2): MCV is the average volume of a red blood cell. In iron deficiency anemia, the MCV is decreased, indicating that the red blood cells are smaller than normal (microcytic).

  • Microcytic, hypochromic cells on peripheral smear (Option 3): On a peripheral blood smear, red blood cells in iron deficiency anemia appear smaller (microcytic) and paler (hypochromic) due to reduced hemoglobin content.

A. a-chain

💡
Explanation

The alpha (a) chains and gamma chains are not primarily affected in sickle cell anemia.

'Sequestration crisis' is related to:'
A. Pernicious anemia
B. Sickle cell anemia ✓
C. Aplastic anemia
D. Iron deficiency anemia
💡
Explanation

The term "Sequestration crisis" is related to Sickle cell anemia.

Specifically, a sequestration crisis (often called splenic sequestration crisis) is a serious complication of sickle cell anemia where sickled red blood cells get trapped in the spleen. This causes the spleen to rapidly enlarge and traps a large volume of blood, leading to a sudden and dangerous drop in circulating blood volume and severe anemia. It mostly occurs in young children with sickle cell disease and can be life-threatening if not treated promptly.​

Question 34 of 50
Hemolytic anemias are characterized by
A. Shortened red cell survival
B. Bone marrow shows erythroid hyperplasia
C. Reticulocyte count and unconjugated bilirubin level will be elevated
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
  • Shortened red cell survival – Red blood cells are destroyed prematurely in hemolytic anemia, leading to a reduced lifespan of the cells.
  • Bone marrow shows erythroid hyperplasia – The bone marrow compensates for the increased destruction of red blood cells by producing more, leading to an increase in erythroid (red blood cell) precursors.
  • Elevated reticulocyte count and unconjugated bilirubin level – Reticulocyte count is elevated as the bone marrow attempts to produce more red blood cells. Unconjugated bilirubin levels rise due to the breakdown of hemoglobin from the destroyed red blood cells.

Explanation

Hemophilia is a genetic disorder that affects the blood's ability to clot properly, leading to excessive bleeding. It is primarily associated with mutations in genes on the X chromosome.

Since males have one X and one Y chromosome (XY), they are more commonly affected by hemophilia if they inherit the defective gene. Females have two X chromosomes (XX), so they are usually carriers unless both X chromosomes carry the defective gene.
Therefore, hemophilia is associated with the X chromosome.

Cell with a horse-shoe shaped nucleus and abundant cytoplasm in a peripheral blood smear is
A. Neutrophil
B. Monocyte ✓
C. Lymphocyte
D. Eosinophil
💡
Explanation

A monocyte is a type of white blood cell that has a distinctive horseshoe-shaped nucleus and abundant blue-gray cytoplasm. In peripheral blood smears, monocytes are the largest type of white blood cell. They are part of the body's immune system and can differentiate into macrophages or dendritic cells once they migrate into tissues.

  • Option 1 (Neutrophil): Neutrophils are smaller and have a multi-lobed nucleus (not horseshoe-shaped).
  • Option 3 (Lymphocyte): Lymphocytes have a large, round nucleus with very little cytoplasm.
  • Option 4 (Eosinophil): Eosinophils have a bilobed nucleus and reddish-orange granules in the cytoplasm, which distinguishes them from monocytes.

Thus, the monocyte is the correct answer, as it has the characteristic horseshoe-shaped nucleus and abundant cytoplasm.
