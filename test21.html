Gastroschisis is a congenital condition characterized by the following:

Question 8 of 50
The repair of cleft palate should be preferred around one of the following timings after birth of the baby -
A. Around one year ✓
B. Around six months
C. Around two years
D. Around four years
💡
Explanation

  • The cleft palate repair (palatoplasty) is usually performed around 9–12 months of age.

  • At this stage, the baby’s growth and health are sufficient to tolerate surgery safely.

  • Performing the surgery before speech development helps the child develop normal speech patterns and prevents nasal regurgitation.

  • Early repair also assists in proper feeding and reduces ear infections associated with Eustachian tube dysfunction.

  • If done too early (before 6 months), tissues may be too delicate and increase the risk of dehiscence.

  • If delayed (beyond 2 years), speech and maxillary growth may be adversely affected.

Question 11 of 50
What is the appropriate feeding technique for the nurse to use with an infant who has the cleft palate?
A. Suction client before feeding
B. Feeding in sitting position ✓
C. Have the nurse feed the client during hospitalization
D. Bubble the infant after feed to reduce risk of respiration
💡
Explanation
The correct option is Option 2: Feeding in sitting position.
Explanation
  • Feeding in a sitting position (upright or semi-upright, 45-90 degrees) is a primary technique for infants with a cleft palate because it uses gravity to help direct milk flow into the stomach and away from the nasal cavity, which is connected due to the cleft. This significantly reduces the risk of milk entering the nose (nasal regurgitation) and the potential for aspiration (milk entering the airway).
  • Why other options are incorrect--
  • Option 1: Suction client before feeding is not a routine feeding technique. Suctioning is used when an infant is having difficulty breathing or managing secretions, not as a standard preparation for every feed.
  • Option 3: Have the nurse feed the client during hospitalization is incorrect because the goal is to teach parents and caregivers proper feeding techniques so they can safely and effectively feed their infant at home. The nurse's role is education and support, not the sole provider of all feedings during the entire hospitalization.
  • Option 4: Bubble the infant after feeding to reduce risk of respiration is incorrect. While infants with cleft palate do swallow more air during feeding and require frequent burping (bubbling) to reduce stomach distension and regurgitation, doing so after the entire feed is not sufficient. They need to be burped frequently during the feeding (e.g., every half to one ounce of milk). Also, burping is primarily to manage swallowed air and abdominal discomfort, while the upright position is the key technique to reduce aspiration/respiration risk during the actual feeding.
Question 12 of 50
When feeding an 18 month old child two days after he underwent a cleft palate repair, a nurse would offer liquid nutrition using a:-
A. Plastic cup ✓
B. Silastic nasogastric
C. Flexible straw
D. Rubber coated infant spoon
💡
Explanation
The correct option is Option 1: Plastic cup.
Explanation
  • After a cleft palate repair, the primary goal for feeding is to avoid any sucking, pressure, or trauma to the newly repaired surgical site to ensure proper healing.
  • Plastic cup: Using an open plastic cup (or a special cleft care sippy cup with no-spill valve removed) is a recommended method because it allows the child to drink without creating negative pressure through sucking, thereby protecting the surgical site.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Silastic nasogastric: This is an invasive method generally reserved for cases where oral feeding is not possible due to complications or inability to take in sufficient nutrition; it is not the standard primary method for a child who can feed orally after surgery.
  • Flexible straw: The act of sucking through a straw creates negative pressure in the mouth, which can seriously compromise the palate repair and should be strictly avoided.
  • Rubber coated infant spoon: While a rubber-coated spoon might be used for feeding soft or pureed foods in a controlled manner, using a cup is often preferred for liquids to avoid placing any potential pressure or trauma directly inside the mouth near the delicate surgical site.

Question 41 of 50
Which medication is commonly used in severe GERD for acid suppression?
A. Omeprazole ✓
B. Ranitidine
C. Metoclopramide
D. Domperidone
💡
Explanation

Omeprazole belongs to a class of drugs called proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), which are the most powerful and effective medications available for treating severe GERD and promoting the healing of associated conditions like erosive esophagitis. PPIs work by irreversibly blocking the H+/K+ ATPase enzyme system (the "acid pump") in the stomach's parietal cells, thereby significantly reducing gastric acid secretion. This potent acid suppression allows damaged esophageal tissue to heal and provides better symptom control compared to other medications.

Why other options are incorrect

  • Option 2: Ranitidine is a histamine H2-receptor antagonist (H2 blocker). H2 blockers are effective for mild to moderate GERD symptoms but are less potent than PPIs for severe cases and are associated with tachyphylaxis (decreased effectiveness over time). Ranitidine products were also withdrawn from the market in many places due to concerns over a contaminant (NDMA), although new formulations may be available in some regions.
  • Option 3: Metoclopramide and Option 4: Domperidone are prokinetic agents, which improve gastrointestinal motility and increase the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) pressure. They do not primarily suppress acid production. They are generally less effective than PPIs for severe GERD and their long-term use is limited by potential serious side effects, including neurological and cardiac complications, respectively. They are typically used as add-on therapy for specific cases (e.g., delayed gastric emptying) rather than as the primary medication for severe acid suppression in GERD.

What is a potential complication of untreated GERD in children?
A. Esophagitis
B. Barrett’s esophagus
C. Aspiration pneumonia
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

Untreated Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) in children can lead to several complications, including:

  • Option 1: Esophagitis: Chronic acid reflux can cause inflammation of the esophagus, known as esophagitis. This occurs when stomach acid damages the lining of the esophagus, leading to discomfort, ulcers, or bleeding.

  • Option 2: Barrett’s esophagus: Over time, untreated GERD can lead to Barrett’s esophagus, a condition where the cells lining the esophagus change, potentially increasing the risk of esophageal cancer. While it is more common in adults, untreated severe GERD in children can also lead to this complication.

  • Option 3: Aspiration pneumonia: In severe cases of GERD, stomach contents can be aspirated (breathed into the lungs), leading to aspiration pneumonia, which is an infection that results from inhaling food, liquid, or acid into the airways and lungs.
