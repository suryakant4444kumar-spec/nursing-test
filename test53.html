Question 1 of 50
All of the following are features of diabetes insipidus include all the following EXCEPT
A. Low specific gravity of urine
B. Increased serum sodium
C. Increased osmolality
D. Hyperkalemia ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Diabetes insipidus (DI) is caused by deficiency of ADH (central DI) or renal unresponsiveness to ADH (nephrogenic DI), leading to inability to concentrate urine.

  • Typical features:

    • Low urine specific gravity (dilute urine)

    • Hypernatremia (increased serum sodium) due to water loss

    • High serum osmolality

  • Hyperkalemia is not a feature — potassium levels are usually normal, and if altered, hypokalemia is more likely in certain cases (especially in nephrogenic DI related to electrolyte imbalances).

Question 2 of 50
Thyroid-binding globulin increases in
A. Diabetes mellitus
B. Pregnancy ✓
C. Grave's disease
D. Nephrotic syndrome
💡
Explanation

Thyroid-binding globulin (TBG) levels are known to increase during pregnancy. This is primarily due to increased estrogen levels, which stimulate the liver to produce more TBG. This binding protein carries thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) in the bloodstream. In pregnancy, higher levels of estrogen lead to increased production of TBG, which results in higher total thyroid hormone levels, even though free hormone levels may remain normal.

  • Diabetes mellitus typically does not cause an increase in TBG levels.

  • Grave's disease is associated with hyperthyroidism, but it does not specifically cause an increase in TBG.

  • Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by protein loss through the kidneys, which can reduce TBG levels due to proteinuria.

Question 3 of 50
Excessive secretion of growth hormone leads to ...
A. Gigantism before the closure of epiphyseal plate
B. Acromegaly after the closure of epiphyseal plate
C. Both a and b ✓
D. Cretinism
💡
Explanation

Excessive secretion of growth hormone (GH) leads to different conditions depending on the timing of the secretion relative to the closure of the epiphyseal plate (growth plate):

  • Gigantism occurs before the closure of the epiphyseal plate, causing abnormal growth in height as the long bones continue to grow.

  • Acromegaly occurs after the closure of the epiphyseal plate, resulting in abnormal growth of bones in the hands, feet, and face, but no increase in height.

Cretinism is a condition related to hypothyroidism (not growth hormone), leading to stunted growth and mental retardation, and is unrelated to excessive GH secretion.

Question 4 of 50
Gigantism is seen in ..
A. Hyperpituitarism ✓
B. Hypopituitarism
C. Hyperthyroidism
D. Hypothyroidism
💡
Explanation
Gigantism is a condition caused by excessive growth hormone (GH) production, typically due to a pituitary adenoma (a benign tumor of the pituitary gland) leading to hyperpituitarism. In children and adolescents, before the growth plates of the bones have closed, this excess growth hormone leads to abnormal and excessive growth, resulting in gigantism.

Short stature, secondary to growth hormone deficiency is associated with
A. Height age is equal to Bone age

Explanation

They may be short in stature, but their limbs and trunk are proportionate (i.e., not disproportionate as seen in conditions like achondroplasia or other forms of dwarfism). The short stature is primarily due to reduced growth, but the body parts themselves remain proportional.
Question 6 of 50
Clinical manifestation of SIADHincludes ................
A. Increased urine Na+
B. Decreased serum Na+ ✓
C. Decreased urine osmolality
D. All of the above
💡
Explanation

SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone secretion) causes excess ADH → water retention → dilutional hyponatremia.

Option-wise analysis:

  • Option 1: Increased urine Na⁺

    • Can be seen, but it is not the single most consistent clinical manifestation tested in exams.

  • Option 2: Decreased serum Na⁺ ✅

    • Hallmark feature of SIADH

    • Due to dilution of sodium from excess water retention.

  • Option 3: Decreased urine osmolality

    • Incorrect — in SIADH, urine osmolality is increased (concentrated urine).

  • Option 4: All of the above

    • Incorrect because Option 3 is wrong.

💡
Explanation

  • Grave's disease – Caused by overproduction of thyroxine (hyperthyroidism), not underproduction.

  • Cushing's disease – Caused by excess cortisol, due to overproduction of ACTH, unrelated to thyroid hormones.

Question 23 of 50
Electrolyte imbalance seen in hyperthyroidism is ........
A. Hypocalcemia
B. Hypercalcemia ✓
C. Hypernatremia
D. Hyperkalemia
💡
Explanation

  • In hyperthyroidism, there is excess secretion of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4), which increases metabolic activity.

  • These hormones stimulate bone resorption (breakdown of bone).

  • As a result, calcium is released from bones into the bloodstream, leading to hypercalcemia.

  • Although mild, this increase in calcium is a characteristic biochemical finding.

  • Other features include weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, and nervousness.

Question 24 of 50
Electrolyte imbalance seen in hypoparathyroidism is ........
A. Hypocalcemia ✓
B. Hypercalcemia
C. Hypernatremia
D. Hyperkalemia
💡
Explanation

  • Hypoparathyroidism is a condition in which there is decreased secretion of parathyroid hormone (PTH).

  • PTH plays a key role in maintaining calcium levels in the blood by:

    • Increasing calcium release from bones

    • Increasing calcium reabsorption from kidneys

    • Enhancing calcium absorption from intestines (via vitamin D activation)

  • When PTH is deficient, these mechanisms fail, leading to low serum calcium levels (hypocalcemia).

  • Clinical features of hypocalcemia include tetany, muscle cramps, paresthesia, and positive Chvostek’s and Trousseau’s signs.

Question 25 of 50

The causes of hypoparathyroidism include:
(A) Damage of glands during thyroidectomy
(B) Congenital anomaly of the gland

(C) Autoimmune destruction of the parathyroid glands

(D) Heavy metal toxicity

A. A, B
B. A, C
C. A, B, D
D. A, B, C, D ✓
💡
Explanation

Hypoparathyroidism can result from several causes:

  • (A) Damage of glands during thyroidectomy – Surgical removal or accidental damage to the parathyroid glands can cause hypoparathyroidism.
  • (B) Congenital anomaly of the gland – Some individuals are born with underdeveloped or absent parathyroid glands (e.g., DiGeorge syndrome).
  • (C) Autoimmune destruction of the parathyroid glands – Autoimmune conditions can lead to the destruction of parathyroid tissues, reducing hormone secretion.
  • (D) Heavy metal toxicity – Exposure to heavy metals like iron and copper can impair parathyroid gland function.
Question 26 of 50
Which of the following hormone antagonize the action of parathyroid hormone?
A. Insulin
B. Glucagon
C. Calcitonin ✓
D. Triiodothyronine
💡
Explanation
Calcitonin is a hormone that antagonizes the action of parathyroid hormone (PTH). It is produced by the thyroid gland and works to lower blood calcium levels. While parathyroid hormone (PTH) increases blood calcium levels by promoting calcium release from bones, increasing calcium reabsorption in the kidneys, and enhancing calcium absorption in the intestines, calcitonin does the opposite. It helps decrease blood calcium levels by inhibiting osteoclast activity in the bones (thus reducing bone resorption), and increasing calcium excretion through the kidneys.
Question 27 of 50
Which among the following intervention is most appropriate in a client who is experiencing hemorrhage following thyroidectomy?
A. Intubate the client
B. Prepare for tracheostomy
C. Lateral position ✓
D. Start IV fluids
💡
Explanation
In a client who is experiencing hemorrhage following a thyroidectomy, it is important to position them in a way that helps prevent airway obstruction and aspiration of blood. The lateral position (lying on the side) is most appropriate as it allows any blood that may be present in the mouth or throat to drain out and helps keep the airway clear.
Question 28 of 50
After thyroidectomy accidental removal of parathyroid gland need to suspect if ..................
A. Easily fracture following a minor fall injury
B. Renal calculi occurred
C. Hypertension and difficulty swallowing
D. Laryngospasms and tingling in the hand and feet. ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Accidental removal or damage to the parathyroid glands causes acute hypocalcemia because parathyroid hormone (PTH) drops suddenly.

  • Hypocalcemia symptoms:

    • Tingling / numbness in hands, feet, and around the mouth

    • Muscle cramps

    • Tetany

    • Laryngospasm (dangerous — airway can close)

    • Positive Chvostek and Trousseau signs

Other options:

  • Fractures → long-term calcium or vitamin D deficiency, not acute post-thyroidectomy.

  • Renal calculi → hypercalcemia (opposite condition).

  • Hypertension & dysphagia → not specific to parathyroid injury.
