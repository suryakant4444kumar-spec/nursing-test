Question 1 of 50
Teaching hospital bed capacity is?
A. More than 300
B. More than 100
C. More than 500 ✓
D. Less than 400
💡
Explanation
While teaching hospitals can vary in size, many larger teaching hospitals indeed have more than 500 beds due to their extensive roles in both patient care and medical education. However, not all teaching hospitals have such a high capacity; some might have more than 300 beds but fewer than 500.
Question 2 of 50
A hospital which have Bed capacity more than 300 is known as:
A. Teaching hospital
B. Large hospital ✓
C. Small hospital
D. Medium hospital
💡
Explanation
Large hospital: This designation is used for hospitals with a significant number of beds, generally exceeding 300.
Question 3 of 50
Hospice is the treatment concept for
A. Cardiac Pt.
B. Psychiatric Pt.
C. Terminally ill Pt. ✓
D. All
💡
Explanation
Hospice care is a treatment concept focused on providing comfort and support to patients who are terminally ill, with a prognosis of six months or less to live. The goal is to improve the quality of life for patients and their families by addressing physical, emotional, and spiritual needs rather than curative treatment.
Question 4 of 50
The process of assessing Pt. who come to emergency department & determining the management priorities is called:
A. Mirage
B. Collage
C. Triage ✓
D. Stage
💡
Explanation

The process of assessing patients who come to the emergency department and determining the management priorities is known as triage. This process involves evaluating the severity of patients' conditions to prioritize treatment based on the urgency of their needs.

Question 6 of 50

The most common site of hospital acquired infection is?

A. Surgical sites
B. Respiratory tract
C. Urinary tract ✓
D. Eyes
💡
Explanation
Central venous catheters are considered the primary source of hospital-acquired bloodstream infections. The other sources of bloodstream infections are catheter-associated urinary tract infections and ventilator-associated Pneumonia.
Question 7 of 50
The most common route of transmission for nosocomial (hospital-acquired) infections is:
A. Droplet transmission
B. Direct contact ✓
C. Indirect contact
D. Vehicle transmission
💡
Explanation
Nosocomial infections (hospital-acquired infections) are most commonly transmitted through direct contact between healthcare workers and patients, including contaminated hands, equipment, and surfaces.
Question 8 of 50
In the ventilator bundle, which step specifically reduces aspiration risk?
A. Oral care with chlorhexidine
B. Elevating head of bed 30–45° ✓
C. Daily sedation interruption
D. Avoiding nasogastric tube feeding
💡
Explanation

Elevation prevents gastric reflux and aspiration into the lungs. Oral chlorhexidine reduces bacterial load but not reflux. Sedation breaks help assess extubation readiness, not aspiration directly. Avoiding NG feeding is not a standard VAP bundle measure.
Question 9 of 50
The optimal timing for surgical prophylactic antibiotic administration is:
A. 15 minutes before incision
B. 30–60 minutes before incision ✓
C. At skin incision time
D. 2 hours before incision
💡
Explanation

This timing ensures peak tissue levels during incision. Giving too early or at the time of incision reduces effectiveness. Vancomycin/fluoroquinolones require ~120 minutes pre-incision.

C. Diabetes
D. Break in aseptic insertion

Question 12 of 50
Which VAP diagnosis tool is most specific?
A. Chest X-ray infiltrates
B. Quantitative culture from BAL ✓
C. Sputum culture from ET tube
D. Clinical Pulmonary Infection Score
💡
Explanation

1. BAL culture directly samples lower respiratory tract.
2. Quantitative threshold confirms infection, not colonization.
3. Chest X-ray may be nonspecific in ICU patients.
4. ET tube cultures often contaminated with upper airway flora.
5. CPIS is supportive but not definitive.
6. BAL culture remains diagnostic gold standard.
Question 13 of 50
Which is the most common organism causing ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) in ICU patients?
A. Pseudomonas aeruginosa ✓
B. Klebsiella pneumoniae
C. Staphylococcus aureus
D. Escherichia coli
💡
Explanation
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the leading cause of late-onset VAP due to its ability to thrive in moist environments like ventilator tubing. It has high antibiotic resistance, making prevention critical with strict infection control measures.

Question 24 of 50
For a patient with a suspected CRBSI, which pair of specimens is required for definitive diagnosis?

B. Stool and Blood
C. Peripheral Blood and Catheter-tip Blood
 ✓
D. Sputum and Stool
💡
Explanation
Definitive diagnosis of a CRBSI often requires paired blood cultures: one drawn from a peripheral vein and one drawn through the central line catheter. If the catheter culture turns positive significantly earlier (differential time to positivity), it confirms the catheter as the source.
Question 25 of 50

Question 31 of 50
Which specimen type is typically not sterile under normal conditions?

 ✓
D. Bladder Urine
💡

Question 32 of 50
A VAP bundle includes all of the following EXCEPT:
A. Sedation vacations and daily assessment of readiness to extubate
B. Prophylactic use of systemic antibiotics
 ✓
C. Peptic ulcer disease prophylaxis
D. Deep vein thrombosis prophylaxis
💡
Explanation
The VAP bundle focuses on mechanical and physiological prevention strategies. Prophylactic systemic antibiotics are not used as they would lead to antimicrobial resistance. The other options (sedation vacation, PUD/DVT prophylaxis) are standard components.
Question 33 of 50

💡
Explanation
An infection related to the surgery that involves any part of the anatomy deeper than the fascia/muscle layers that was opened or manipulated during the procedure is an organ/space SSI. A drain is placed into a potential organ/space.
Question 34 of 50

Question 35 of 50
