Question 6 of 50
Most useful parameter to predict the virulence of acute illness is:
A. Standardised mortality ratio (SMR)
B. Case fatality rate (CFR) ✓
C. Secondary attack rate (SAR)
D. Incidence
💡
Explanation

  1. Standardized Mortality Ratio (SMR): This compares the observed number of deaths in a study population with the number of deaths expected based on a larger population. While useful for assessing mortality in specific populations, it doesn’t directly measure the virulence of a disease.

  2. Case Fatality Rate (CFR): This measures the proportion of deaths among confirmed cases of a disease. CFR provides a direct indication of how deadly a disease is, making it a key parameter for assessing virulence.

  3. Secondary Attack Rate (SAR): This measures the likelihood of disease spread among contacts of infected individuals. While it indicates transmissibility, it doesn’t provide direct information about the severity or virulence of the illness.

  4. Incidence: This measures the number of new cases in a population over a specified time period. While it indicates how widespread a disease is, it doesn’t provide insights into the severity or virulence.

Thus, the solution is:

  • Correct Answer: Case Fatality Rate (CFR)

Question 8 of 50
Demographic Processes' does not include:
A. Fertility
B. Morbidity
C. Mortality
D. Social mobility ✓
💡
Explanation

The term demographic processes refers to the changes in the size and structure of populations. These processes are primarily related to factors that influence the growth or decline of populations, including:

  1. Fertility: The birth rate or number of live births in a population.
  2. Morbidity: The incidence or prevalence of diseases and health conditions in a population.
  3. Mortality: The death rate or number of deaths in a population.

However, social mobility refers to the movement of individuals or groups within or between social strata in a society, often in terms of economic or educational status. It is not directly related to demographic processes, which focus on population dynamics such as births, deaths, and health.

Question 12 of 50
Cause to effect progression is seen in all except:
A. Case control study ✓
B. Ecological study
C. Cohort study
D. Randomized control trial
💡
Explanation

Cause-to-effect progression is typically studied in study designs that follow subjects over time to observe the development of outcomes after exposure. These include:
Cohort study: Follows a group over time to see if exposure leads to an effect (cause to effect).
Randomized control trial (RCT): Participants are randomly assigned to exposure or control groups, and outcomes are observed prospectively, showing cause and effect.
Ecological study: Studies populations or groups and may infer cause-effect relationships, though less direct than cohort or RCT.

Question 15 of 50
True about prevalence:
A. It is a ratio
B. Prevalence rate is the ideal measure for studying disease etiology or causation
C. Increases with increase in duration of disease ✓
D. Decreases with decrease in case fatality
💡
Explanation

Prevalence refers to the total number of cases (both new and existing) of a disease in a population at a specific point in time or over a specified period.

  • Option 1: Prevalence is not simply a ratio, but rather a measure that considers the number of existing cases in a population relative to the total population. It's typically expressed as a percentage.

  • Option 2: Prevalence is not the ideal measure for studying disease etiology or causation. Incidence is a better measure for understanding the causation and spread of a disease, as it focuses on new cases.

  • Option 3: Prevalence increases with the duration of disease because longer-lasting diseases accumulate more cases in the population, leading to higher prevalence.

  • Option 4: Decreased case fatality would increase the number of people living with the disease, thereby increasing the prevalence, not decreasing it.

Question 38 of 50
Robert Koch discovered the etiologic agent of
A. Typhoid
B. Small pox
C. Tuberculosis ✓
D. Polio
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 3: Tuberculosis.

    Robert Koch, a pioneering German physician and microbiologist, famously discovered Mycobacterium tuberculosis, the bacterium responsible for tuberculosis (TB), on March 24, 1882. His meticulous work provided the first definitive proof that a specific microorganism caused the disease, which was previously thought to be hereditary. For this groundbreaking research and his investigations into the nature of the disease, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1905.
    Why other options are incorrect
    Option 1: Typhoid: The etiologic agent of typhoid (Salmonella typhi) was discovered in 1884 by Georg Theodor August Gaffky, who was one of Koch's students, not Koch himself.
    Option 2: Smallpox: Smallpox is caused by the variola virus. Robert Koch focused on bacterial pathogens; the most famous advancement regarding smallpox was the development of the first vaccine by Edward Jenner in 1796.
    Option 4: Polio: Polio is caused by a virus (poliovirus). Koch's postulates were designed for identifying bacteria, and viruses cannot be grown in pure culture using the solid media techniques he pioneered.

Question 40 of 50

A. Filaria
B. Tuberculosis ✓
C. Malaria
D. Hookworm infestation
💡
Explanation

  • Tuberculosis (TB): TB is a contagious airborne disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It spreads directly from person to person through the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks.

  • Filaria (Filariasis): This is a vector-borne disease, not a directly contagious one. It is transmitted through the bite of an infected mosquito (vector), not by direct contact with an infected person.​

  • Malaria: Similar to filariasis, malaria is a vector-borne disease transmitted by the bite of an infected Anopheles mosquito. It does not spread directly from person to person.​

  • Hookworm infestation: This is a soil-transmitted helminth infection. It is contracted by walking barefoot on contaminated soil where hookworm larvae penetrate the skin, not by contact with an infected person.
