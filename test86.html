The position in which a patient with Dyspnea should be placed is:
A. Sims

D. Prone

Explanation
Orthopneic Position: This position involves the patient sitting up and leaning slightly forward, often with support on their knees or a table. It helps open the chest and improves lung expansion, which can ease breathing difficulties.
Question 2 of 50
During physical assessment of children, asymmetry of the skull indicates:
A. Hydrocephalus
B. Craniotabes
C. Craniosynostosis ✓
D. Osteogenesis imperfect
💡
Explanation
–Craniotabes are softening or thinning of skull in infants and children especially seen in newborn. Osteogenesis imperfecta are genetic disorders that affect bones. Hydrocephalous is a condition in which there is an accumulation of CSF in brain.
Question 3 of 50
A low-pitched, continuous gurgling sounds caused by secretions in the large airways is called:
A. Wheezes
B. Rhonchi ✓
C. Coughing
D. Stridor
💡
Explanation
–Wheezing is a high-pitched whistling sound made while breathing. Stridor is a high-pitched, wheezing sound caused by disrupted airflow.
Question 4 of 50
Instrument used for examining eye is ............
A. Otoscope
B. Proctoscope

D. Endoscope

Explanation
–Ophthalmoscope, also called funduscope, is an instrument used for inspecting the interior of the eye. Otoscope is used for examining ears and proctoscope is used to examine the anal cavity, rectum and sigmoid colon. Endoscope is used to visualize body cavity.

Inspection: Observing the patient for any signs or abnormalities.

Palpation: Using the hands to feel for physical characteristics of organs and tissues.

Percussion: Tapping on the body to assess the underlying structures and their density.

Question 7 of 50
Crackling sounds bilaterally at the bases of lungs is referred
A. Rhonchi
B. Wheezing
C. Rales ✓
D. Pleural rub
💡
Explanation

Rales, also known as crackles, are abnormal lung sounds heard during auscultation and are often associated with fluid in the airways or alveoli. These sounds can be indicative of conditions such as pneumonia, congestive heart failure, or pulmonary fibrosis.

Question 8 of 50
The patient has voided only 50-100 mL of urine after the removal of Foley's catheter. What is the priority nursing action?
A. Check for bladder distention ✓
B. Encourage fluid intake
C. Re-catheterize the client
D. Monitor I/O chart
💡
Explanation

Assessing for bladder distention is crucial to determine if the bladder is not emptying properly or if there is a possible obstruction. This can help identify if the patient needs further intervention, such as catheterization or additional medical evaluation. After checking for bladder distention, you may then consider other actions like encouraging fluid intake or monitoring the intake and output (I/O) chart, based on the findings.

Question 9 of 50
Which of the following strategy is NOT effective in enhancing a patient's impaired vision?
A. Use bright colors around patient
B. Stand in patient's field of vision
C. Use of fluorescent lighting ✓
D. Ensure enough light inside the room
💡
Explanation
Fluorescent lighting can sometimes create glare or cause discomfort for individuals with vision impairments. Instead, it’s often better to use natural or warm light sources and ensure the room is well-lit in a way that reduces glare and provides even illumination. The other strategies—using bright colors, standing in the patient’s field of vision, and ensuring adequate light—are generally helpful for enhancing visual accessibility.
Question 10 of 50
Redness over the bony prominence of a bed ridden patient suggests
A. Ulceration
B. Bedsore ✓
C. Poor nutritional status
D. Wound infection
💡
Explanation

Redness in these areas can be an early sign of a pressure ulcer, also known as a bedsore. These sores occur due to prolonged pressure on the skin, particularly over bony areas, which can compromise blood flow and lead to tissue damage. Prompt intervention is essential to prevent further progression to more severe stages of pressure ulcers.

A. Auscultation

C. Inspection

💡
Explanation

History taking is a critical part of the overall assessment but is not a physical examination method. The physical examination methods include:

Auscultation: Listening to body sounds with a stethoscope.

Inspection: Observing the patient’s physical appearance, behavior, and movements.

Palpation: Using the hands to feel the body for texture, size, consistency, and location of structures.

Question 15 of 50
Which of the following assessment data is a normal finding when performing percussion of the client's chest?
A. Resonance ✓
B. Hyperresonance
C. Tympany
D. Dullness
💡
Explanation

Resonance is the normal sound heard over healthy lung tissue when percussing the chest. It indicates a normal amount of air in the lungs.

Hyperresonance can indicate increased air in the lungs, such as in cases of emphysema or pneumothorax.

Tympany is more commonly heard over the abdomen, not the chest, and suggests a hollow, drum-like sound.

Dullness can indicate fluid or solid matter in the chest, such as in cases of pleural effusion or pneumonia.

Question 36 of 50
Which of the following is an accurate description of vesicular breath sounds?
A. They are soft, low-pitched sounds heard best over the base of the lungs during respiration. Inspiration is longer than expiration ✓
B. They are noisy, strenuous respirations.
C. They are high-pitched sounds heard on inspiration when there is a narrowing of the upper airway.
D. They are high-pitched, harsh sounds.
With expiration being longer than inspiration
💡
Explanation

Vesicular breath sounds These are normal breath sounds that are soft and low-pitched. They are heard over most of the lung fields, especially the peripheral areas. During vesicular breathing, inspiration is longer and louder compared to expiration, which is shorter and softer.

Noisy, strenuous respirationsThis description is not typical of vesicular breath sounds. Noisy, strenuous respirations may indicate abnormal breath sounds such as wheezes or stridor.

High-pitched sounds heard on inspiration when there is a narrowing of the upper airway This describes stridor or wheezes, not vesicular breath sounds. Stridor is a high-pitched sound heard primarily on inspiration due to upper airway obstruction.

High-pitched, harsh sounds with expiration being longer than inspiration This describes bronchial breath sounds, not vesicular. Bronchial sounds are louder and higher-pitched with a longer expiration phase compared to inspiration.

Explanation

Homan's sign: This is a clinical test used to check for the presence of deep vein thrombosis (DVT). It is performed by dorsiflexing the foot while palpating the calf, which may elicit pain if a thrombus is present. A positive Homan’s sign is not a definitive test for DVT but may indicate a need for further investigation.

Encourage good venous circulation For patients suspected of having DVT or at risk for thromboembolism, promoting good venous circulation is crucial. This includes activities like encouraging movement, using compression stockings, and avoiding prolonged periods of immobility. These measures help prevent complications related to poor venous circulation and reduce the risk of further clot formation.

Encourage fluid and electrolyte balance While important in overall patient care, this is not the primary focus related to a positive Homan’s sign. Fluid and electrolyte balance is more relevant to other aspects of patient management.

Secure patent airway This is a priority in cases of respiratory distress or compromised airway, not specifically related to a positive Homan’s sign.

Promote skin care This is important for general patient care but is not directly related to the immediate concerns of a positive Homan’s sign or potential DVT.
