Question 1 of 50
Which of these is not a congenital heart disease?
A. Atrial septal defect
B. Tetralogy of fallot
C. Ischemic heart disease ✓
D. Tricuspid atresia
💡
Explanation

Ischemic heart disease is not a congenital heart disease; it is an acquired condition that develops after birth due to reduced blood supply to the heart, usually caused by atherosclerosis or other acquired risk factors.​

  • Atrial septal defect: This is a structural defect in the heart present at birth, making it a congenital heart disease.​

  • Tetralogy of Fallot: This is a combination of heart defects that are present from birth (congenital).​

  • Ischemic heart disease: This condition is acquired, not present at birth, and is primarily caused by factors like cholesterol, hypertension, and blockage of coronary arteries.​

  • Tricuspid atresia: This is another congenital heart defect, involving improper formation of the tricuspid valve, and it is present from birth.

Question 2 of 50
In the following which is a congenital cyanotic heart disease:
A. Atrial septal defect
B. Ventricular septal defect
C. Patent ductus arteriosus
D. Pulmonary stenosis ✓
💡
Explanation
  • Cyanotic heart diseases cause a right-to-left shunt, leading to decreased oxygenation of blood and resulting in cyanosis (bluish discoloration of the skin due to low oxygen levels).
  • Pulmonary stenosis (severe cases) can lead to Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF), a well-known congenital cyanotic heart defect. TOF includes four abnormalities:
    • Pulmonary stenosis
    • Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
    • Overriding aorta
    • Right ventricular hypertrophy

A. Fowler's position
B. Knee-Chest position ✓
C. Supine position
D. Prone position
💡
Explanation
A baby experiencing cyanotic spells (commonly seen in conditions like Tetralogy of Fallot) can be calmed and relieved by placing them in the knee-chest position. This position increases systemic vascular resistance, which helps reduce the right-to-left shunting of blood, thereby improving oxygenation. It also decreases venous return to the heart, reducing the workload on the right ventricle.

Question 16 of 50

💡
Explanation

Group A beta-hemolytic streptococci is indeed the most common causative organism responsible for rheumatic fever. This bacterium causes streptococcal throat infections, and if left untreated, it can lead to rheumatic fever as a delayed immune response.

The Jones criteria is the diagnostic tool used to diagnose rheumatic fever. It includes major criteria (like carditis, erythema marginatum, and polyarthritis) and minor criteria (like fever and arthralgia), which help clinicians confirm the diagnosis based on clinical findings.

Question 20 of 50
A child with cyanotic heart disease brought to emergency with cyanotic spell, most important nursing management is
A. Place the child in high Fowler's position
B. Start IV fluid
C. Decrease hypoxic spells ✓
D. Minimize activity
💡
Explanation

A cyanotic spell (Tet spell) in a child with cyanotic congenital heart disease is a medical emergency caused by a sudden decrease in pulmonary blood flow, leading to severe hypoxia.

Immediate nursing priorities are to:

  • Reduce hypoxia

  • Improve oxygenation

  • Calm the child and reduce oxygen demand

Although the classic and most effective position is the knee–chest position (not given in the options), the primary goal of all emergency nursing interventions is to decrease hypoxic spells.

Why other options are less appropriate:

  • High Fowler’s position – increases venous pooling and can worsen hypoxia

  • Start IV fluids – supportive, but not the most immediate priority

  • Minimize activity – helpful, but secondary to correcting hypoxia

Question 27 of 50
Which imaging modality is most accurate for assessing the severity of aortic stenosis?
A. Chest X-ray
B. Echocardiography with Doppler ✓
C. Cardiac MRI
D. Electrocardiography (ECG)
💡
Explanation

Option 2: Echocardiography with Doppler

Explanation:

Echocardiography with Doppler is the most accurate and widely used imaging modality for assessing the severity of aortic stenosis. It provides real-time imaging of the aortic valve and allows measurement of key parameters such as:

  • Aortic valve area (AVA)
  • Peak and mean transvalvular pressure gradients
  • Velocity across the aortic valve (using continuous-wave Doppler)
  • Left ventricular function assessment

Why not the other options?

  1. Chest X-ray – It may show signs of left ventricular hypertrophy or post-stenotic dilation of the aorta but does not directly assess valve function.
  2. Cardiac MRI – While useful for structural evaluation, it is not the first-line modality for assessing dynamic flow across the stenotic valve.
  3. Electrocardiography (ECG) – It can detect left ventricular hypertrophy and strain patterns but cannot directly assess stenosis severity.

Thus, Echocardiography with Doppler remains the gold standard for evaluating aortic stenosis severity.
