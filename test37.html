Question 38 of 50
A 25-year-old construction worker presents to the Urgent Care center after a metal spring recoiled and struck him in the face. He is alert and oriented. His primary complaints are a profuse, active nosebleed and severe pain in his left eye. On initial inspection, the left eye appears deeply red with a teardrop-shaped pupil and blood in the anterior chamber (hyphema). There is no obvious foreign body protruding from the eye.
What is the most critical and immediate sequence of action for the initial responder?
A. Immediately lay the patient flat, apply a sterile moist pad to the left eye, and arrange for emergent transport to an ophthalmologist.
B. Sit the patient up, lean him forward, and pinch the soft part of his nose. Once the epistaxis is controlled, gently place a rigid eye shield over the left eye without applying pressure. ✓
C. Administer a systemic analgesic for pain, then simultaneously pack the nostrils to control the bleed and apply a firm pressure patch to the injured eye.
D. Have the patient lie on his side (recovery position) and irrigate both the nose and the affected eye with sterile saline to clear the blood.
💡
Explanation
This sequence correctly addresses both problems without causing harm.
–Step 1 (Nosebleed): The nosebleed is an active haemorrhage and an airway risk. Sitting the patient up and leaning forward prevents aspiration of blood. Direct pressure is the first-line, effective treatment. This follows the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) of emergency response.
–Step 2 (Eye Injury): The findings (teardrop pupil, hyphema) are highly suggestive of a ruptured globe, a sight-threatening ophthalmologic emergency. The only appropriate pre-transport management for a suspected ruptured globe is to cover it with a rigid shield (like a Fox shield) that does not touch the eye itself. This prevents any external pressure from exacerbating the injury. No irrigation, no patch, no pressure.

Question 40 of 50
A 15-year-old boy is brought to the emergency department after being hit in the face with a baseball. He has a noticeable nosebleed (epistaxis) and a significant injury to his right eye. The eye is painful, red, and his vision is blurry. There is no obvious foreign body.
What is the most appropriate initial action by the healthcare provider?
A. Sit the patient upright, lean him forward, and apply direct pressure to the soft part of his nose to control the epistaxis. ✓
B. Immediately apply a firm, pressure bandage covering both the injured eye and the nose.
C. Lay the patient flat to improve blood flow to the head and cover the injured eye with a sterile pad.
D. Focus solely on the eye injury by gently irrigating it with saline and applying a protective eye shield, as vision is the priority.
💡
Explanation
Airway and Aspiration Risk: A significant nosebleed can cause blood to trickle down the back of the throat. If the patient is lying down or leaning back, this blood can be aspirated into the lungs, causing choking or respiratory complications. Sitting upright and leaning forward prevents this.
Control of Haemorrhage: Direct pressure is the first-line, effective treatment for most anterior nosebleeds. Controlling bleeding is a fundamental step in any trauma assessment (following the ABCs - Airway, Breathing, Circulation).
–Clears the Field: Once the nosebleed is controlled, it is much easier to properly assess the more complex and serious eye injury without blood obscuring the view.

Which is the universal color code for poison container in India?
A. Blue
B. Black
C. Red
D. Black with red label ✓

Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4 :Black with red label.
  • Explanation
  • In India, the Insecticides Rules, 1971 mandate a specific labeling scheme for pesticide and poison containers to indicate their toxicity levels. These containers feature a diamond-shaped label divided into two halves. The lower half of this label for extremely toxic substances is colored bright red. While the container itself may be various materials, the standard coding for identifying "poison" in a regulatory and safety context—especially in industrial or pharmaceutical disposal—often utilizes a black container (for discarded medicines/chemicals) specifically paired with a red label to signal the highest level of danger.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1 :Blue: This color code is used for moderately toxic substances. According to Indian toxicity labeling, a blue label indicates a pesticide is only slightly hazardous or "moderately toxic" compared to the extreme danger of red.
  • Option 2 :Black: In biomedical waste management, plain black bags or containers are typically reserved for non-hazardous general waste, such as kitchen waste, paper, and office stationery.
  • Option 3 :Red: While red is the symbol for danger, a solid red container in Indian biomedical rules is specifically used for recyclable contaminated waste like syringes (without needles) and IV sets, rather than the primary identification of a poison container itself.
