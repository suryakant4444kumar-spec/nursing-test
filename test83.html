Question 1 of 50
The nurse is working client with a somatoform disorder. Which client outcome goal would the nurse most likely establish in this situation?
A. The client will express anxiety verbally rather than through physical symptoms. ✓
B. The client able to recognizes symptoms of physical illness.
C. The client will cope with physical illness.
D. The client will take prescribed medications.
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1: The client will express anxiety verbally rather than through physical symptoms.
  • Explanation
  • A somatoform disorder (now often referred to as somatic symptom disorder) involves the unconscious displacement of psychological distress or anxiety into physical (somatic) symptoms. Because these symptoms are a maladaptive way of dealing with emotional conflict, the primary goal of nursing care is to help the client identify and express those underlying emotions directly.
  • Verbalizing anxiety instead of "converting" it into a physical complaint is a significant sign of clinical progress and improved coping. Nursing interventions typically focus on encouraging this verbal expression through therapeutic communication and journal keeping to help the client see the link between stress and their body’s reactions.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: The client able to recognize symptoms of physical illness. Clients with somatoform disorders are already hyper-focused on physical symptoms and often misinterpret normal bodily sensations as signs of serious illness. Encouraging more focus on "recognizing" physical symptoms would likely reinforce their preoccupation rather than resolve the underlying psychological cause.
  • Option 3: The client will cope with physical illness. In somatoform disorders, there is typically no underlying organic pathology that explains the severity of the symptoms. The "illness" is psychological in origin; Therefore, "coping with physical illness" is inappropriate because the primary issue is the psychological conflict, not a biological disease.
  • Option 4: The client will take prescribed medication. While medications may be used to treat co-occurring conditions like depression or anxiety, there are no specific medications to "cure" somatoform disorders themselves. Furthermore, a reliance on medication can sometimes reinforce the client's belief that their problem is purely medical, which may hinder the psychological work needed for recovery.
Question 2 of 50
Three months after the incident the client complains of difficulty to concentrate, poor appetite, inability to sleep. She is likely suffering from:
A. Post traumatic disorder ✓
B. Adjustment disorder
C. Somatoform Disorder
D. Generalized Anxiety Disorder
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1: Post traumatic disorder.
  • Explanation
  • Based on the symptoms and timeline, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is the most likely diagnosis.
  • Timeline: PTSD is diagnosed when symptoms persist for more than one month after a traumatic event. Symptoms typically begin within the first three months, which aligns perfectly with the "three months after the incident" timeframe mentioned.
  • Symptom Match: The client's specific complaints—difficulty concentrating, poor appetite, and inability to sleep—are core "arousal and reactivity" symptoms of PTSD. Specifically:
  • Inability to sleep and difficulty concentrating are classic signs of hyperarousal.
  • Poor appetite and sleep disturbances can also be associated with the negative changes in mood and cognition seen in PTSD.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: Adjustment disorder: While it also occurs within three months of a stressor, it is typically a response to "common" life stressors (like a breakup or job loss) rather than a "traumatic incident". Furthermore, if the symptoms meet the criteria for PTSD, that diagnosis takes precedence.
  • Option 3: Somatoform Disorder: This disorder (now often called Somatic Symptom Disorder) involves an extreme focus on physical symptoms (like pain or fatigue) that causes major emotional distress and problems functioning, rather than a clear psychological response to a specific traumatic event.
  • Option 4: Generalized Anxiety Disorder: GAD requires excessive worry about a variety of topics occurring on more days than not for at least six months, which does not fit the three-month post-incident timeline provided.

Question 6 of 50
A client reports losing his sight in both eyes. He's diagnosed as having conversion disorder and is admitted to the psychiatric unit. Which nursing intervention would be most appropriate for this client?
A. Not focusing on his blindness ✓
B. Providing self-care for him
C. Telling him that his blindness isn't real
D. Teaching eye exercises to strengthen his eyes
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1: Not focusing on his blindness.
  • Explanation
  • For a client with conversion disorder, nursing interventions should focus on the client's feelings and the psychological stressors that precipitated the symptoms, rather than the physical symptoms themselves. Focusing on the blindness or other physical impairments can provide secondary gain (the reinforcement or attention received from being ill), which may inadvertently prolong the symptoms. By not focusing on the blindness, the nurse encourages the client to engage in socialization and therapeutic conversation about other topics, helping to shift the focus away from the physical manifestation of their psychological distress.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: Providing self-care for him
  • This intervention fosters dependency and reinforces the sick role, which is counterproductive. The nurse should instead encourage the client to perform as much self-care as possible to promote independence and prevent the "secondary gain" associated with having others perform tasks for them.
  • Option 3: Telling him that his blindness isn't real
  • Conversion disorder symptoms are real to the client even though there is no underlying organic cause. Confronting the client by telling them the symptom isn't "real" can increase anxiety, damage the therapeutic relationship, and make the client more defensive or resistant to treatment.
  • Option 4: Teaching eye exercises to strengthen his eyes
  • This intervention is inappropriate because it validates the client's belief that there is a physical or organic problem with the eyes. Since conversion disorder is a psychological condition manifesting as a physical loss of function, strengthening the eyes will not address the root psychological cause.
Question 7 of 50
Loss of personal identity and wandering away from psychiatric hospital is referred as:
A. Fugue ✓
B. Split personality
C. Possession state
D. Trance state
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1: Fugue.
  • Explanation
  • Dissociative fugue (derived from the Latin fuga, meaning "flight") is a psychological state characterized by a sudden, unexpected loss of personal identity and wandering away from one's usual surroundings. During such an episode, an individual may travel long distances and even assume a completely new identity without memory any of their past life. It is often triggered by severe emotional trauma or extreme stress.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: Split personality (now known as Dissociative Identity Disorder): While it involves a fragmented identity with two or more distinct personality states, it is characterized by "switching" between these personalities within the same individual, rather than being specifically defined by the act of wandering or fleeing.
  • Option 3: Possession state: This is a dissociative condition where an individual’s identity is temporarily replaced by that of a spirit, deity, or other "external" being, typically occurring within cultural or religious contexts rather than involving physical flight and travel to a new location.
  • Option 4: Trance state: This refers to a temporary alteration in consciousness marked by a lack of awareness or unresponsiveness to the environment, often accompanied by automatic movements, but it does not necessarily include a total loss of identity or the act of wandering away to start a new life.
Question 8 of 50
Revised name of the Multiple Personality is:
A. DID ✓
B. MDP
C. OCD
D. None of the Above
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 1: DID.
  • Explanation
  • The condition formerly known as Multiple Personality Disorder (MPD) was officially renamed to Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID) in the 1994 publication of the DSM-IV by the American Psychiatric Association. This change was made to more accurately reflect the nature of the condition—which is a fragmentation or splintering of a single identity into different states, rather than the growth of completely separate, independent personalities.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 2: MDP typically stands for Manic Depressive Psychosis, which is an older term for what is now known as Bipolar Disorder.
  • Option 3: OCD stands for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder, a completely different mental health condition characterized by intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors (compulsions).
  • Option 4: None of the Above is incorrect because DID is the globally recognized revised medical name for the disorder
Question 9 of 50
The recording of conversation between the nurse and patient in a psychiatric set up is termed:
A. Therapeutic communication
B. Interview technique
C. Process recording ✓
D. Case study
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 3: Process recording.
  • Explanation
  • In a psychiatric or mental health nursing setup, a process recording is a verbatim (word-for-word) written account of the interaction between the nurse and the patient. It captures not only the spoken words (verbal communication) but also non-verbal cues such as body language, facial expressions, and pauses. The primary purpose of this tool is for the nurse or student to critically analyze their communication skills, assess the patient’s psychological state, and evaluate the dynamics of the therapeutic relationship.
  • Why other options are incorrect
  • Option 1: Therapeutic communication refers to the style or collection of techniques (such as active listening, restating, and clarifying) used by a nurse to help a patient achieve emotional well-being. It is the act of communicating, not the record of it.
  • Option 2: Interview technique refers to the specific methods (like open-ended questions or probing) used to gather information during an interview. While an interview may be recorded, the term "interview technique" does not describe the documentation itself.
  • Option 4: Case study is a comprehensive, in-depth investigation of a patient's history, symptoms, diagnosis, and treatment plan over time. It is much broader than a single recorded conversation between a nurse and a patient.

Question 12 of 50
A person who deliberately pretends an illness is usually through to be:
A. Neurotic
B. Psychosis
C. Out of concept with reality
D. Using conversion defences ✓
💡
Explanation
  • A person who deliberately pretends an illness is most accurately described as malingering or having a factitious disorder, both of which involve conscious deception. Within the specific options provided in your list:
  • Option 1: Neurotic is the most common answer in classic psychiatric testing for this specific question. Historically, "neurosis" referred to mental disorders where the person remains in touch with reality but uses maladaptive behaviors to cope with deep-seated anxiety or conflict.
  • Option 4: Using conversion defences is incorrect because conversion disorder (Functional Neurological Disorder) is characterized by symptoms that are unintentional and not under the person's conscious control.
  • Options 2 and 3 refer to psychosis, which involves a "loss of touch with reality" (hallucinations or delusions). A person pretending an illness is, by definition, aware of the reality that they are not actually sick.
  • Key Distinctions in "Pretending" Illness
  • Psychiatry distinguishes between faking for external vs. internal gain:
  • Malingering: Deliberate faking for a tangible external benefit, such as avoiding work, skipping military service, or obtaining a financial settlement.
  • Factitious Disorder (Munchausen Syndrome): Deliberate faking to assume the "sick role" and receive sympathy or attention, driven by internal psychological needs rather than material gain.

Question 14 of 50
Visual hallucinations, illusions, disorientation are typical features of
A. Delirium ✓
B. Dementia
C. Schizophrenia
D. Dissociative disorders
💡
Explanation
  • Visual hallucinations, illusions, and disorientation are classic, hallmark features of Delirium.
  • Delirium is an acute, fluctuating syndrome characterized by a disturbance in consciousness, decreased attention, and perceptual disturbances (like visual hallucinations). Visual hallucinations in delirium are often vivid, complex, and may be frightening.
  • Dementia generally involves a slow, progressive decline in cognitive function, and while hallucinations can occur (particularly in Lewy body dementia), they are not usually associated with the acute disorientation and fluctuating consciousness seen in delirium.
  • Schizophrenia is primarily characterized by auditory hallucinations (voices), although visual hallucinations can occur.
  • Dissociative disorders involve disruptions in consciousness, memory, and identity, not typical visual hallucinations and disorientation.
  • Option 1 :Delirium
Question 15 of 50
Dissociation is present in all, EXCEPT:
A. Fugue
B. Delirium ✓
C. Multiple personality
D. Somnambulism
💡
Explanation
Dissociation refers to a mental process where a person disconnects from their thoughts, feelings, memories, or sense of identity.
● Fugue involves dissociation, where a person may forget their identity and engage in unexpected travel.
● Multiple personality (Dissociative Identity Disorder) involves dissociation, where a person has multiple distinct identities or personality states.
● Somnambulism (sleepwalking) can also be considered a dissociative state, as the person may not be fully aware of their actions while asleep.
● Delirium, however, involves an acute state of confusion and altered consciousness but does not primarily involve dissociation. It is more related to cognitive disturbances rather than dissociative processes.
