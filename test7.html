Question 1 of 50
Time duration of cardiac cycle is
A. 0.5 sec
B. 0.8 sec ✓
C. 1 sec
D. 2 sec
💡
Explanation
The cardiac cycle refers to the sequence of events that occur from the beginning of one heartbeat to the beginning of the next. The average duration of the cardiac cycle in a healthy adult at rest, with a typical heart rate of 72 beats per minute, is approximately 0.8 seconds. This includes both the contraction (systole) and relaxation (diastole) phases of the heart.
Question 2 of 50
Slowest conduction is in
A. SA node
B. Purkinje fibers
C. AV node ✓
D. Bundle of His
💡
Explanation

  1. The cardiac impulse starts from the SA node (pacemaker).

  2. It travels through the atria and reaches the AV node.

  3. The AV node delays the conduction of the impulse to allow the atria to contract completely before the ventricles begin to contract.

  4. This delay is due to slow conduction velocity in the AV node.

  5. The conduction velocity in the AV node is about 0.02–0.05 m/s, which is the slowest in the heart.

  6. After the AV node, the impulse travels rapidly through the Bundle of His, bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers.

  7. Thus, the AV node provides the necessary delay for proper cardiac cycle coordination.

Question 3 of 50
Which of these planes does the chest leads of a 12-leaded ECG (electrocardiograph) record the heart electrical activity?
A. Sagittal
B. Coronal
C. Transverse
D. Horizontal ✓
💡
Explanation

In a 12-lead ECG, the chest (precordial) leads record the heart's electrical activity along the horizontal plane. These leads (V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6) are placed on the chest and capture electrical signals from the heart's anterior, lateral, and left regions in the horizontal plane.The horizontal plane divides the body into upper and lower parts, and the chest leads are oriented to measure the heart's electrical activity in this direction. This allows the ECG to capture the heart's activity from the front, sides, and left part of the chest.

Explanation
The P wave on the electrocardiogram (ECG) represents atrial depolarization, which is the electrical activity associated with the contraction of the atria. When the sinoatrial (SA) node fires, it causes depolarization of the atrial myocardium, resulting in the P wave on the ECG.

Atrial repolarization is not seen on ECG because it is masked by the QRS complex. Hence, the P wave shows atrial depolarization initiated by the SA node.
Question 6 of 50
'T' wave in an ECG represents
A. Ventricular depolarization
B. Ventricular repolarization ✓
C. Atrial depolarization
D. Atrial repolarization
💡
Explanation

The T wave on the electrocardiogram (ECG) represents ventricular repolarization, which is the process of the ventricles returning to their resting electrical state after contracting (systole).

  • Ventricular depolarization is represented by the QRS complex.
  • Atrial depolarization is represented by the P wave.
  • Atrial repolarization occurs during the QRS complex, but it is not visible on the ECG due to the larger electrical activity of the ventricles.

Question 10 of 50
Normal PR interval in ECG is:
A. 0.20-0.35 sec
B. 0.30-0.45 sec
C. 0.35-0.40 sec
D. 0.12-0.20 sec ✓
💡
Explanation

The PR interval on an electrocardiogram (ECG) represents the time it takes for the electrical impulse to travel from the atria (starting at the sinoatrial node) to the ventricles (through the atrioventricular node). A normal PR interval is typically **120 to 200 milliseconds (0.12-0.20 seconds).
If the PR interval is longer than 0.20 seconds, it may indicate first-degree heart block. If it's shorter than 0.12 seconds, it might suggest conditions like Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome.

Question 11 of 50
Hyperkalemia leads to what changes in ECG?
A. Inverted T waves
B. Tall T waves ✓
C. Prolonged QT interval
D. Wide
💡
Explanation
Hyperkalemia (elevated potassium levels in the blood) causes several changes in the electrocardiogram (ECG). One of the earliest and most characteristic changes is the appearance of tall, peaked T waves. As the potassium level increases, it can lead to other changes, such as a prolonged PR interval, widening of the QRS complex, and in severe cases, sine wave pattern, but the tall T waves are one of the most prominent and early signs of hyperkalemia on an ECG.
Question 12 of 50
QRS complex QRS complex in ECG represents
A. Atrial depolarization
B. Ventricular depolarization ✓
C. Ventricular Repolarization
D. Atrial repolarization
💡
Explanation
The QRS complex in an electrocardiogram (ECG) represents ventricular depolarization. It is the electrical activity that triggers the contraction of the ventricles, which is essential for pumping blood out of the heart. The QRS complex is characterized by three main waves: the Q wave (downward deflection), the R wave (upward deflection), and the S wave (downward deflection following the R wave). It does not represent atrial depolarization (which is reflected by the P wave), nor does it represent ventricular repolarization (which is shown by the T wave).
Question 13 of 50
A patient with ST depression in ECG represents with last history of smoking and alcohol and nursing officer analysis the ECG ST segment depression indicate?
A. COPD
B. Angina ✓
C. Hyperkalemia
D. Hypercalcemia
💡
Explanation

The presence of ST depression on an ECG, combined with the patient’s history of smoking and alcohol consumption, strongly points towards angina.

  • Angina is often associated with myocardial ischemia, where there is insufficient blood flow to the heart muscle. This ischemia is reflected on an ECG by ST segment depression and/or T-wave inversion. Angina can be triggered by physical exertion or emotional stress and often presents with chest pain.

  • COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease) can result from smoking, but it primarily affects the lungs and may cause changes like low oxygen levels or arrhythmias, but ST depression is not typically a primary feature of COPD.

  • Hyperkalemia (high potassium levels) can cause peaked T waves on an ECG, not ST depression.

  • Hypercalcemia (high calcium levels) can cause shortened QT intervals, but it does not typically cause ST depression.

Given the combination of ST depression and a history of smoking and alcohol use, the most likely diagnosis here is angina, particularly if the patient presents with chest pain or discomfort.

Question 19 of 50
In an adult patient, arterial function of the lower extremity can be determined by assessing the
A. Allen's test
B. Homan's sign
C. Pedal pulse ✓
D. Cullen's sign
💡
Explanation

In an adult, the arterial function of the lower extremities is best assessed by palpating the pedal pulses — specifically the dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial arteries. The presence, strength, and symmetry of these pulses indicate proper arterial blood flow to the legs and feet.

  • Allen’s test checks arterial flow in the hand (radial and ulnar arteries).

  • Homan’s sign indicates possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT), not arterial function.

  • Cullen’s sign relates to internal bleeding around the umbilicus.
    Hence, pedal pulse assessment is the correct method to determine lower limb arterial function.

Question 26 of 50
ECG changes in Digoxin toxicity are
A. Prolonged QT interval
B. Prolonged PR interval ✓
C. ST segment elevation
D. Tall T-waves
💡
Explanation

Digoxin toxicity produces characteristic ECG changes due to its effects on cardiac conduction and repolarization. The classic findings include:

  • Prolonged PR interval due to slowed AV nodal conduction.

  • ST segment depression with a “scooped” or hockey stick appearance (known as digoxin effect).

  • Flattened or inverted T waves and shortened QT interval may also be seen.

Hence, among the given options, prolonged PR interval is the most characteristic ECG change of digoxin toxicity.

When performing Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR), the steps follow the CAB (Compressions-Airway-Breathing) sequence as per the American Heart Association (AHA) guidelines.

After ascertaining unresponsiveness, the first step is:
✅ Call for help (activate emergency response system) – This ensures that professional medical assistance is on the way.

Correct Sequence for CPR:

  1. Check responsiveness – Tap the person and shout.
  2. Call for help – Dial emergency services (e.g., 911 or 112) and get an Automated External Defibrillator (AED) if available.
  3. Check carotid pulse and breathing – Do this within 10 seconds.
  4. Start chest compressions if no pulse is found (30 compressions, then 2 breaths).
  5. Establish an airway and give rescue breaths.

Thus, calling for help is the immediate priority before starting CPR.
