Question 1 of 50
Which laboratory parameter is most reliable in assessing kidney function?
A. (eGFR) Estimated glomerular filtration rate ✓
B. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
C. Serum potassium
D. Serum sodium
💡
Explanation
eGFR provides an estimate of how well the kidneys are filtering blood by measuring the filtration rate of waste products, primarily creatinine clearance. It reflects the functional capacity of the kidneys more accurately than other parameters. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) can be influenced by factors like hydration and protein intake, while serum potassium and sodium levels indicate electrolyte balance but are not direct markers of kidney filtration function. Thus, eGFR is the preferred and most reliable indicator for assessing kidney function
Question 2 of 50
Which drug is contraindicated in renal failure due to risk of accumulation?
A. Metformin ✓
B. Insulin
C. Lisinopril
D. Furosemide
💡
Explanation
Metformin is contraindicated in renal failure because impaired kidney function reduces its clearance, leading to accumulation and increased risk of lactic acidosis, a potentially fatal condition. Insulin can be safely used in renal failure with dose adjustments. Lisinopril (an ACE inhibitor) and furosemide (a diuretic) require caution and dose adjustment but are not absolutely contraindicated; they may even provide renal protective effects or symptom relief when monitored carefully. Therefore, metformin is specifically contraindicated due to the high risk of accumulation and toxicity.
Question 3 of 50
Which urine finding is typical in acute tubular necrosis?
A. Muddy brown granular casts ✓
B. Hyaline casts
C. WBC casts
D. RBC casts
💡
Explanation
Muddy brown granular casts in urine are characteristic of acute tubular necrosis (ATN). They represent sloughed-off tubular epithelial cells and cellular debris from damaged renal tubules. Hyaline casts are nonspecific and often seen in normal urine or dehydration. White blood cell (WBC) casts indicate inflammation or infection such as pyelonephritis, and red blood cell (RBC) casts are typically seen in glomerulonephritis or vasculitis. Thus, muddy brown granular casts are the typical urine finding in ATN.
Question 4 of 50
The treatment of choice for urinary retention due to benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is:
A. Alpha-1 blockers ✓
B. Antibiotics
C. Surgical removal of bladder stones
D. Beta blockers
💡
Explanation
Alpha-1 blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) are the treatment of choice for urinary retention caused by benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). They work by relaxing the smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck, improving urine flow and relieving symptoms. Antibiotics are not used unless infection is present. Surgical removal of bladder stones addresses stones but not BPH-induced retention. Beta blockers have no role in BPH management. Therefore, alpha-1 blockers are the first-line pharmacologic treatment for urinary retention due to BPH.
Question 5 of 50
Which test is essential to confirm the diagnosis of diabetic nephropathy?
A. Kidney biopsy
B. Urine microalbumin test ✓
C. Serum creatinine
D. Renal ultrasound
💡
Explanation
he urine microalbumin test is essential to confirm early diabetic nephropathy by detecting small amounts of albumin in the urine, which indicate early kidney damage before significant decline in kidney function occurs. Kidney biopsy is rarely needed unless the diagnosis is uncertain or atypical features are present. Serum creatinine and renal ultrasound assess kidney function and structure but cannot specifically diagnose early diabetic nephropathy. Hence, urine microalbumin is the key diagnostic test.
Question 6 of 50
Which of the following is the most common site of urinary tract infection in males?
A. Prostate
B. Bladder ✓
C. Kidney
D. Urethra
💡
Explanation
In males, while prostatitis (prostate infection) is common, the bladder (cystitis) is the most common site of urinary tract infection overall. Most UTIs begin in the lower urinary tract, primarily the bladder. Prostatitis is usually a complication or chronic form, whereas bladder infections are more frequent as the initial site. Kidneys (pyelonephritis) are involved in upper tract infections and less common as the primary site. Urethral infections (urethritis) are less common as typical UTIs. Thus, the bladder is the most common site of UTI in males.
Question 7 of 50
Which is the preferred long-term treatment for urge urinary incontinence?
A. Anticholinergic medications ✓
B. Surgical intervention
C. Alpha blockers
D. Beta blockers
💡
Explanation
Anticholinergic medications are the preferred long-term treatment for urge urinary incontinence because they reduce bladder muscle overactivity by blocking muscarinic receptors, thus decreasing urgency and frequency. Surgical interventions are generally reserved for refractory cases. Alpha blockers are mainly used for bladder outlet obstruction, and beta blockers have no significant role in managing urge incontinence. Therefore, anticholinergics are the first-line therapy for urge urinary incontinence.

Which condition is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in adults?
A. Membranous nephropathy
B. Minimal change disease
C. Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis
D. Diabetic nephropathy ✓
💡
Explanation

Diabetic nephropathy is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in adults, especially in developed countries. It results from long-standing diabetes mellitus causing damage to the glomerular basement membrane, leading to:

  • Massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day)

  • Hypoalbuminemia

  • Edema

  • Hyperlipidemia

Question 9 of 50
Which of the following is a common complication of polycystic kidney disease (PCKD)?
A. Cerebral aneurysm ✓
B. Hepatic fibrosis
C. Pancreatic cancer
D. Adrenal insufficiency
💡
Explanation
Cerebral (berry) aneurysms are a common and serious complication of polycystic kidney disease (PCKD). These aneurysms can lead to subarachnoid hemorrhage if they rupture. Hepatic cysts are also common in PCKD but hepatic fibrosis is less typical. Pancreatic cancer and adrenal insufficiency are not associated complications of PCKD. Therefore, cerebral aneurysm is the key complication to monitor in patients with PCKD.
Question 10 of 50
Which symptom is more suggestive of overflow incontinence rather than stress incontinence?
A. Leakage on coughing
B. Continuous dribbling with weak stream ✓
C. Urgency with frequent urination
D. Nocturia only
💡
Explanation
Overflow incontinence is characterized by continuous dribbling of urine and a weak urinary stream, often due to bladder overdistention and incomplete emptying. This typically occurs when the bladder is unable to empty fully, leading to leakage. Stress incontinence, on the other hand, is triggered by physical pressure (e.g., coughing, sneezing, or exercise) that puts stress on the bladder. Urgency with frequent urination and nocturia can occur in both conditions, but the hallmark of overflow incontinence is continuous dribbling with a weak stream, suggesting bladder retention or obstruction.
Question 11 of 50
Which of the following laboratory findings suggest acute tubular necrosis?
A. Urine sodium >40 mEq/L ✓
B. Urine sodium <20 mEq/L
C. Low fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa)
D. High urine specific gravity (>1.030)
💡
Explanation
In acute tubular necrosis (ATN), damaged tubular cells lose their ability to reabsorb sodium, leading to increased sodium excretion in urine. Thus, urine sodium is typically elevated (>40 mEq/L). In contrast, prerenal acute kidney injury shows low urine sodium (<20 mEq/L) due to intact tubular function conserving sodium. Fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa) is high (>2%) in ATN, not low. Urine specific gravity is usually low or fixed (~1.010) in ATN due to impaired concentrating ability, not high. Therefore, urine sodium >40 mEq/L is indicative of ATN.
Question 12 of 50
In which stage of chronic kidney disease is dialysis usually initiated?
A. Stage 1 (eGFR >90)
B. Stage 2 (eGFR 60-89)
C. Stage 4-5 (eGFR <30) ✓
D. Stage 3 (eGFR 30-59)
💡
Explanation
Dialysis is typically initiated in chronic kidney disease (CKD) during Stage 4 or Stage 5 when the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) falls below 30 mL/min/1.73 m². This reflects severe kidney impairment with symptoms of uremia or complications such as fluid overload, electrolyte imbalances, or refractory acidosis. Earlier stages (1-3) generally do not require dialysis and are managed with conservative treatment and monitoring. Therefore, dialysis is usually started in Stage 4-5 CKD.
Question 13 of 50
The classic triad of symptoms in renal transplant rejection includes:
A. Fever, graft tenderness, and rise in serum creatinine ✓
B. Hypertension, edema, and hematuria
C. Dysuria, frequency, and urgency
D. Flank pain, nausea, and vomiting
💡
Explanation
The classic triad of acute renal transplant rejection includes fever, tenderness over the graft site, and an increase in serum creatinine indicating worsening kidney function. These symptoms reflect the immune-mediated injury to the transplanted kidney. Hypertension, edema, and hematuria are common in various kidney diseases but not specific to rejection. Dysuria, frequency, and urgency suggest urinary tract infection, while flank pain, nausea, and vomiting are nonspecific systemic symptoms. Therefore, the triad of fever, graft tenderness, and rising creatinine is most characteristic of transplant rejection.
Question 14 of 50
Which is the initial imaging test to evaluate suspected hydronephrosis
A. Ultrasound kidney and bladder ✓
B. Intravenous pyelogram
C. CT scan with contrast
D. MRI
💡
Explanation
Ultrasound of the kidney and bladder is the initial imaging test of choice for suspected hydronephrosis because it is non-invasive, readily available, does not involve radiation, and can effectively detect dilatation of the renal pelvis and calyces. Intravenous pyelogram and CT scan with contrast provide more detailed anatomical information but are usually reserved for further evaluation. MRI is less commonly used initially due to cost and availability. Hence, ultrasound is the preferred first-line imaging.
Question 15 of 50
Which condition is a contraindication for renal transplantation?
A. Controlled diabetes mellitus
B. Active malignancy ✓
C. Hypertension controlled with medications
D. Previous kidney stones
💡
Explanation
Active malignancy is a contraindication for renal transplantation because immunosuppressive therapy post-transplant can exacerbate cancer progression or recurrence. Controlled diabetes mellitus and hypertension, when well managed, are not contraindications. Previous kidney stones are generally not a contraindication but may require evaluation and management before transplantation. Therefore, active malignancy must be ruled out or treated before considering renal transplantation.

The most common cause of acute interstitial nephritis is:
A. Drug hypersensitivity ✓
B. Bacterial infection
C. Viral infection

💡
Explanation

Acute interstitial nephritis (AIN) is most commonly caused by a hypersensitivity reaction to drugs.   • Option 1 (Drug hypersensitivity): This is the leading cause of AIN, accounting for the majority of cases. The immune response to the drug or its metabolites causes infiltration of inflammatory cells in the interstitium.

Question 17 of 50

Question 18 of 50
Which of the following drugs is most nephrotoxic?
A. Gentamicin ✓
B. Penicillin
C. Azithromycin
D. Ciprofloxacin
💡
Explanation

Gentamicin, an aminoglycoside antibiotic, is well known for its nephrotoxic potential. It accumulates in the renal proximal tubular cells, causing direct tubular damage leading to acute tubular necrosis. This nephrotoxicity can manifest as elevated serum creatinine, decreased glomerular filtration rate, and electrolyte imbalances.

Other drugs listed—penicillin, azithromycin, and ciprofloxacin—have much lower nephrotoxic risk profiles. Penicillin rarely causes nephrotoxicity; azithromycin and ciprofloxacin are generally safe for the kidneys but can cause other side effects.

Question 19 of 50
The main cause of death in patients with end-stage renal disease is:
A. Infection
B. Cardiovascular disease ✓
C. Hyperkalemia
D. Metabolic alkalosis
💡
Explanation
Patients with end-stage renal disease (ESRD) have a significantly increased risk of cardiovascular disease due to factors like hypertension, accelerated atherosclerosis, fluid overload, and electrolyte imbalances. Cardiovascular disease, including heart failure, coronary artery disease, and sudden cardiac death, is the leading cause of mortality in ESRD patients.
Question 20 of 50
Which of the following findings is expected in prerenal acute kidney injury?
A. Fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa) <1% ✓
B. Fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa) >2%
C. Muddy brown casts in urine
D. Granular casts in urine
💡
Explanation

In prerenal acute kidney injury (AKI), the kidney responds to decreased perfusion by conserving sodium to maintain intravascular volume, resulting in a low fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa <1%).

  • FeNa >2% suggests intrinsic renal damage like acute tubular necrosis (ATN).

  • Muddy brown casts and granular casts in urine are typical of ATN (intrinsic AKI), not prerenal AKI.

Question 21 of 50
Which test is used to confirm vesicoureteral reflux in children with recurrent UTIs?
A. Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG) ✓
B. Renal ultrasound
C. Intravenous pyelogram (IVP)
D. Urodynamic study
💡
Explanation

Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG) is the gold standard test to diagnose and confirm vesicoureteral reflux (VUR). It involves filling the bladder with contrast through a catheter and taking X-ray images during voiding to visualize any backward flow of urine from the bladder into the ureters and kidneys.
-Renal ultrasound can detect hydronephrosis but cannot confirm VUR.
-Intravenous pyelogram (IVP) is less sensitive and not routinely used for VUR diagnosis.
-Urodynamic studies assess bladder function but do not diagnose reflux.

Question 22 of 50
Which urinary test is most useful to screen for bladder cancer in patients with hematuria?
A. Urine cytology ✓
B. Urine culture
C. Urine dipstick for blood
D. Urinary protein estimation
💡
Explanation

Urine cytology is the most useful non-invasive screening test for bladder cancer in patients presenting with hematuria. It detects malignant urothelial cells shed into the urine, especially in high-grade tumors.

  • Urine culture is used to detect infections, not cancer.

  • Urine dipstick for blood detects the presence of blood but cannot differentiate causes such as cancer.

  • Urinary protein estimation assesses proteinuria, which is unrelated to bladder cancer diagnosis.

💡
Explanation

Question 25 of 50
Which immunosuppressive agent used in renal transplantation inhibits T-cell activation by blocking the calcineurin pathway?
A. Azathioprine
B. Cyclosporine ✓
C. Prednisone
D. Mycophenolate mofetil
💡
Explanation

Cyclosporine is a calcineurin inhibitor that prevents T-cell activation by blocking the calcineurin pathway. It inhibits the phosphatase activity of calcineurin, thereby preventing the dephosphorylation and activation of nuclear factor of activated T-cells (NFAT). This leads to reduced transcription of interleukin-2 (IL-2) and other cytokines critical for T-cell proliferation and activation, making it effective in preventing transplant rejection.

  • Azathioprine is an antimetabolite that inhibits DNA synthesis, affecting proliferating cells.

  • Prednisone is a corticosteroid that suppresses inflammation and immune responses broadly.

  • Mycophenolate mofetil inhibits purine synthesis in lymphocytes but does not block the calcineurin pathway.

Question 26 of 50
Which of the following urinary incontinence types is characterized by involuntary leakage with increased intra-abdominal pressure (coughing, sneezing)?
A. Urge incontinence
B. Stress incontinence ✓
C. Overflow incontinence
D. Functional incontinence
💡
Explanation

Stress urinary incontinence is defined by involuntary leakage of urine during activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure, such as coughing, sneezing, laughing, or physical exertion. This occurs due to weakness or dysfunction of the pelvic floor muscles or urethral sphincter, leading to inability to maintain closure under stress.
-Urge incontinence involves a sudden, intense urge to urinate followed by involuntary leakage.
-Overflow incontinence occurs when the bladder is overfilled and leaks due to incomplete emptying.
-Functional incontinence is caused by physical or cognitive impairments preventing timely toileting, not due to urinary tract dysfunction.

A. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
B. Neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin (NGAL) ✓
C. C-reactive protein (CRP)
D. Serum albumin
💡
Explanation

NGAL is a biomarker that rises rapidly in the blood and urine following acute kidney injury (AKI), often within a few hours of injury. It is considered more sensitive and earlier than serum creatinine, which usually rises 24-48 hours after kidney damage.

  • Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) is less specific and slower to change.

  • C-reactive protein (CRP) is a marker of inflammation, not kidney injury specifically.

  • Serum albumin reflects nutritional status, not acute kidney injury.

C. Microalbuminuria ✓
D. Hypokalemia
💡
Explanation

  • Hypokalemia is not typical in CKD; hyperkalemia is more common.

Question 32 of 50
In renal transplant patients, which immunosuppressive drug is commonly associated with nephrotoxicity?
A. Tacrolimus ✓
B. Mycophenolate mofetil
C. Azathioprine
D. Prednisone
💡
Explanation

Tacrolimus is a calcineurin inhibitor widely used as an immunosuppressant in renal transplant patients. It is commonly associated with nephrotoxicity due to vasoconstriction of afferent arterioles, leading to reduced renal blood flow and potential chronic interstitial fibrosis if used long-term.

  • Mycophenolate mofetil primarily causes gastrointestinal and hematologic side effects but is less nephrotoxic.

  • Azathioprine is less nephrotoxic and mainly causes bone marrow suppression.

  • Prednisone has broad immunosuppressive effects but does not directly cause nephrotoxicity.

  • BUN is influenced by many factors and is not a reliable prognostic marker.

D. Urethral stricture
💡
Explanation

Question 47 of 50
Which of the following is the most accurate test for evaluating glomerular filtration rate (GFR) in clinical practice?
A. Serum creatinine
B. Creatinine clearance ✓
C. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
D. Urinalysis
💡
Explanation

Creatinine clearance, measured either by 24-hour urine collection or estimated by formulas, is the most accurate test for evaluating glomerular filtration rate (GFR) in clinical practice. It accounts for the amount of creatinine cleared from the blood by the kidneys, providing a direct estimate of kidney filtration function.

  • Serum creatinine alone is influenced by muscle mass and is less precise for GFR estimation.

  • Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) is affected by factors like hydration and protein intake, making it less reliable.

  • Urinalysis assesses urine characteristics but does not measure GFR.
