Question 1 of 50
A 27-year-old female without past medical history presents with 2 days of pain in her right ear. The patient notes that the symptoms started with an itchy ear which progressed to pain, discharge, and hearing loss. Examination reveals an afebrile patient who is nontoxic, with moderate tenderness on manipulation of the auricle, erythema and edema of the tympanic canal, and no external rash. Cranial nerve examination is normal. Which of the following will be most helpful in treating this condition?
A. Acetic acid otic washes ✓
B. Tympanostomy tubes
C. Systemic antivirals
D. Systemic antivirals
💡
Explanation
The patient has otitis externa, inflammation of the external ear, and tympanic canal almost always because of infection. Trauma and excessive moisture are commonly implicated in the development of the condition. Patients generally present with otalgia and otorrhea. Debridement of the external ear canal is the most important aspect of treatment. In nontoxic patients, this is achieved with topical acetic acid washes with or without topical antibiotics and steroids. A cotton or methylcellulose wick for drainage of the tympanic canal may be placed 1 cm deep in the ear and stays for 2 days. Systemically ill patients or diabetic patients require systemic antibiotics and sometimes admission. Antihistamines can be used for symptomatic relief but do not affect the duration of illness. Tympanostomy tubes and adenoidectomy may be indicated for prevention of chronic/recurrent otitis media but have no effect on acute management of otitis externa. Herpes zoster can occur in the ear and is referred to as Ramsay Hunt syndrome. This condition requires antivirals and is associated with a vesicular rash and sometimes cranial nerve palsies.

The tympanic membrane vibrates in response to sound waves, transmitting these vibrations to the ossicles (malleus, incus, and stapes) in the middle ear, which in turn send the signals to the inner ear for processing.

Question 23 of 50
Which of the following cells are primarily responsible for mucus secretion in the nasal cavity?
A. Goblet cells ✓
B. Basal cells
C. Olfactory receptor cells
D. Ciliated epithelial cells
💡
Explanation

• Goblet cells are specialized epithelial cells found throughout the respiratory tract, including the nasal mucosa.

• They secrete mucus, which traps dust, pathogens, and other particles, helping to humidify and protect the respiratory tract.

• Ciliated epithelial cells move the mucus toward the pharynx for swallowing but do not secrete it.

• Basal cells are stem cells that regenerate the epithelium.

• Olfactory receptor cells are involved in smell, not mucus production

Question 24 of 50
The sense of smell (olfaction) is detected by receptors located in the:
A. Middle concha
B. Nasal septum
C. Olfactory receptor ✓
D. Nasolacrimal duct
💡
Explanation

The olfactory receptors are specialized sensory neurons located in the olfactory epithelium, which lines the roof of the nasal cavity, including the superior nasal concha and a part of the nasal septum.

• Olfactory receptor neurons detect odor molecules and send signals via the olfactory nerve (CN I) to the brain.

• Middle concha is not involved in olfaction.

• Nasal septum contains part of the olfactory region, but the correct structure detecting smell is the olfactory receptor.

• Nasolacrimal duct drains tears from the eyes into the nasal cavity; it has no role in smell.

Question 25 of 50
What is the medical procedure called when a long needle is used to puncture the middle ear to aspirate its contents?
A. Tympanoplasty
B. Myringotomy
C. Myringopuncture ✓
D. Paracentesis
💡
Explanation
Myringopuncture is a procedure where a fine, long needle is used to puncture the tympanic membrane (eardrum) to aspirate fluid or pus from the middle ear cavity, usually in cases of otitis media with effusion or acute middle ear infections.
• Myringotomy is a surgical incision into the eardrum, often to insert a tube, but not done with a needle.
• Tympanoplasty is a surgical repair of the eardrum or ossicles.
• Paracentesis typically refers to fluid aspiration from the peritoneal cavity, not the ear.

Question 27 of 50
Which of the following is NOT a typical complication of otitis media?
A. Hearing loss
B. Mastoiditis
C. Throat infection ✓
D. Cholesteatoma
💡
Explanation
Otitis media, an infection of the middle ear, can lead to several complications, including hearing loss (due to fluid buildup in the middle ear), mastoiditis (infection of the bone behind the ear), and cholesteatoma (abnormal skin growth in the middle ear). However, throat infections are not typically considered a direct complication of otitis media. While the ears and throat are both part of the upper respiratory system, throat infections are more commonly associated with conditions like pharyngitis or tonsillitis, rather than otitis media.

    Correct. Antibiotics are the first-line treatment for bacterial sinusitis, especially when symptoms last longer than 10 days or worsen after an initial improvement.   • Option 2: Intranasal corticosteroids
    While intranasal corticosteroids are effective for managing chronic sinusitis or allergic rhinitis, they are not the first choice for treating bacterial sinus infections. They may be used in cases of inflammatory sinusitis, but they would not address the bacterial infection directly.
