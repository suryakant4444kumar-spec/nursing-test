Question 2 of 50
A patient was brought to the emergency department after a head injury. The nurse noticed that there is a clear drainage from the nose. Which of the following test is useful and most reliable to determine if the drainage is a cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)?
A. pH test
B. Test for Beta2-transferrin ✓
C. Test for glucose
D. Test for chlorides
💡
Explanation
–The CSF contains a large amount of glucose. A rapid but highly unreliable test is glucose-content determination with the use of glucose oxidase paper. This method of detecting cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) in rhinorrhea is no longer reliable screening or confirmatory test. Due to the presence of reducing substance in the lacrimal-gland secretions, it can lead to false positive results. Test for beta 2-transferrin is a most reliable test to determine whether the nasal drainage is CSF because the substance is only present in CSF.

Question 5 of 50
Which of the following patients with a febrile seizure most likely requires further evaluation with a lumbar puncture (LP) to look for an infectious source?
A. A fully vaccinated 10-month-old
B. An 18-month-old with a recent “cold”
C. A 9-month-old currently receiving antibiotics for otitis media ✓
D. A 4-year-old with a history of epilepsy
💡
Explanation
The optimal criteria used to determine which patients require an LP after a first febrile seizure are constantly evolving and remain unclear. However, the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) released new, less aggressive guidelines in 2011. According to its most recent guidelines, the AAP states that an LP should be considered for all infants aged 6 to 12 months who are inadequately vaccinated against H. influenzae or Streptococcus pneumoniae or in whom the vaccination status is not known. In addition, an LP should be considered for any infant receiving current antibiotic therapy since the use of antibiotics may mask meningeal signs and symptoms. Other higher risk groups include patients with focal or prolonged seizures, abnormal physical examinations, or toxic appearance. Regardless of vaccination status, older patients are able to participate in a complete physical examination to aid the physician in determining whether meningitis is a consideration. Most ordinary febrile seizures are treated symptomatically with antipyretics. Approximately one-third of patients with febrile seizures will have at least one more febrile seizure episode. Risk factors for this include young age at first seizure, lower temperature with the seizure episode, a first-degree relative with febrile seizure, and short duration between fever onset and seizure event. Patients with febrile seizures are twice as likely to develop epilepsy compared to the general population.

Question 8 of 50
The nerves which take impulses away from the brain towards affected organ is known as
A. Sensory nerves
B. Neurones
C. Mixed nerves
D. Motor nerves ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Motor nerves are the nerves that carry impulses away from the brain and spinal cord to the muscles and organs, causing movement or function in those organs.

  • Sensory nerves (Option 1) carry sensory information toward the brain from the body, such as touch, temperature, and pain.

  • Neurones (Option 2) are the individual cells that make up the nervous system and transmit nerve impulses, not a specific category of nerves.

  • Mixed nerves (Option 3) contain both sensory and motor fibers and can carry both types of impulses in different directions.

Question 12 of 50
Position given to a patient with multiple injury over the face and neck is .................
A. Supine position
B. Semiprone position ✓
C. Side lying
D. Trendelenburg
💡
Explanation

  • Option 2 (Semiprone position) is correct. For a patient with multiple injuries to the face and neck, the semiprone (also known as the recovery or lateral recumbent) position is often recommended. This position helps maintain airway patency by preventing the tongue from obstructing the airway and allows drainage of secretions (e.g., blood or vomitus) from the mouth, reducing the risk of aspiration. It is particularly useful when cervical spine injury has been ruled out or is being managed with immobilization.

  • Option 1 (Supine position) is incorrect. Lying supine (on the back) may compromise the airway in patients with facial or neck injuries due to swelling, bleeding, or secretions, increasing the risk of obstruction or aspiration.

  • Option 3 (Side lying) is incorrect. While side lying can be beneficial for airway management, the semiprone position is specifically preferred in trauma settings to optimize drainage and airway protection without exacerbating potential spinal injuries.

  • Option 4 (Trendelenburg position) is incorrect. The Trendelenburg position (head down) is contraindicated in head, facial, or neck injuries as it can increase intracranial pressure and worsen edema or bleeding.

Question 13 of 50
Effectiveness of levodopa in a myasthenia gravis client will be evaluated through.........
A. No ptosis
B. Fatigue improving at the end of the day
C. Reduction in short-term memory
D. Lessened rigidity and tremor ✓
💡
Explanation
Levodopa is primarily used in the treatment of Parkinson’s disease, where it helps to reduce rigidity and tremors. In the case of Myasthenia Gravis (MG), the effectiveness of treatment is typically monitored by looking for signs of improvement in muscle strength and fatigue, rather than directly assessing symptoms like rigidity and tremor. Therefore, levodopa's effect would likely be observed through the reduction of rigidity and tremor, which is more characteristic of Parkinson’s disease rather than MG. However, it is important to note that levodopa is not commonly used to treat MG, and acetylcholinesterase inhibitors are more commonly prescribed for MG.

Question 16 of 50
. A 35-year-old female presents in a coma (Glasgow Coma Scale 3) after a motor vehicle crash and is intubated for airway protection. Further evaluation reveals no life-threatening chest, abdomen, or pelvic injuries. Vital signs are normal. A computed tomography (CT) scan of the head is normal. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Epidural hematoma
B. Subdural hematoma
C. Diffuse axonal injury (DAI) ✓
D. Cerebral contusion
💡
Explanation
DAI is an important traumatic cause of coma that is not due to a mass lesion or frank intracerebral hemorrhage. Initial CT scan is almost always normal in patients with DAI, but MRI may show diffuse white matter disruption due to axonal fiber injury. Because of the difficulty in gauging DAI on neuroimaging, the prognosis is based totally on clinical parameters. The duration of coma obviously correlates with severity of injury. Patients with DAI who awaken from coma within 24 hours may have few permanent disabilities. Those in coma for longer than 24 hours tend to have much more grim outcomes, including persistent vegetative state or extreme cognitive dysfunction. Most types of acute intracranial hemorrhage severe enough to lead to coma would be evident on initial CT scan, including epidural or subdural hematomas, cerebral contusions, and intraparenchymal hematoma. An important exception is subarachnoid hemorrhage, which may not be visible on CT scan and is a common hemorrhagic cause of altered mental status after trauma.

C. Aneurysm
D. Blunt trauma
💡
Explanation

  • Embolism and aneurysm are also causes of strokes, but hypertension remains the most common and significant risk factor.

  • Blunt trauma is generally not a leading cause of strokes, though it can result in brain injury.

Question 21 of 50
What does the nurse understand that clients with myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barre syndrome, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) share in common?
A. Progressive deterioration until death
B. Deficiencies of essential neurotransmitters
C. Increased risk for respiratory complications ✓
D. Involuntary twitching of small muscle groups
💡
Explanation

All three conditions—myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barré syndrome, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)—involve neurological impairments that can affect muscle function and result in respiratory complications.

Myasthenia Gravis: A chronic autoimmune disorder that affects the neuromuscular junction, leading to weakness in voluntary muscles, including the muscles used for breathing. This can result in respiratory difficulties if the diaphragm or respiratory muscles are affected.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome: An acute inflammatory disorder where the immune system attacks the peripheral nerves, leading to progressive weakness that can involve the respiratory muscles, which may result in respiratory failure if not promptly treated.

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS): A progressive neurodegenerative disease that affects the motor neurons, leading to muscle weakness and atrophy, including the muscles necessary for breathing. Respiratory failure is a common cause of death in ALS patients.
