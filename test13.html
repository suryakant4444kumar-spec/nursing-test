Question 1 of 50
Which symptom is most commonly associated with renal stones?
A. Hematuria ✓
B. Polyuria
C. Dysuria
D. Anuria
💡
Explanation
Hematuria, or blood in the urine, is the most common symptom associated with renal stones. Stones irritate and damage the lining of the urinary tract as they move, causing bleeding. This bleeding leads to hematuria, which can be visible (gross) or detected only under a microscope (microscopic). Hence, hematuria is the key clinical sign indicating the presence of renal stones..
Question 2 of 50
Which of the following is the most common cause of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)?
A. Infection
B. Hormonal imbalance with increased dihydrotestosterone ✓
C. Cancer
D. Autoimmune disease
💡
Explanation
Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is primarily caused by hormonal changes, particularly involving dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a potent androgen derived from testosterone. DHT binds to androgen receptors in the prostate and stimulates prostate cell growth, leading to enlargement of the prostate gland, which causes BPH. This hormonal imbalance is the main factor driving the hyperplastic process in aging men.
Question 3 of 50
The most common causative organism of pyelonephritis is:
A. Staphylococcus aureus
B. Escherichia coli ✓
C. Streptococcus pyogenes
D. Pseudomonas aeruginosa
💡
Explanation
Escherichia coli (E. coli) is the most common causative organism of pyelonephritis, an infection of the kidney and renal pelvis. It originates from the normal intestinal flora and typically ascends from the lower urinary tract (bladder) to the kidneys, causing infection. E. coli possesses several virulence factors that help it adhere to the urinary tract lining, resist host defenses, and cause inflammation
Question 4 of 50
A patient with nephrotic syndrome is likely to have which of the following complications?
A. Hypertension
B. Hypercoagulability ✓
C. Polycythemia
D. Hypoglycemia
💡
Explanation
Patients with nephrotic syndrome are prone to a state of hypercoagulability due to the loss of anticoagulant proteins (like antithrombin III, protein C, and protein S) in the urine along with excessive protein loss. This results in increased risk of thrombosis (blood clots), which is a well-known and serious complication of nephrotic syndrome.
Question 5 of 50
Which test is most diagnostic for nephrotic syndrome?
A. Blood urea nitrogen
B. 24-hour urinary protein excretion ✓
C. Serum creatinine
D. Urine culture
💡
Explanation
The hallmark of nephrotic syndrome is heavy proteinuria (excess protein loss in urine). The 24-hour urinary protein excretion test quantitatively measures the amount of protein lost in urine over a full day and is the most diagnostic test to confirm nephrotic syndrome. A protein loss exceeding 3.5 grams per 24 hours is diagnostic of nephrotic syndrome.

Question 17 of 50
Which part of the nephron is primarily responsible for the secretion of hydrogen ions ?
A.

Proximal convoluted tubule
B.

Loop of Henle
C.

Collecting duct
D. Distal convoluted tubule ✓
💡
Explanation

The distal convoluted tubule (DCT) plays a key role in the secretion of hydrogen ions (H⁺). This process helps regulate the acid-base balance in the body by lowering the blood's acidity, in addition to reabsorbing sodium and other ions.

  • Proximal convoluted tubule (Option 1): Primarily involved in reabsorption of water, glucose, and ions, but not in significant hydrogen ion secretion.

  • Loop of Henle (Option 2): Primarily responsible for creating a concentration gradient in the renal medulla and reabsorbing water and sodium, but not for hydrogen ion secretion.

  • Collecting duct (Option 3): Also involved in hydrogen ion secretion, but the distal convoluted tubule is the primary site for this process.

Question 24 of 50
Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by which of the following
A. Proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia ✓
B. Hematuria and hypertension
C. Oliguria and azotemia
D. Fever and flank pain
💡
Explanation
Nephrotic syndrome is a kidney disorder defined by a specific set of clinical features:
• Proteinuria: Excessive loss of protein in the urine (more than 3.5 grams per day), due to increased permeability of the glomerular basement membrane.
• Hypoalbuminemia: Low blood albumin levels occur because large amounts of albumin are lost in urine.
• Edema: Due to hypoalbuminemia, plasma oncotic pressure decreases, leading to fluid leaking into tissues and causing swelling, especially in the legs and around the eyes.
• Hyperlipidemia: The liver compensates for low albumin by increasing lipoprotein synthesis, causing elevated blood lipids
Question 25 of 50
Which urine finding is typical in acute tubular necrosis?
A. Muddy brown granular casts ✓
B. Hyaline casts
C. WBC casts
D. RBC casts
💡
Explanation
Muddy brown granular casts in urine are characteristic of acute tubular necrosis (ATN). They represent sloughed-off tubular epithelial cells and cellular debris from damaged renal tubules. Hyaline casts are nonspecific and often seen in normal urine or dehydration. White blood cell (WBC) casts indicate inflammation or infection such as pyelonephritis, and red blood cell (RBC) casts are typically seen in glomerulonephritis or vasculitis. Thus, muddy brown granular casts are the typical urine finding in ATN.
Question 26 of 50
The treatment of choice for urinary retention due to benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is:
A. Alpha-1 blockers ✓
B. Antibiotics
C. Surgical removal of bladder stones
D. Beta blockers
💡
Explanation
Alpha-1 blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) are the treatment of choice for urinary retention caused by benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). They work by relaxing the smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck, improving urine flow and relieving symptoms. Antibiotics are not used unless infection is present. Surgical removal of bladder stones addresses stones but not BPH-induced retention. Beta blockers have no role in BPH management. Therefore, alpha-1 blockers are the first-line pharmacologic treatment for urinary retention due to BPH.

💡
Explanation

Question 30 of 50

A. Fever, graft tenderness, and rise in serum creatinine ✓
B. Hypertension, edema, and hematuria
C. Dysuria, frequency, and urgency
D. Flank pain, nausea, and vomiting
💡
Explanation
The classic triad of acute renal transplant rejection includes fever, tenderness over the graft site, and an increase in serum creatinine indicating worsening kidney function. These symptoms reflect the immune-mediated injury to the transplanted kidney. Hypertension, edema, and hematuria are common in various kidney diseases but not specific to rejection. Dysuria, frequency, and urgency suggest urinary tract infection, while flank pain, nausea, and vomiting are nonspecific systemic symptoms. Therefore, the triad of fever, graft tenderness, and rising creatinine is most characteristic of transplant rejection.
