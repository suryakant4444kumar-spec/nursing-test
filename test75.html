Question 5 of 50
Census population count is in reference to
A. 1st march ✓
B. 1st July
C. 30th June
D. 1st January
💡
Explanation
  • The census population count is in reference to Option 1: 1st March.
  • In India, the census is conducted with a specific "reference moment" to ensure an accurate snapshot of the population at a single point in time. For the majority of the country, this is 00:00 hours on 1st March of the census year.
  • Key Facts about Census Reference Dates
  • National Reference Date: The standard reference date for the decennial census (including the upcoming 2027 Census) is 1st March.
  • Himalayan/Snow-bound Regions: In specific regions such as Ladakh, Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand, a different reference date of 1st October is used due to weather-related accessibility issues.
  • Enumeration Period: While the data refers to 1st March, the actual population enumeration phase typically takes place in February, followed by a "revisional round" in the first week of March to update the count to the reference moment.
  • Legal Framework: These dates are formally notified by the Government of India under the Census Act, 1948

Question 16 of 50
Most universally accepted indicator of health status of whole population and their socioeconomic conditions among the following is:
A. MMR
B. IMR ✓
C. Life expectancy
D. Disease notification rates
💡
Explanation

The most universally accepted indicator of health status and socioeconomic conditions for a whole population is IMR (Infant Mortality Rate).

While all the options provided are important health metrics, IMR stands out as the "gold standard" for several specific reasons:

Why IMR is the Leading Indicator:-

  • Sensitivity: Infants are the most vulnerable segment of a population. IMR reacts quickly to changes in environmental conditions, nutrition, and sanitation.
  • Socioeconomic Mirror: It reflects the availability and quality of primary health care, the educational status of mothers, and the overall economic well-being of a community.
  • Ease of Comparison: It is a standardized metric that allows for easy comparison between different countries or regions.

Analysis of Other Options:-

  • MMR (Maternal Mortality Ratio): While a vital indicator of reproductive health services and the status of women, it focuses on a specific subgroup rather than the population as a whole.
  • Life Expectancy: This is an excellent long-term indicator of health and longevity, but it is often considered a result of health improvements rather than a sensitive, real-time indicator of current socioeconomic shifts.
  • Disease Notification Rates: These are highly dependent on the quality of a country's surveillance system and may not capture the health status of those who do not seek formal medical care.

Question 20 of 50
Disease elimination is helped by:
A. Herd immunity ✓
B. Isolation
C. Quarantine
D. None
💡
Explanation

Disease elimination is primarily helped by herd immunity. This concept refers to a state where a significant portion of a population becomes immune to an infectious disease, thereby reducing the likelihood of disease spread and protecting those who are not immune.​

While other methods contribute to control, herd immunity is the critical mechanism for elimination:

  • Herd Immunity (Option 1): Breaks the chain of transmission. When enough people are immune (usually through vaccination or prior infection), the virus or bacteria cannot find new hosts easily, leading to the disease fading out in that specific population.​

  • Isolation (Option 2): Involves separating people who are already sick with a contagious disease from those who are not sick. While it prevents spread, it focuses on managing active cases rather than broad population-level elimination.​

  • Quarantine (Option 3): Separates and restricts the movement of people who were exposed to a contagious disease to see if they become sick. Like isolation, it is a containment measure rather than an elimination strategy on its own

Question 21 of 50
First in sequence:
A. Impairment
B. Disease ✓
C. Disability
D. Rehabilitation
💡
Explanation

The question refers to the sequential model of the disabling process, famously conceptualized by the WHO (ICIDH model) and Nagi. The standard progression typically follows this order:

  1. Disease (Pathology): The intrinsic pathology or disorder (e.g., a viral infection or trauma).​

  2. Impairment: A loss or abnormality of physiological, psychological, or anatomical structure or function resulting from the disease (e.g., paralysis of a limb).​

  3. Disability: A restriction or lack of ability to perform an activity in the manner considered normal for a human being (e.g., inability to walk).​

  4. Handicap (or Participation Restriction in newer models): A disadvantage for a given individual that limits or prevents the fulfillment of a role (e.g., inability to work at a job requiring walking)

Question 31 of 50
One of the following is a universally accepted indicator of health status and socio economic condition of a country:
A. Maternal mortality rate
B. Life expectancy ✓
C. Under 5 proportionate rate
D. Infant mortality rate
💡
Explanation
  • The correct option is Option 4: Infant mortality rate.

    The Infant Mortality Rate (IMR) is considered the most sensitive and universally accepted indicator of a country's health status and socio-economic condition [6, 14, 40]. It measures the number of deaths of infants under one year of age per 1,000 live births in a given year [6, 19].
    It is a uniquely powerful proxy for a nation's well-being because:
    Sensitivity: It reacts quickly to changes in environmental conditions, sanitation, and nutrition [14, 21].
    Comprehensive Reach: It reflects not just the health of children, but the overall effectiveness of the healthcare system, maternal health services, and public health infrastructure [6, 15, 27].
    Socio-economic Proxy: High IMR typically correlates with poverty, lower education levels (particularly among women), and lack of access to clean water and sanitation [6, 21].
    Why other options are incorrect

  • Option 1: Maternal mortality rate (MMR): While a critical indicator of maternal health and the quality of obstetric care, it is narrower in scope than IMR [6, 37]. It specifically reflects the risks associated with pregnancy and childbirth rather than the general socio-economic condition of the entire population [32].
    Option 2: Life expectancy: This is an important indicator of longevity and is a key component of the Human Development Index (HDI) [11, 39]. However, it is a broad statistical average that does not always capture the immediate environmental and socio-economic sensitivities as accurately or quickly as IMR does [14, 37].

    Option 4: Adult mortality rate — This focuses on the probability of dying within a specific age range (e.g., 15–60 years). While useful for assessing adult health risks, it does not capture the critical vulnerabilities of the youngest population segments that are essential for evaluating overall societal health.
