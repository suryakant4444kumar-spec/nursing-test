Question 10 of 50
Hypothermia is defined as a core body temperature below:
A. 35°C (95°F) ✓
B. 36°C (96.8°F)
C. 37°C (98.6°F)
D. 38°C (100.4°F)
💡
Explanation

Hypothermia is a medical emergency that occurs when the body loses heat faster than it can produce it, causing the core body temperature to drop below 35°C (95°F).
At this temperature, normal body functions begin to fail, particularly in the cardiovascular and nervous systems.
Symptoms can include shivering, confusion, slurred speech, and slow breathing.
If untreated, it can lead to coma and death.
Question 11 of 50
A nurse is assessing a newborn’s temperature. Which site is most appropriate?
A. Oral
B. Rectal
C. Axillary ✓
D. Tympanic
💡
Explanation

In newborns, the axillary (underarm) route is the most appropriate and safest method for temperature assessment.

  • Oral temperature is not suitable because newborns cannot hold a thermometer properly and there is a risk of injury.

  • Rectal temperature may give an accurate core reading but carries a risk of rectal perforation and vagal stimulation, so it is generally avoided in routine newborn assessment.

  • Tympanic temperature is unreliable in newborns because the ear canal is too small.

Therefore, axillary temperature measurement is recommended as it is safe, non-invasive, and sufficiently accurate for newborns.

Question 12 of 50
Which hormone plays a key role in regulating body temperature?
A. Insulin
B. Thyroxine ✓
C. Estrogen
D. Cortisol
💡
Explanation

Thyroxine (T4), a hormone secreted by the thyroid gland, plays a crucial role in regulating body temperature by controlling the body's basal metabolic rate (BMR). It influences how much heat is produced by the body during normal metabolic processes.
Increased thyroxine levels raise metabolism, producing more body heat (e.g., in hyperthyroidism, patients often feel hot).
Decreased levels (as in hypothyroidism) result in cold intolerance and low body temperature.
Question 13 of 50
A patient has a fever of 39.5°C (103.1°F). What is the priority nursing intervention?
A. Provide warm blankets
B. Administer antipyretics as ordered ✓
C. Encourage hot fluids
D. Restrict fluid intake
💡
Explanation

A body temperature of 39.5°C (103.1°F) indicates a high-grade fever, which can increase metabolic demands and cause dehydration, discomfort, and even neurological complications if not managed.
The priority nursing intervention is to administer antipyretics (e.g., paracetamol or ibuprofen) as prescribed. These medications help reduce the fever, provide comfort, and prevent complications related to hyperthermia.
Warm blankets and hot fluids would worsen the fever.
Restricting fluids is contraindicated since fever increases fluid loss—patients should be encouraged to stay hydrated.
Question 14 of 50
The hypothalamus regulates body temperature by acting as a:
A. Thermostat ✓
B. Insulator
C. Coolant
D. Barrier
💡
Explanation

The hypothalamus, located in the brain, functions as the body’s thermoregulatory center—much like a thermostat. It detects changes in body temperature through thermoreceptors and triggers appropriate physiological responses:
If the body is too hot, it initiates sweating and vasodilation.
If the body is too cold, it triggers shivering and vasoconstriction.
This helps maintain homeostasis by keeping the internal temperature within a narrow, healthy range.
Insulator and coolant refer to physical properties or substances, not regulatory mechanisms.
A barrier blocks or separates, not regulates.

Explanation

Hyperpyrexia refers to an extremely high body temperature, typically ≥40°C (104°F), and is a medical emergency. It may be caused by:

High fever: 39.1–39.9°C (102.4–103.8°F)
Hyperpyrexia: ≥40°C (≥104°F)

Question 28 of 50
A BP reading of 150/95 mmHg would be classified as:
A. Hypotension
B. Prehypertension
C. Stage 1 hypertension ✓
D. Hypertensive crisis
💡
Explanation

A blood pressure of 150/95 mmHg is above the normal range.

According to standard classification:

  • Normal: <120/80 mmHg

  • Prehypertension / Elevated: 120–139 / 80–89 mmHg

  • Stage 1 Hypertension: 140–159 / 90–99 mmHg

  • Hypertensive Crisis: ≥180 / ≥120 mmHg

Since 150 systolic falls between 140–159 and 95 diastolic falls between 90–99, this reading is classified as Stage 1 Hypertension.

It is not hypotension (which is low BP), not prehypertension (which is lower range), and not hypertensive crisis (which is much higher values).

D. Tachycardia ✓
💡
Explanation

This results in several characteristic physiological changes, including an increase in heart rate (tachycardia), increased blood pressure (hypertension), pupillary dilation, diaphoresis (sweating), and an increased respiratory rate.

  • Correct Answer: Option 4
Question 36 of 50
The "auscultatory gap" refers to:
A. Loss of Korotkoff sounds during deflation ✓
B. Inability to palpate a radial pulse
C. Absence of diastolic sounds
D. False-low systolic readings
💡
Explanation

  • Auscultatory gap = a temporary disappearance of Korotkoff sounds when taking a blood pressure reading, typically occurring between systolic and diastolic pressures.

  • If unrecognized, it can lead to falsely low systolic or falsely high diastolic readings.

  • Common in hypertensive patients and those with arterial stiffness (e.g., atherosclerosis).

  • To avoid errors: palpate the radial pulse first to estimate systolic pressure, then inflate the cuff 20–30 mmHg above that before auscultating.
