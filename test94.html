Question 2 of 50
Which mode of mechanical ventilation delivers a preset tidal volume with each breath, regardless of the pressure required?
A. Pressure-cycled ventilation
B. Volume-cycled ventilation ✓
C. Time-cycled ventilation
D. Pressure support ventilation (PSV)
💡
Explanation

Volume-cycled ventilation delivers a preset tidal volume of gas to the patient with each breath, regardless of the airway pressure that may be required to deliver that volume. This mode is commonly used because it ensures a consistent volume of tidal air, which is critical in maintaining adequate ventilation and gas exchange, especially in patients who cannot generate adequate breaths themselves. In contrast, pressure-cycled ventilation stops delivering air once a preset pressure is reached, which may result in variable tidal volumes. Time-cycled ventilation delivers breaths based on a preset inspiratory time and is less commonly used in adults. Pressure support ventilation (PSV) supplements spontaneous breaths with positive pressure but does not control tidal volume outright.

Question 9 of 50
What is the most common cause of hypotension in a mechanically ventilated patient?
A. Excessive tidal volume
B. Under-sedation
C. Increased intrathoracic pressure ✓
D. High PEEP levels
💡
Explanation
• Increased intrathoracic pressure due to positive pressure ventilation can decrease venous return to the heart, leading to hypotension. This is especially seen in patients with high PEEP levels or other ventilator settings that increase intrathoracic pressure. • Excessive tidal volume can lead to barotrauma and other complications but does not directly cause hypotension. • Under-sedation can lead to patient discomfort and agitation but does not directly cause hypotension. • High PEEP levels can contribute to increased intrathoracic pressure, which in turn can cause hypotension.

A. Low tidal volume, high PEEP ✓
B. High tidal volume, low PEEP
C. High tidal volume, high PEEP
D. Low tidal volume, low PEEP
💡
Explanation
• In ARDS, low tidal volume (usually 6-8 mL/kg of ideal body weight) and high PEEP are used to minimize ventilator-associated lung injury while optimizing oxygenation. • High tidal volume can worsen lung injury in ARDS by increasing barotrauma and volutrauma. • Low tidal volume combined with low PEEP would likely result in inadequate oxygenation in ARDS patients. • High tidal volume, high PEEP can be harmful and is not recommended in ARDS

Question 50 of 50
The following are the complications oxygen administration
I. It supports combustion
II. Causes CO2 Narcosis
III. Oxygen Toxicity
IV. Absorption Atelectasis
A. I, II and III
B. II, III and IV
C. I, II and IV
D. I, II, III and IV ✓
💡
Explanation

Supports Combustion: Oxygen is an oxidizing agent and can enhance combustion. Therefore, precautions are needed to prevent fire hazards in environments where high concentrations of oxygen are used.

Causes CO2 Narcosis: In patients with chronic respiratory conditions like COPD, high levels of oxygen can reduce their respiratory drive due to the lower levels of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the blood, potentially leading to CO2 narcosis (an excessive accumulation of CO2 in the blood).

Oxygen Toxicity: High concentrations of oxygen, particularly at elevated pressures (e.g., in hyperbaric oxygen therapy), can lead to oxygen toxicity, which may damage the lungs and central nervous system.

Absorption Atelectasis: When high concentrations of oxygen are administered, the increased absorption of oxygen into the blood can lead to the collapse of alveoli (atelectasis) due to the displacement of nitrogen that normally keeps the alveoli open.
