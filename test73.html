Explanation
  • While the terms sound similar, it is important to distinguish between rubeola and rubella to avoid confusion:
  • Rubeola (Measles): Often called "hard measles" or "red measles," this is a highly contagious respiratory infection caused by the measles virus (a paramyxovirus). It is typically more severe than German measles and can lead to serious complications like pneumonia.

  • Other Options:
  • Option 3: Paratoditis (Mumps) refers to the inflammation of the parotid glands.
  • Option 4: Meningitis is the inflammation of the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord.

Question 20 of 50
Which of the following strategies is most effective in preventing the progression of RDS in preterm neonates?
A. Delayed cord clamping
B. Early use of high-frequency oscillatory ventilation (HFOV)
C. Timely administration of antenatal corticosteroids ✓
D. Immediate administration of antibiotics after birth
💡
Explanation
Antenatal corticosteroids (such as betamethasone or dexamethasone) are the most effective strategy for preventing the progression of RDS in preterm neonates. Administering these corticosteroids to pregnant women at risk of preterm birth helps accelerate the maturation of the fetal lungs, increasing surfactant production and reducing the incidence and severity of RDS. Early use of mechanical ventilation, antibiotics, or delayed cord clamping may be part of supportive care, but antenatal corticosteroid therapy has the most direct impact on lung development and surfactant production.
Question 21 of 50
Which of the following is the primary cause of respiratory failure in a neonate with severe RDS?
A. Increased airway resistance due to inflammation
B. Reduced ability of the lungs to expand due to alveolar collapse ✓
C. Overproduction of mucus leading to airway obstruction
D. Vascular congestion and poor perfusion to the lungs
💡
Explanation
In severe RDS, the primary cause of respiratory failure is alveolar collapse due to the lack of adequate surfactant. Surfactant normally reduces surface tension, allowing the alveoli to remain open during exhalation and facilitating proper gas exchange. Without sufficient surfactant, the alveoli collapse, reducing lung compliance and impairing oxygenation. This leads to hypoxemia (low oxygen) and hypercapnia (high carbon dioxide), which are hallmarks of respiratory failure in RDS.
Question 22 of 50
Which of the following is the most common cause of RDS in preterm infants?
A. Surfactant deficiency ✓
B. Meconium aspiration
C. Neonatal pneumonia
D. Pulmonary hypoplasia
💡
Explanation
The most common cause of Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) in preterm infants is surfactant deficiency. Preterm infants, especially those born before 32 weeks gestation, have immature lungs that produce insufficient surfactant, a substance essential for reducing surface tension within the alveoli. Surfactant deficiency leads to alveolar collapse, poor gas exchange, and respiratory failure. Meconium aspiration, pneumonia, and pulmonary hypoplasia are less common causes in preterm infants and tend to affect term or near-term infants more.
Question 23 of 50
Which of the following is the primary mechanism by which antenatal corticosteroids reduce the incidence of RDS?
A. They reduce maternal blood pressure, improving uterine blood flow
B. They stimulate the production of fetal surfactant ✓
C. They improve the fetal immune system
D. They increase fetal lung growth by accelerating cellular differentiation
💡
Explanation
Antenatal corticosteroids (e.g., betamethasone or dexamethasone) are administered to pregnant women at risk of preterm delivery to stimulate the fetal lungs to produce surfactant. Surfactant reduces surface tension in the alveoli, promoting lung expansion and improving gas exchange. This significantly reduces the risk of RDS in preterm infants by enhancing lung compliance and preventing alveolar collapse. Corticosteroids do not directly affect blood pressure, immune system, or lung growth in the same way.
Question 24 of 50
Which of the following is the most appropriate initial therapy for a neonate born at 28 weeks gestation with mild RDS?
A. Immediate mechanical ventilation
B. Administration of surfactant and non-invasive ventilation (CPAP) ✓
C. Administration of intravenous fluids and antibiotics
D. Oxygen therapy only, with no further intervention
💡
Explanation
For a neonate born at 28 weeks gestation with mild RDS, the most appropriate initial therapy is the administration of surfactant followed by non-invasive ventilation (such as CPAP). Surfactant therapy improves lung compliance and helps stabilize the alveoli, while CPAP provides positive pressure to prevent alveolar collapse. Mechanical ventilation is typically reserved for more severe cases, and intravenous fluids or antibiotics are not first-line treatments for mild RDS unless other complications are suspected.
Question 25 of 50
What is the most likely chest X-ray finding in a neonate with RDS?
A. Hyperinflation with flattened diaphragm
B. Air bronchograms with a ground-glass appearance ✓
C. Consolidation and lobar collapse
D. Pleural effusion with bilateral infiltrates
💡
Explanation

The hallmark chest X-ray findings in RDS include a ground-glass appearance due to alveolar fluid accumulation and air bronchograms, which occur when the bronchi are visible because of surrounding alveolar collapse and consolidation. These findings reflect the impaired lung function caused by surfactant deficiency and poor gas exchange. Hyperinflation or pleural effusion is not typical in RDS, and lobar collapse is usually seen in conditions like meconium aspiration syndrome or pneumonia.

Question 26 of 50
Why are preterm infants at a higher risk of developing RDS compared to term infants?
A. Preterm infants are more likely to aspirate meconium at birth
B. Preterm infants have less mature lung structure and insufficient surfactant ✓
C. Preterm infants have a higher rate of infection at birth
D. Preterm infants have impaired cardiac function, leading to pulmonary congestion
💡
Explanation
Preterm infants are at a higher risk of developing RDS because their lungs are immature and produce insufficient surfactant, a substance that lowers surface tension in the alveoli, preventing alveolar collapse. This lack of surfactant causes poor lung compliance and impaired gas exchange, leading to respiratory failure. Term infants generally have more mature lungs, with adequate surfactant production, and are less prone to developing RDS.
Question 27 of 50
Which of the following is the most likely complication in a neonate with severe RDS who requires prolonged mechanical ventilation?
A. Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) ✓
B. Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC)
C. Spontaneous pneumothorax
D. Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
💡
Explanation
Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) is a chronic lung condition that occurs in infants who require prolonged mechanical ventilation, especially those with severe RDS. BPD results from oxygen toxicity, barotrauma (lung damage from high-pressure ventilation), and inflammation due to prolonged mechanical ventilation. The condition leads to scarring and abnormal lung development, requiring ongoing respiratory support and often resulting in long-term pulmonary complications. NEC, pneumothorax, and PDA are not typical complications specifically related to mechanical ventilation for RDS.
