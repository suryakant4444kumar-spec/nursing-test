Question 1 of 50
Referred pain in external hemorrhoids is due to:
A. Common pudendal nerve
B. Inferior rectal nerve ✓
C. Splanchnic nerve
D. Sympathetic nerve
💡
Explanation

Referred pain in external hemorrhoids is typically due to the inferior rectal nerve, which provides sensory innervation to the anal canal, particularly the area around the anus where external hemorrhoids are located. The inferior rectal nerve is a branch of the pudendal nerve, and pain from external hemorrhoids is often referred to areas that are innervated by this nerve.

  • Option 1 (Common pudendal nerve): The common pudendal nerve is a branch of the sacral plexus, and while it supplies motor and sensory function to various pelvic structures, the referred pain in external hemorrhoids is more specifically associated with the inferior rectal nerve.

  • Option 3 (Splanchnic nerve): The splanchnic nerves are primarily involved in the innervation of the abdominal and pelvic viscera and are not responsible for the sensory input from the anal canal or external hemorrhoids.

  • Option 4 (Sympathetic nerve): Sympathetic nerves are involved in the autonomic control of visceral organs, but they do not directly innervate the anal region where external hemorrhoids occur, and they are not responsible for the referred pain from hemorrhoids.
