Question 1 of 50
Normal umbilical cord contains ..
A. One vein and two arteries ✓
B. One artery and two veins
C. One vein and one artery
D. Two arteries and two veins
💡
Explanation

  • A normal umbilical cord contains three blood vessels.

  • Two umbilical arteries → carry deoxygenated blood from the fetus to the placenta.

  • One umbilical vein → carries oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood from the placenta to the fetus.

  • This 2 arteries + 1 vein arrangement is considered normal anatomy.

  • Presence of a single umbilical artery (only 1 artery) is an abnormal finding and may be associated with congenital anomalies.

Question 2 of 50
Communication between aorta and pulmonary artery in fetal circulation is known as..................
A. Foramen ovale
B. Ductus arteriosus ✓
C. Ductus venosus
D. Fossa ovalis
💡
Explanation

  • In fetal circulation, the ductus arteriosus is a vital blood vessel.

  • It connects the pulmonary artery directly to the aorta.

  • This shunt allows most of the blood to bypass the non-functional fetal lungs, which are not yet used for respiration.

  • After birth, with the initiation of breathing, the ductus arteriosus closes functionally and later becomes the ligamentum arteriosum.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • Foramen ovale → connects right atrium to left atrium.

  • Ductus venosus → connects umbilical vein to inferior vena cava.

  • Fossa ovalis → is the postnatal remnant of the foramen ovale.

💡
Explanation
When suctioning a newborn's mouth and nose, the mouth should be suctioned first. The reason for this is that if the nose is suctioned first and the baby is then stimulated to breathe, they may inhale secretions from the mouth into the airways. Suctioning the mouth first clears any oral secretions, reducing the risk of aspiration into the lungs when suctioning the nose afterward.

Question 6 of 50
Size of suction catheter used in neonate for oral and nasal suction is
A. 8 Fr ✓
B. 12 Fr
C. 14 Fr
D. 16 Fr
💡
Explanation

  • For neonates, a smaller size suction catheter is required to safely clear secretions without causing trauma to delicate tissues.

  • An 8 French (Fr) suction catheter is the appropriate size commonly used for both oral and nasal suctioning in neonates.

  • Larger catheters like 12, 14, or 16 Fr are typically used for older infants or children, not neonates.

  • Using the correct size catheter ensures effective suctioning while minimizing the risk of injury or discomfort to the newborn.

Question 7 of 50
In Neonatal Resuscitation are seldom needed to stimulate the heart, increase tissue perfusion, and restore acid base balance.
A. Medication ✓
B. Chest Compression.
C. Bag and mask ventilation
D. Tactile stimulation.
💡
Explanation

In Neonatal Resuscitation, the vast majority of newborns respond to effective ventilation (bag and mask ventilation).

  • Medications are rarely required because correcting ventilation and oxygenation usually restores heart rate, tissue perfusion, and acid–base balance.

  • Chest compressions are used only if the heart rate remains <60/min despite adequate ventilation.

  • Bag and mask ventilation is the most important and commonly used intervention.

  • Tactile stimulation is an initial step to initiate breathing.

Question 9 of 50
A pregnant woman with a history of morphine exposure during the antenatal period delivers a baby. Which of the following drugs is most likely to be administered to the newborn immediately after delivery to manage potential complications of opioid exposure?
A. Naloxone ✓
B. N-acetyl cysteine
C. Acetazolamide
D. Sodium bicarbonate
💡
Explanation
Morphine, an opioid, can cross the placenta and affect the fetus during pregnancy. Neonates exposed to opioids in utero may develop opioid withdrawal symptoms after birth, such as respiratory depression, lethargy, poor feeding, and irritability. Naloxone, an opioid antagonist, is widely used in clinical practice to reverse opioid-induced respiratory depression and withdrawal symptoms in newborns. It is considered the standard treatment in cases where neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome (NOWS) or respiratory depression is suspected, especially following exposure to opioids like morphine during pregnancy.
Question 10 of 50
Clinical manifestation of fetal alcohol syndrome includes .............
A. Anencephaly, hydrocephalus and microglossia.
B. Hepatomegaly, hypotonia, and microphthalmia.
C. Hyperbilirubinemia, jaundice, and failure to thrive.
D. Microcephaly, short philtrum, and prenatal growth retardation ✓
💡
Explanation

Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS) is a severe form of fetal alcohol spectrum disorders, caused by maternal alcohol consumption during pregnancy.

Classic clinical features include:

  • Microcephaly (abnormally small head)

  • Short/smooth philtrum (the vertical groove between the nose and upper lip)

  • Thin upper lip

  • Prenatal and postnatal growth retardation

  • Developmental delays

  • Facial abnormalities

  • Neurological and cognitive impairments

These features are specific indicators of FAS and help differentiate it from other congenital syndromes.

Question 11 of 50
All are seen in fetal alcohol syndrome except:
A. Microcephaly
B. Poor coordination
C. Overgrowth of body ✓
D. Flat face
💡
Explanation

Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS) results from alcohol exposure during pregnancy and is characterized by growth restriction, not overgrowth.

Key features of FAS include:-

  • Microcephaly
    – Reduced head circumference due to impaired brain development.

  • Poor coordination
    – Caused by central nervous system dysfunction (motor and cognitive deficits).

  • Flat face
    – Typical facial features include flat midface, smooth philtrum, and thin upper lip.

  • Overgrowth of body
    – This is NOT seen in FAS.
    – In fact, children with FAS usually show prenatal and postnatal growth retardation (low weight, short stature).

Question 16 of 50
Key features of kangaroo mother care are all the following, except?
A. Skin to skin contact between mother and baby
B. Exclusive breast feeding
C. Initiated in a facility and continued at home
D. Done for babies with cyanosis ✓
💡
Explanation
–Kangaroo care is a technique practiced on newborn, usually preterm, infants wherein the infant is held, skin-to-skin, with an adult.
The kangaroo position provided ready access to nourishment. The parent's stable body temperature helps to regulate the neonate's temperature more smoothly than an incubator, and allows for readily accessible breastfeeding. Babies who are eligible for kangaroo care include pre-term infants, weighing less than 1,500 grams, and breathing independently. Cardiopulmonary monitoring, oximetry, supplemental oxygen or nasal continuous positive airway pressure ventilation, intravenous infusion, and monitor leads do not prevent kangaroo care. Babies who are in kangaroo care tend to be less prone to apnea and bradycardia and have stabilization of oxygen needs.
