💡
Explanation

Daily weight is considered one of the most reliable indicators of hydration status. Significant changes in body weight, especially over a short period, can reflect shifts in fluid balance (e.g., dehydration or overhydration).

  • Urine color (Option 4) can indicate hydration levels but may not always be reliable, as it can be influenced by diet, medications, or other factors.

Question 12 of 50
A patient with von Willebrand (vWF) deficiency undergoing surgery, this patient needs to be treated with ...........along with vWF
A. Thrombin
B. Factor VII
C. Factor VIII ✓
D. Factor IX
💡
Explanation

Von Willebrand disease (vWD) is a bleeding disorder caused by a deficiency or dysfunction of von Willebrand factor (vWF), which plays a crucial role in platelet adhesion and stabilizing factor VIII in the blood clotting process. When a patient with vWD undergoes surgery, they are at increased risk of bleeding, and treatment is often necessary to prevent excessive bleeding.

  • Factor VIII (Option 3) is essential for proper blood clotting and works in conjunction with von Willebrand factor. In patients with von Willebrand disease, factor VIII levels are often low or dysfunctional, so administering factor VIII along with vWF helps to stabilize clot formation.

  • Thrombin (Option 1) is an enzyme involved in the clotting cascade but is not specifically used in the treatment of von Willebrand disease.

  • Factor VII (Option 2) and Factor IX (Option 4) are clotting factors, but they are not typically used in the treatment of von Willebrand disease, as they are more relevant to hemophilia A and B, respectively.

Production site for Von Willebrand factor is.
A. Vascular endothelium ✓
B. Platelet
C. Liver
D. RBCs
💡
Explanation

:
Von Willebrand factor (vWF) is primarily produced in the vascular endothelium, which is the lining of blood vessels. It is also synthesized in smaller amounts by megakaryocytes (the precursor cells of platelets) and the subendothelial connective tissue. vWF plays a crucial role in blood clotting by promoting platelet adhesion to sites of vascular injury and by stabilizing Factor VIII, an essential component of the clotting cascade.

  • Platelet (Option 2) does not produce vWF, but platelets store it and release it during the clotting process.

  • Liver (Option 3) is responsible for producing many other clotting factors, but not vWF.

  • RBCs (Option 4) do not produce vWF.

Question 16 of 50
The laboratory results of a patient with disseminated intravascular coagulation are likely to include
A. Prolonged prothrombin and partial thromboplastin times ✓
B. Increased platelet counts
C. Increased levels of fibrinogen
D. Decreased levels of fibrin split products.
💡
Explanation

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) is a condition characterized by widespread activation of the clotting cascade, leading to the formation of small clots throughout the bloodstream. As a result, it uses up clotting factors and platelets, leading to bleeding complications.

  • Prolonged prothrombin time (PT) and partial thromboplastin time (PTT) (Option 1) are common laboratory findings in DIC. The clotting factors are consumed in the widespread formation of microthrombi, leading to a prolonged PT and PTT.

  • Increased platelet counts (Option 2) are typically not seen in DIC. Instead, platelet counts are usually decreased due to consumption in the clotting process.

  • Increased levels of fibrinogen (Option 3) are also typically decreased in DIC, as fibrinogen is consumed in the clotting process, leading to low levels.

  • Decreased levels of fibrin split products (Option 4) are incorrect, as in DIC, fibrin split products (FSP) or D-dimers are increased due to the breakdown of fibrin clots.

Question 19 of 50
Clotting time is more in ........
A. Severe hemophilia
B. Afibrinogenemia
C. DIC
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation
Clotting time is the time it takes for blood to form a clot after an injury. In each of the conditions listed, clotting time can be prolonged due to different mechanisms of impaired clot formation:
Option 1: Severe hemophilia: In severe hemophilia, there is a deficiency or absence of clotting factors (usually factor VIII or IX), which leads to prolonged clotting time because the blood cannot form a clot effectively.
Option 2: Afibrinogenemia: Afibrinogenemia is a rare genetic disorder characterized by the complete absence of fibrinogen, a crucial protein in the clotting process. This results in an extended clotting time as fibrinogen is necessary for the formation of the fibrin clot.
Option 3: DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation): DIC is a complex condition where abnormal clotting occurs in the bloodstream, consuming clotting factors and platelets, leading to prolonged clotting time. Even though clotting happens inappropriately, the overall ability to form effective clots is impaired.
Question 20 of 50
Feature of factor XIII deficiency is ............
A. Early solubilization of clot ✓
B. Fibrinogen to fibrin conversion will not be there
C. Activation of platelet will be delayed
D. Small clot fragments
💡
Explanation

:
Factor XIII (also known as fibrin-stabilizing factor) is important for the final step in blood clot formation, where it cross-links fibrin strands to form a stable clot. A deficiency in factor XIII leads to a clot that is unstable and dissolves prematurely.

  • Early solubilization of clot (Option 1) is the primary feature of factor XIII deficiency. The clot formed is not stable and will break down or dissolve sooner than normal because the fibrin strands are not properly cross-linked.

  • Fibrinogen to fibrin conversion will not be there (Option 2) is incorrect because the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin still happens normally in factor XIII deficiency. The issue lies in the stabilization of the fibrin clot.

  • Activation of platelet will be delayed (Option 3) is not characteristic of factor XIII deficiency. Platelet activation is not directly affected by a deficiency of factor XIII.

  • Small clot fragments (Option 4) are not a typical feature of factor XIII deficiency. The problem is not with the size of the clot but with its premature breakdown.

Question 21 of 50
All of the following are platelet associated coagulation factors EXCEPT
A. Von Willebrand Factor
B. Factor IX ✓
C. Factor XI
D. Factor XIII
💡
Explanation
  • Platelet-associated coagulation factors are substances present on or released from platelets that help in the coagulation process. Von Willebrand factor is stored in platelet alpha granules and plays an important role in platelet adhesion during clot formation. Factor XI and Factor XIII can also be associated with platelets and contribute to the coagulation mechanism and stabilization of fibrin clot. However, Factor IX is mainly a plasma coagulation factor produced in the liver and circulates in the blood as part of the intrinsic pathway of coagulation. It is not considered a platelet-associated coagulation factor. Therefore, Factor IX is the correct exception.

Question 23 of 50
Splenomegaly is evident in .......
A. Malaria
B. Kala azar
C. Hemolytic anemia
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

Splenomegaly, or enlargement of the spleen, can occur in various conditions. It is often a result of increased blood cell destruction, infections, or other underlying health conditions that affect the spleen's function. Here's a breakdown:

  • Malaria (Option 1): In malaria, the spleen enlarges due to the destruction of red blood cells infected with Plasmodium parasites. The spleen works to filter out these infected cells.

  • Kala azar (Visceral leishmaniasis) (Option 2): Kala azar is caused by the Leishmania parasite, which infects the liver, spleen, and bone marrow. This results in splenomegaly as the spleen becomes involved in fighting the infection.

  • Hemolytic anemia (Option 3): In hemolytic anemia, red blood cells are destroyed prematurely, and the spleen works to remove these cells, leading to splenomegaly.

Question 26 of 50
In high altitude individual RBC count is more because ...
A. Variation in pressure cause increased RBC production
B. Erythropoiesis will be stimulated because of atmospheric hypoxia ✓
C. RBC destruction is more in high altitude
D. Bone marrow become more active
💡
Explanation

:
At high altitudes, the oxygen levels in the atmosphere are lower (hypoxia), which leads to reduced oxygen availability in the blood. In response to this, the body compensates by stimulating the production of more red blood cells (erythropoiesis) through the release of erythropoietin (EPO), a hormone produced primarily by the kidneys. This increased production of red blood cells helps improve oxygen transport in the blood.

  • Variation in pressure causing increased RBC production (Option 1) is not the primary reason for the increase in RBC count at high altitudes. It's more related to oxygen availability, not just pressure changes.

  • Erythropoiesis will be stimulated because of atmospheric hypoxia (Option 2) is the correct explanation. The body responds to lower oxygen levels by producing more RBCs to enhance oxygen delivery to tissues.

  • RBC destruction is more in high altitude (Option 3) is incorrect. At high altitudes, RBC destruction does not increase; rather, RBC production increases to compensate for lower oxygen levels.

  • Bone marrow becomes more active (Option 4) is somewhat true, but it is a result of the stimulation of erythropoiesis due to hypoxia, rather than being a direct cause of the increase in RBC count.

A patient with hemoglobin of 10 g/dl likely to present with
A. Pallor ✓
B. Palpitations
C. Shortness of breath
D. No clinical signs
💡
Explanation

A hemoglobin level of 10 g/dL is considered low and typically indicates mild anemia. Anemia occurs when there is a deficiency in the number or quality of red blood cells or hemoglobin in the blood, which impairs the ability of blood to carry oxygen to tissues.

  • Pallor (Option 1) is the most common clinical sign of mild anemia due to the reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. Pallor can be observed in the skin, mucous membranes, and conjunctiva.

  • Palpitations (Option 2) and shortness of breath (Option 3) may occur in more severe cases of anemia (typically when hemoglobin levels are significantly lower, around 7-8 g/dL or less), but they are less likely to be prominent in a patient with a hemoglobin level of 10 g/dL.

  • No clinical signs (Option 4) is unlikely, as mild anemia (hemoglobin 10 g/dL) often presents with at least some signs, such as pallor.
