Question 1 of 50
Patient has intravenous fluids infusing in the right arm when taking a blood pressure on this patient, the nurse would:
A. Take the blood pressure in the left arm ✓
B. Take the blood pressure in the right arm
C. Use the smallest possible cuff
D. Report inability to take the blood pressure
💡
Explanation
Infusing intravenous fluids can interfere with accurate blood pressure readings if measured on the same arm, as the pressure from the cuff might affect the flow or accuracy of the reading. To avoid this issue, the nurse should use the opposite arm, in this case, the left arm, for measuring blood pressure.
Question 2 of 50
A client had extensive, prolonged surgery. Which electrolyte level should the nurse monitor most closely?
A. Sodium
B. Calcium
C. Chloride
D. Potassium ✓
💡
Explanation

Potassium is critically important to monitor after extensive and prolonged surgery because it plays a vital role in maintaining cardiac and neuromuscular function. Surgical stress, blood loss, fluid shifts, and use of intravenous fluids can lead to imbalances such as hypokalaemia or hyperkalaemia, both of which can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias. Moreover, prolonged fasting and diuretic use postoperatively increase the risk of potassium depletion. Sodium, chloride, and calcium are important electrolytes too, but potassium disturbances post-surgery most directly affect heart function and require close monitoring. Early identification and correction of potassium imbalance help prevent postoperative complications and ensure patient safety.

Question 3 of 50
Using the Holiday-Segar method, calculate the daily fluid requirement for a 20 kg child.
A. 500 mL
B. 700 mL
C. 1500 mL ✓
D. 1000 mL
💡
Explanation
The Holiday-Segar formula calculates maintenance fluid requirements based on the child's weight:
• For the first 10 kg: 100 mL/kg/day
• For the second 10 kg: 50 mL/kg/day
• For each kg above 20 kg: 20 mL/kg/day

Question 5 of 50
Movement across fluid compartments is controlled by
A. ATP
B. Sodium
C. Osmatic and hydrostatic pressures ✓
D. Potassium
💡
Explanation

Osmotic pressure: This is the pressure exerted by the concentration of solutes (such as sodium and other electrolytes) that draws water into or out of a compartment. It is crucial in maintaining fluid balance between intracellular and extracellular spaces.

Hydrostatic pressure: This is the pressure exerted by the fluid within the compartments, which pushes fluids out of the capillaries into the interstitial spaces. It is essential for the exchange of fluids between blood vessels and tissues.

Question 13 of 50
Electrolyte needs to be replaced in a client receiving furosemide (Lasix) is ......
A. Sodium
B. Potassium ✓
C. Chlorine
D. Magnesium
💡
Explanation

Furosemide is a loop diuretic that increases the excretion of sodium, chloride, and potassium. As a result, patients on furosemide are at risk of hypokalemia (low potassium levels). It is important to monitor and, if necessary, replace potassium levels to prevent complications such as muscle weakness, arrhythmias, and other symptoms of potassium deficiency.

Question 14 of 50
Fluid loss first occurs from ...........
A. Intracellular
B. Intravascular ✓
C. Extravascular
D. Interstitial
💡
Explanation
When fluid loss begins, the body first loses fluid from the intravascular compartment (the blood vessels). This is because the intravascular space is where fluid is most readily available for exchange with the extravascular compartments. As fluid loss continues, the body then starts to draw fluid from the interstitial and intracellular compartments to maintain blood volume and pressure.

Question 23 of 50
Non-inflammatory edema occurs due to .............
A. Raise in intravascular hydrostatic pressure
B. Decreased plasma oncotic pressure
C. Obstruction in the lymphatic flow channels
D. All of the above ✓
💡
Explanation

Raise in intravascular hydrostatic pressure: Increased hydrostatic pressure in the blood vessels can push fluid out into the interstitial space, leading to edema. This is a common cause of non-inflammatory edema. Decreased plasma oncotic pressure: Low levels of plasma proteins (such as albumin) reduce the osmotic pressure in the blood vessels, leading to fluid leakage into the interstitial space.
Obstruction in the lymphatic flow channels: When lymphatic channels are obstructed, they cannot effectively drain excess fluid from the interstitial space, leading to edema.

Question 24 of 50
When administering large volume of colloids nurse need to monitor for ............
A. Adventitious respiratory sounds
B. Interstitial edema
C. Hypotension
D. Both a and b ✓
💡
Explanation

  • Adventitious respiratory sounds: Large volumes of colloids can lead to fluid overload, which may cause pulmonary edema and result in abnormal respiratory sounds, such as crackles or wheezes.

  • Interstitial edema: Colloids can shift fluid from the intravascular space to the interstitial space, potentially causing edema.

Monitoring for these signs helps ensure that the patient does not experience complications related to fluid overload.

Question 29 of 50
Most abundant buffer in blood is
A. Bicarbonates ✓
B. Hemoglobin
C. Protein
D. Phosphate
💡
Explanation

The bicarbonate buffer system is the primary buffering system in the blood and plays a crucial role in maintaining the pH balance. It is composed of bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) and carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), and it helps neutralize excess acids or bases to maintain a stable blood pH.

Although other buffers like hemoglobin, proteins, and phosphate are also important in buffering, bicarbonates are the most abundant and dominant buffering system in the blood.
